        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>

      <p>
       <b>
        Post-production
       </b>
       is part of the process of
       <a href="/wiki/Filmmaking" title="Filmmaking">
        filmmaking
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Video_production" title="Video production">
        video production
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">
        photography
       </a>
       . Post-production includes all stages of production occurring after shooting or recording individual program segments.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Traditional (analogue) post-production has mostly been replaced by
       <a href="/wiki/Video_editing_software" title="Video editing software">
        video editing software
       </a>
       that operates on a
       <a href="/wiki/Non-linear_editing_system" title="Non-linear editing system">
        non-linear editing system
       </a>
       (NLE).
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Processes">
        Processes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Processes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tainted_blue_studios_control_room.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="498" data-file-width="750" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Tainted_blue_studios_control_room.jpg/220px-Tainted_blue_studios_control_room.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Tainted_blue_studios_control_room.jpg/330px-Tainted_blue_studios_control_room.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Tainted_blue_studios_control_room.jpg/440px-Tainted_blue_studios_control_room.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tainted_blue_studios_control_room.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A sound control room at Tainted Blue Studios, 2010
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Post-production is many different processes grouped under one name. These typically include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Video_editing" title="Video editing">
         Video editing
        </a>
        the picture of a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">
         television program
        </a>
        using an
        <a href="/wiki/Edit_decision_list" title="Edit decision list">
         edit decision list
        </a>
        (EDL)
       </li>
       <li>
        Writing,
        <a href="/wiki/Re-recording_(filmmaking)" title="Re-recording (filmmaking)">
         (re)recording
        </a>
        , and editing the
        <a href="/wiki/Soundtrack" title="Soundtrack">
         soundtrack
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Adding visual
        <a href="/wiki/Special_effect" title="Special effect">
         special effects
        </a>
        - mainly
        <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">
         computer-generated imagery
        </a>
        (CGI) and digital copy from which
        <a href="/wiki/Release_print" title="Release print">
         release prints
        </a>
        will be made (although this may be made obsolete by
        <a href="/wiki/Digital_cinema" title="Digital cinema">
         digital-cinema
        </a>
        technologies).
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Sound_design" title="Sound design">
         Sound design
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Sound_effect" title="Sound effect">
         sound effects
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Dubbing_(filmmaking)#Automated_Dialogue_Replacement_/_post-sync" title="Dubbing (filmmaking)">
         ADR
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Foley_(filmmaking)" title="Foley (filmmaking)">
         foley
        </a>
        , and music, culminating in a process known as sound re-recording or mixing with
        <a href="/wiki/Professional_audio" title="Professional audio">
         professional audio
        </a>
        equipment.
       </li>
       <li>
        Transfer of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colour_motion_picture_film" title="Colour motion picture film">
         colour motion picture film
        </a>
        to
        <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">
         video
        </a>
        or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DPX" title="DPX">
         DPX
        </a>
        with a
        <a href="/wiki/Telecine" title="Telecine">
         telecine
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Color_grading" title="Color grading">
         color grading
        </a>
        (correction) in a
        <a href="/wiki/Color_suite" title="Color suite">
         color suite
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The post-production phase of creating a film usually takes longer than the actual shooting of the film and can take several months to complete because it includes the complete editing, colour correction, and the addition of music and sound. The process of editing a movie is also seen as the second directing because through post-production it is possible to change the intention of the movie. Furthermore, through the use of color grading tools and the addition of music and sound, the atmosphere of the movie can be heavily influenced. For instance, a blue-tinted movie is associated with a cold atmosphere and the choice of music and sound increases the effect of the shown scenes to the audience.
      </p>
      <p>
       Post-production was named a "dying industry" by Phil Izzo.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The once exclusive service offered by high-end post-production facilities have been eroded away by
       <a href="/wiki/Video_editing_software" title="Video editing software">
        video editing software
       </a>
       that operates on a
       <a href="/wiki/Non-linear_editing_system" title="Non-linear editing system">
        non-linear editing system
       </a>
       (NLE). As such, traditional (analogue) post-production services are being surpassed by digital, leading to sales of over $6 billion annually.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Television">
        Television
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Television">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In television, the phases of post-production include: editing, video editing, sound editing, animation and visual effects insertions, viewing and the start of the airing process. It is imperative that post-production executes and oversees the preparation until the final product is completely ready.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Photography">
        Photography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Photography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Original_research" role="presentation">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <div style="width:52px">
          <img alt="" data-file-height="40" data-file-width="40" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" width="40"/>
         </div>

        <td class="mbox-text">

      <p>
       Professional post-producers usually apply a certain range of
       <a href="/wiki/Image_editing" title="Image editing">
        image editing
       </a>
       operations to the
       <a href="/wiki/Raw_image_format" title="Raw image format">
        raw image format
       </a>
       provided by a photographer or an image-bank. There is a range of proprietary and free and open-source software, running on a range of operating systems available to do this work.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first stage of post-production usually requires loading the raw images into the post-production software. If there is more than one image, and they belong to a set, ideally post-producers try to equalize
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (July 2015)">
           further explanation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       the images before loading them. After that, if necessary, the next step would be to cut the objects in the images with the Pen Tool for a perfect and clean cut. The next stage would be cleaning the image using tools such as the healing tool, clone tool, and patch tool.
      </p>
      <p>
       The next stages depend on what the client ordered. If it's a photo-montage, the post-producers would usually start assembling the different images into the final document, and start to integrate the images with the background.
      </p>
      <p>
       In advertising, it usually requires assembling several images together in a photo-composition.
      </p>
      <p>
       Types of work usually done:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Advertising that requires one background (as one or more images to assemble) and one or more models. (Usually, the most time consuming as a lot of times these are image bank images which don't have much quality, and they all have different light and color as they were not controlled by only one photographer in one set location)
       </li>
       <li>
        Product-photography that usually requires several images of the same object with different lights, and assembled together, to control light and unwanted reflections, or to assemble parts that would be difficult to get in one shot, such as a beer glass for a beer advertising. (Sometimes to composite one image of a beer glass it requires 4 or 5 images: one for the base, one for the beer, one for the label, one for the foam, and one or more for splashing beer if that is desired)
       </li>
       <li>
        Fashion photography that usually requires a really heavy post-production for editorial or advertising.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Music">
        Music
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Music">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Techniques used in music post-production include comping (compiling the best portions of multiple takes into one superior take), timing and
       <a href="/wiki/Pitch_correction" title="Pitch correction">
        pitch correction
       </a>
       (perhaps through
       <a href="/wiki/Quantization_(music)" title="Quantization (music)">
        beat quantization
       </a>
       ), and adding
       <a href="/wiki/Audio_signal_processing" title="Audio signal processing">
        effects
       </a>
       . This process is typically referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Audio_mixing_(recorded_music)" title="Audio mixing (recorded music)">
        mixing
       </a>
       and can also involve
       <a href="/wiki/Equalization_(audio)" title="Equalization (audio)">
        equalization
       </a>
       and adjusting the levels of each individual track to provide an optimal sound experience.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Contrary to the name, post-production may occur at any point during recording and production process and is non-linear and
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/veridical" title="wikt:veridical">
        nonveridic
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

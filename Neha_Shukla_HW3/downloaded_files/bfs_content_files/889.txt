      <p>
       <b>
        Principal photography
       </b>
       is the phase of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_production" title="Film production">
        film production
       </a>
       in which the movie is filmed, with actors on set and cameras rolling, as distinct from
       <a href="/wiki/Pre-production" title="Pre-production">
        pre-production
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">
        post-production
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jr.2010_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jr.2010-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Principal photography is typically the most expensive phase of film production, due to actor, director, and set crew salaries, as well as the costs of certain shots, props, and on-set special effects. Its start generally marks a
       <a href="/wiki/Point_of_no_return" title="Point of no return">
        point of no return
       </a>
       for the financiers, because until it is complete, there is unlikely to be enough material filmed to release a final product needed to recoup costs.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While it is common for a film to lose its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenlight" title="Greenlight">
        greenlight status
       </a>
       during pre-production – for example, because an important cast member drops out – it is extremely uncommon for financing to be withdrawn once principal photography has begun.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Feature_film" title="Feature film">
        Feature films
       </a>
       usually have insurance in place by the time principal photography begins. The death of a
       <a href="/wiki/Bankable_star" title="Bankable star">
        bankable star
       </a>
       before completing all planned takes, or the loss of sets or footage can render a film impossible to complete as planned. For example, sets are notoriously flammable. Furthermore, professional-quality
       <a href="/wiki/Movie_camera" title="Movie camera">
        movie cameras
       </a>
       are normally rented as needed, and most camera houses will not allow rentals of their equipment without proof of insurance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Once a film concludes principal photography, it is said to have
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wrap_(filming)" title="Wrap (filming)">
        wrapped
       </a>
       , and a wrap party may be organized to celebrate. During
       <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">
        post-production
       </a>
       , it may become clear that certain
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shot_(filming)" title="Shot (filming)">
        shots
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sequence_(filming)" title="Sequence (filming)">
        sequences
       </a>
       are missing or incomplete and are required to complete the film, or that a certain
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scene_(filming)" title="Scene (filming)">
        scene
       </a>
       is not playing as expected, or even that a particular actor's performance has not turned out as desired. In these circumstances, additional material may have to be shot. If the material has already been shot once, or is substantial, the process is referred to as a re-shoot, but if the material is new and relatively minor, it is often referred to as a
       <a href="/wiki/Pick-up_(filmmaking)" title="Pick-up (filmmaking)">
        pick-up
       </a>
       .
      </p>

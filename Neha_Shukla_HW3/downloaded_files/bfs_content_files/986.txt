      <p>
       The
       <b>
        visual arts
       </b>
       are
       <a href="/wiki/Art#Forms,_genres,_media,_and_styles" title="Art">
        art forms
       </a>
       such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceramics_(art)" title="Ceramics (art)">
        ceramics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">
        drawing
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">
        painting
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">
        sculpture
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">
        printmaking
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">
        design
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">
        crafts
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">
        photography
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">
        video
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Filmmaking" title="Filmmaking">
        filmmaking
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">
        architecture
       </a>
       . Many artistic disciplines (
       <a href="/wiki/Performing_arts" title="Performing arts">
        performing arts
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_art" title="Conceptual art">
        conceptual art
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Textile_arts" title="Textile arts">
        textile arts
       </a>
       ) involve aspects of the visual arts as well as arts of other types. Also included within the visual arts
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       are the
       <a href="/wiki/Applied_arts" title="Applied arts">
        applied arts
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Industrial_design" title="Industrial design">
        industrial design
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">
        graphic design
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Fashion_design" title="Fashion design">
        fashion design
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Interior_design" title="Interior design">
        interior design
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decorative_art" title="Decorative art">
        decorative art
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Current usage of the term "visual arts" includes
       <a href="/wiki/Fine_art" title="Fine art">
        fine art
       </a>
       as well as the applied, decorative arts and
       <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">
        crafts
       </a>
       , but this was not always the case. Before the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_Movement" title="Arts and Crafts Movement">
        Arts and Crafts Movement
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        Britain
       </a>
       and elsewhere at the turn of the 20th century, the term 'artist' was often restricted to a person working in the fine arts (such as painting, sculpture, or printmaking) and not the
       <a href="/wiki/Handicraft" title="Handicraft">
        handicraft
       </a>
       , craft, or applied art media. The distinction was emphasized by artists of the Arts and Crafts Movement, who valued vernacular art forms as much as high forms.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Art_school" title="Art school">
        Art schools
       </a>
       made a distinction between the fine arts and the crafts, maintaining that a craftsperson could not be considered a practitioner of the arts.
      </p>
      <p>
       The increasing tendency to privilege painting, and to a lesser degree sculpture, above other arts has been a feature of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_art" title="Western art">
        Western art
       </a>
       as well as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_art_history" title="Eastern art history">
        East Asian
       </a>
       art. In both regions painting has been seen as relying to the highest degree on the imagination of the artist, and the furthest removed from manual labour – in
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_painting" title="Chinese painting">
        Chinese painting
       </a>
       the most highly valued styles were those of "scholar-painting", at least in theory practiced by gentleman amateurs. The Western
       <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy_of_genres" title="Hierarchy of genres">
        hierarchy of genres
       </a>
       reflected similar attitudes.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_training">
        Education and training
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and training">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Visual_arts_education" title="Visual arts education">
        Visual arts education
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Training in the visual arts has generally been through variations of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apprentice" title="Apprentice">
        apprentice
       </a>
       and workshop systems. In Europe the
       <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
        Renaissance
       </a>
       movement to increase the prestige of the artist led to the
       <a href="/wiki/Academy" title="Academy">
        academy
       </a>
       system for training artists, and today most of the people who are pursuing a career in arts train in
       <a href="/wiki/Art_school" title="Art school">
        art schools
       </a>
       at tertiary levels. Visual arts have now become an elective subject in most education systems.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Drawing">
        Drawing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Drawing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">
        Drawing
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">
        Drawing
       </a>
       is a means of making an
       <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">
        image
       </a>
       , using any of a wide variety of tools and techniques. It generally involves making marks on a surface by applying pressure from a tool, or moving a tool across a surface using dry media such as
       <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">
        graphite
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Pencil" title="Pencil">
        pencils
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pen_and_ink" title="Pen and ink">
        pen and ink
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">
        inked
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Brush" title="Brush">
        brushes
       </a>
       , wax
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Color_pencil" title="Color pencil">
        color pencils
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Crayon" title="Crayon">
        crayons
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">
        charcoals
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pastel" title="Pastel">
        pastels
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Marker_pen" title="Marker pen">
        markers
       </a>
       . Digital tools that simulate the effects of these are also used. The main techniques used in drawing are: line drawing,
       <a href="/wiki/Hatching" title="Hatching">
        hatching
       </a>
       , crosshatching, random hatching, scribbling,
       <a href="/wiki/Stippling" title="Stippling">
        stippling
       </a>
       , and blending. An artist who excels in drawing is referred to as a
       <i>
        draftsman
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        draughtsman
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Drawing goes back at least 16,000 years to
       <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">
        Paleolithic
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Cave_painting" title="Cave painting">
        cave representations
       </a>
       of animals such as those at
       <a href="/wiki/Lascaux" title="Lascaux">
        Lascaux
       </a>
       in France and
       <a href="/wiki/Cave_of_Altamira" title="Cave of Altamira">
        Altamira
       </a>
       in Spain. In
       <a href="/wiki/Art_of_ancient_Egypt" title="Art of ancient Egypt">
        ancient Egypt
       </a>
       , ink drawings on
       <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">
        papyrus
       </a>
       , often depicting people, were used as models for painting or sculpture. Drawings on
       <a href="/wiki/Pottery_of_ancient_Greece" title="Pottery of ancient Greece">
        Greek vases
       </a>
       , initially geometric, later developed to the human form with
       <a href="/wiki/Black-figure_pottery" title="Black-figure pottery">
        black-figure pottery
       </a>
       during the 7th century BC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       With
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_paper" title="History of paper">
        paper
       </a>
       becoming common in Europe by the 15th century, drawing was adopted by masters such as
       <a href="/wiki/Sandro_Botticelli" title="Sandro Botticelli">
        Sandro Botticelli
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">
        Raphael
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">
        Michelangelo
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">
        Leonardo da Vinci
       </a>
       who sometimes treated drawing as an art in its own right rather than a preparatory stage for painting or sculpture.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">
        Painting
       </a>
       taken literally is the practice of applying
       <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">
        pigment
       </a>
       suspended in a carrier (or
       <a href="/wiki/Paint#Components" title="Paint">
        medium
       </a>
       ) and a binding agent (a
       <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">
        glue
       </a>
       ) to a surface (support) such as
       <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">
        paper
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Canvas" title="Canvas">
        canvas
       </a>
       or a wall. However, when used in an artistic sense it means the use of this activity in combination with
       <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">
        drawing
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Composition_(visual_arts)" title="Composition (visual arts)">
        composition
       </a>
       , or other aesthetic considerations in order to manifest the expressive and conceptual intention of the practitioner. Painting is also used to express spiritual motifs and ideas; sites of this kind of painting range from artwork depicting mythological figures on pottery to
       <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel" title="Sistine Chapel">
        The Sistine Chapel
       </a>
       to the human body itself.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_early_history">
        Origins and early history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Origins and early history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_painting" title="History of painting">
        History of painting
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Like drawing, painting has its documented origins in caves and on rock faces. The finest examples, believed by some to be 32,000 years old, are in the
       <a href="/wiki/Chauvet_Cave" title="Chauvet Cave">
        Chauvet
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lascaux" title="Lascaux">
        Lascaux
       </a>
       caves in southern France. In shades of red, brown, yellow and black, the paintings on the walls and ceilings are of bison, cattle, horses and deer.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Raphael_Spasimo.jpg">
         <img alt="Raphael painting of Christ Falling on the Way to Calvary from 1514–1516" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2952" data-file-width="2110" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Raphael_Spasimo.jpg/150px-Raphael_Spasimo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Raphael_Spasimo.jpg/225px-Raphael_Spasimo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Raphael_Spasimo.jpg/300px-Raphael_Spasimo.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Raphael_Spasimo.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Raphael:
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christ_Falling_on_the_Way_to_Calvary_(Raphael)" title="Christ Falling on the Way to Calvary (Raphael)">
          Spasimo
         </a>
         (1514–1516)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Paintings of human figures can be found in the tombs of ancient Egypt. In the great temple of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramses_II" title="Ramses II">
        Ramses II
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nefertari" title="Nefertari">
        Nefertari
       </a>
       , his queen, is depicted being led by
       <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">
        Isis
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Greeks contributed to painting but much of their work has been lost. One of the best remaining representations are the Hellenistic
       <a href="/wiki/Fayum_mummy_portraits" title="Fayum mummy portraits">
        Fayum mummy portraits
       </a>
       . Another example is mosaic of the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Issus" title="Battle of Issus">
        Battle of Issus
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">
        Pompeii
       </a>
       , which was probably based on a Greek painting. Greek and Roman art contributed to
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_art" title="Byzantine art">
        Byzantine art
       </a>
       in the 4th century BC, which initiated a tradition in icon painting.
      </p>

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">

       <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_painting" title="Italian Renaissance painting">

      </div>
      <p>
       Apart from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscripts" title="Illuminated manuscripts">
        illuminated manuscripts
       </a>
       produced by monks during the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </a>
       , the next significant contribution to European art was from
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_painting" title="Italian Renaissance painting">
        Italy's renaissance painters
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Giotto" title="Giotto">

        Leonardo da Vinci
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">
        Raphael
       </a>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_of_Italy" title="Art of Italy">
        Italian art
       </a>
       as the
       <i>
        chiaroscuro
       </i>

      <h3>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Baroque">
        Baroque
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Baroque">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">
        Baroque
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">
        Baroque
       </a>
       started after the Renaissance, from the late 16th century to the late 17th century. Main artists of the Baroque included
       <a href="/wiki/Caravaggio" title="Caravaggio">
        Caravaggio
       </a>
       , who made heavy use of
       <a href="/wiki/Tenebrism" title="Tenebrism">
        tenebrism
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">
        Peter Paul Rubens
       </a>
       was a
       <a href="/wiki/Flemish" title="Flemish">
        flemish
       </a>
       painter who studied in Italy, worked for local churches in
       <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">
        Antwerp
       </a>
       and also painted a series for
       <a href="/wiki/Marie_de%27_Medici" title="Marie de' Medici">
        Marie de' Medici
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Annibale_Carracci" title="Annibale Carracci">
        Annibale Carracci
       </a>
       took influences from the
       <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel" title="Sistine Chapel">
        Sistine Chapel
       </a>
       and created the genre of
       <a href="/wiki/Illusionistic_ceiling_painting" title="Illusionistic ceiling painting">
        illusionistic ceiling painting
       </a>
       . Much of the development that happened in the Baroque was because of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">
        Protestant Reformation
       </a>
       and the resulting
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counter_Reformation" title="Counter Reformation">
        Counter Reformation
       </a>
       . Much of what defines the Baroque is dramatic lighting and overall visuals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Impressionism">
        Impressionism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Impressionism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">
        Impressionism
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">
        Impressionism
       </a>
       began in France in the 19th century with a loose association of artists including
       <a href="/wiki/Claude_Monet" title="Claude Monet">
        Claude Monet
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Auguste_Renoir" title="Pierre-Auguste Renoir">
        Pierre-Auguste Renoir
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_C%C3%A9zanne" title="Paul Cézanne">
        Paul Cézanne
       </a>
       who brought a new freely brushed style to painting, often choosing to paint realistic scenes of modern life outside rather than in the studio. This was achieved through a new expression of aesthetic features demonstrated by brush strokes and the impression of reality. They achieved intense colour vibration by using pure, unmixed colours and short brush strokes. The movement influenced art as a dynamic, moving through time and adjusting to new found techniques and perception of art. Attention to detail became less of a priority in achieving, whilst exploring a biased view of landscapes and nature to the artists eye.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Post-impressionism">
        Post-impressionism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Post-impressionism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Post-Impressionism" title="Post-Impressionism">
        Post-Impressionism
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Towards the end of the 19th century, several young painters took impressionism a stage further, using geometric forms and unnatural colour to depict emotions while striving for deeper symbolism. Of particular note are
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gauguin" title="Paul Gauguin">
        Paul Gauguin
       </a>
       , who was strongly influenced by Asian, African and Japanese art,
       <a href="/wiki/Vincent_van_Gogh" title="Vincent van Gogh">
        Vincent van Gogh
       </a>
       , a Dutchman who moved to France where he drew on the strong sunlight of the south, and
       <a href="/wiki/Henri_de_Toulouse-Lautrec" title="Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec">
        Toulouse-Lautrec
       </a>
       , remembered for his vivid paintings of night life in the Paris district of
       <a href="/wiki/Montmartre" title="Montmartre">
        Montmartre
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Symbolism.2C_expressionism_and_cubism">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Symbolism,_expressionism_and_cubism">
        Symbolism, expressionism and cubism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Symbolism, expressionism and cubism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">
        Modern art
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Edvard_Munch" title="Edvard Munch">
        Edvard Munch
       </a>
       , a Norwegian artist, developed his symbolistic approach at the end of the 19th century, inspired by the French impressionist
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manet" title="Manet">
        Manet
       </a>
       .
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Scream" title="The Scream">
         The Scream
        </a>
       </i>
       (1893), his most famous work, is widely interpreted as representing the universal anxiety of modern man. Partly as a result of Munch's influence, the German
       <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">
        expressionist
       </a>
       movement originated in Germany at the beginning of the 20th century as artists such as
       <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner" title="Ernst Ludwig Kirchner">
        Ernst Kirschner
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Erich_Heckel" title="Erich Heckel">
        Erich Heckel
       </a>
       began to distort reality for an emotional effect. In parallel, the style known as
       <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">
        cubism
       </a>
       developed in France as artists focused on the volume and space of sharp structures within a composition.
       <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">
        Pablo Picasso
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Georges_Braque" title="Georges Braque">
        Georges Braque
       </a>
       were the leading proponents of the movement. Objects are broken up, analyzed, and re-assembled in an abstracted form. By the 1920s, the style had developed into surrealism with
       <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dalí">
        Dali
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Magritte" title="René Magritte">
        Magritte
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">

      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">
        Printmaking
       </a>
       is creating, for artistic purposes, an image on a
       <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(printing)" title="Matrix (printing)">

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monotype" title="Monotype">

       </a>
       , the same matrix can be used to produce many examples of the print.
      </p>

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:D%C3%BCrer_Melancholia_I.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>

         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melancholia_I" title="Melancholia I">
          Melancholia I
         </a>
         (1541)

       Historically, the major techniques (also called media) involved are
       <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">
        woodcut
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Line_engraving" title="Line engraving">
        line engraving
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Etching" title="Etching">
        etching
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lithography" title="Lithography">
        lithography
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Vellum" title="Vellum">

       <span class="mw-headline" id="European_history">
        European history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Old_master_print" title="Old master print">

       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Prints in the Western tradition produced before about 1830 are known as
       <a href="/wiki/Old_master_print" title="Old master print">
        old master prints
       </a>
       . In Europe, from around 1400 AD
       <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">
        woodcut
       </a>
       , was used for master prints on paper by using printing techniques developed in the Byzantine and Islamic worlds.
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_Wolgemut" title="Michael Wolgemut">
        Michael Wolgemut
       </a>
       improved German woodcut from about 1475, and
       <a href="/wiki/Erhard_Reuwich" title="Erhard Reuwich">
        Erhard Reuwich
       </a>
       , a Dutchman, was the first to use cross-hatching. At the end of the century
       <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">
        Albrecht Dürer
       </a>
       brought the Western woodcut to a stage that has never been surpassed, increasing the status of the single-leaf woodcut.

        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Woodblock_printing_in_Japan" title="Woodblock printing in Japan">
        Woodblock printing in Japan
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Woodblock printing in Japan (Japanese: 木版画, moku hanga) is a technique best known for its use in the ukiyo-e artistic genre; however, it was also used very widely for printing books in the same period. Woodblock printing had been used in China for centuries to print books, long before the advent of movable type, but was only widely adopted in Japan surprisingly late, during the Edo period (1603–1867). Although similar to woodcut in western printmaking in some regards, moku hanga differs greatly in that water-based inks are used (as opposed to western woodcut, which uses oil-based inks), allowing for a wide range of vivid color, glazes and color transparency.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Photography">
        Photography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Photography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">
        Photography
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">
        Photography
       </a>
       is the process of making pictures by means of the action of light. Light patterns reflected or emitted from objects are recorded onto a sensitive medium or storage chip through a timed
       <a href="/wiki/Exposure_(photography)" title="Exposure (photography)">
        exposure
       </a>
       . The process is done through mechanical
       <a href="/wiki/Shutter_(photography)" title="Shutter (photography)">
        shutters
       </a>
       or electronically timed exposure of
       <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
        photons
       </a>
       into
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">
        chemical
       </a>
       processing or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digitizing" title="Digitizing">
        digitizing
       </a>
       devices known as
       <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">
        cameras
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The word comes from the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_words&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Greek words (page does not exist)">
        Greek words
       </a>
       φως
       <i>
        phos
       </i>
       ("light"), and γραφις
       <i>
        graphis
       </i>
       ("stylus", "paintbrush") or γραφη
       <i>
        graphê
       </i>
       , together meaning "drawing with light" or "representation by means of lines" or "drawing." Traditionally, the product of photography has been called a
       <a href="/wiki/Photograph" title="Photograph">
        photograph
       </a>
       . The term
       <i>
        photo
       </i>
       is an abbreviation; many people also call them
       <i>
        pictures.
       </i>
       In digital photography, the term
       <i>
        image
       </i>
       has begun to replace
       <i>
        photograph.
       </i>
       (The term
       <i>
        image
       </i>
       is traditional in geometric
       <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">
        optics
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Filmmaking">
        Filmmaking
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Filmmaking">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Filmmaking" title="Filmmaking">
        Filmmaking
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Filmmaking is the process of making a motion-picture, from an initial conception and research, through scriptwriting, shooting and recording, animation or other special effects, editing, sound and music work and finally distribution to an audience; it refers broadly to the creation of all types of films, embracing documentary, strains of theatre and literature in film, and poetic or experimental practices, and is often used to refer to video-based processes as well.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_art">
        Computer art
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Computer art">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_art" title="Computer art">
        Computer art
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Visual artists are no longer limited to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_(arts)" title="Media (arts)">
        traditional art media
       </a>
       . Computers have been used as an ever more common tool in the visual arts since the 1960s. Uses include the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Image_capture" title="Image capture">
        capturing
       </a>
       or creating of images and forms, the editing of those images and forms (including exploring multiple
       <a href="/wiki/Composition_(visual_arts)" title="Composition (visual arts)">
        compositions
       </a>
       ) and the final
       <a href="/wiki/Artistic_rendering" title="Artistic rendering">
        rendering
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">
        printing
       </a>
       (including
       <a href="/wiki/3D_printing" title="3D printing">
        3D printing
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Computer art
       </b>
       is any in which
       <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">
        computers
       </a>
       played a role in production or display. Such art can be an image, sound,
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_animation" title="Computer animation">
        animation
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">
        video
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">
        CD-ROM
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">
        DVD
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">
        video game
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">
        website
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">
        algorithm
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Performance" title="Performance">
        performance
       </a>
       or gallery installation. Many traditional disciplines are now integrating
       <a href="/wiki/Digital_data" title="Digital data">
        digital
       </a>
       technologies and, as a result, the lines between traditional works of art and
       <a href="/wiki/New_media" title="New media">
        new media
       </a>
       works created using computers have been blurred. For instance, an artist may combine traditional
       <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">
        painting
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Algorithmic_art" title="Algorithmic art">
        algorithmic art
       </a>
       and other digital techniques. As a result, defining computer art by its end product can be difficult. Nevertheless, this type of art is beginning to appear in art museum exhibits, though it has yet to prove its legitimacy as a form unto itself and this technology is widely seen in contemporary art more as a tool rather than a form as with painting.
      </p>
      <p>
       Computer usage has blurred the distinctions between
       <a href="/wiki/Illustrator" title="Illustrator">
        illustrators
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Photographer" title="Photographer">
        photographers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Image_editing" title="Image editing">
        photo editors
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/3D_modeling" title="3D modeling">
        3-D modelers
       </a>
       , and handicraft artists. Sophisticated rendering and editing software has led to multi-skilled image developers.
       <a href="/wiki/Photographer" title="Photographer">
        Photographers
       </a>
       may become
       <a href="/wiki/Digital_art" title="Digital art">
        digital artists
       </a>
       . Illustrators may become
       <a href="/wiki/Animator" title="Animator">
        animators
       </a>
       . Handicraft may be
       <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided" title="Computer-aided">
        computer-aided
       </a>
       or use
       <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">
        computer-generated imagery
       </a>
       as a template. Computer
       <a href="/wiki/Clip_art" title="Clip art">
        clip art
       </a>
       usage has also made the clear distinction between visual arts and
       <a href="/wiki/Page_layout" title="Page layout">
        page layout
       </a>
       less obvious due to the easy access and editing of clip art in the process of
       <a href="/wiki/Pagination" title="Pagination">
        paginating
       </a>
       a document, especially to the unskilled observer.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plastic_arts">
        Plastic arts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Plastic arts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Plastic_arts" title="Plastic arts">
        Plastic arts
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Plastic arts
       </b>
       is a term, now largely forgotten, encompassing art forms that involve physical manipulation of a plastic medium by moulding or modeling such as sculpture or ceramics. The term has also been applied to
       <i>
        all the visual (non-literary, non-musical) arts
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Materials that can be carved or shaped, such as stone or wood, concrete or steel, have also been included in the narrower definition, since, with appropriate tools, such materials are also capable of modulation.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       This use of the term "plastic" in the arts should not be confused with
       <a href="/wiki/Piet_Mondrian" title="Piet Mondrian">
        Piet Mondrian
       </a>
       's use, nor with the movement he termed, in French and English, "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neoplasticism" title="Neoplasticism">
        Neoplasticism
       </a>
       ."
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sculpture">
        Sculpture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Sculpture">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">
        Sculpture
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Sculpture is
       <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_space" title="Three-dimensional space">
        three-dimensional
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Work_of_art" title="Work of art">
        artwork
       </a>
       created by shaping or combining hard or
       <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">
        plastic
       </a>
       material, sound, or text and or light, commonly
       <a href="/wiki/Stone_sculpture" title="Stone sculpture">
        stone
       </a>
       (either
       <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">
        rock
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">
        marble
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">
        clay
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">
        metal
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">
        glass
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">
        wood
       </a>
       . Some sculptures are created directly by finding or
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/carving" title="wikt:carving">
        carving
       </a>
       ; others are assembled, built together and
       <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">
        fired
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">
        welded
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Molding_(process)" title="Molding (process)">
        molded
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Casting" title="Casting">
        cast
       </a>
       . Sculptures are often
       <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">
        painted
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A person who creates sculptures is called a sculptor.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because sculpture involves the use of materials that can be moulded or modulated, it is considered one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Plastic_arts" title="Plastic arts">
        plastic arts
       </a>
       . The majority of
       <a href="/wiki/Public_art" title="Public art">
        public art
       </a>
       is sculpture. Many sculptures together in a
       <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">
        garden
       </a>
       setting may be referred to as a
       <a href="/wiki/Sculpture_garden" title="Sculpture garden">
        sculpture garden
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Sculptors do not always make sculptures by hand. With increasing technology in the 20th century and the popularity of
       <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_art" title="Conceptual art">
        conceptual art
       </a>
       over technical mastery, more sculptors turned to
       <a href="/wiki/Art_fabrication" title="Art fabrication">
        art fabricators
       </a>
       to produce their artworks. With fabrication, the artist creates a design and pays a fabricator to produce it. This allows sculptors to create larger and more complex sculptures out of material like cement, metal and plastic, that they would not be able to create by hand. Sculptures can also be made with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-d_printing" title="3-d printing">
        3-d printing
       </a>
       technology.
      </p>
      <div style="clear:both;">
      </div>
      <h2>
       <span id="Copyright_definition_of_visual_art_.28US.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_definition_of_visual_art_(US)">
        Copyright definition of visual art (US)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Copyright definition of visual art (US)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the United States, the law protecting the copyright over a piece of visual art gives a more restrictive definition of "visual art".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        A “work of visual art” is —
        <br/>
        (1) a painting, drawing, print or sculpture, existing in a single copy, in a limited edition of 200 copies or fewer that are signed and consecutively numbered by the author, or, in the case of a sculpture, in multiple cast, carved, or fabricated sculptures of 200 or fewer that are consecutively numbered by the author and bear the signature or other identifying mark of the author; or
        <br/>
        (2) a still photographic image produced for exhibition purposes only, existing in a single copy that is signed by the author, or in a limited edition of 200 copies or fewer that are signed and consecutively numbered by the author.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        A work of visual art does not include —
        <br/>
        (A)(i) any poster, map, globe, chart,
        <a href="/wiki/Technical_drawing" title="Technical drawing">
         technical drawing
        </a>
        , diagram, model, applied art, motion picture or other audiovisual work, book, magazine, newspaper, periodical, data base, electronic information service, electronic publication, or similar publication;
        <br/>
        (ii) any merchandising item or advertising, promotional, descriptive, covering, or packaging material or container;
        <br/>
        (iii) any portion or part of any item described in clause (i) or (ii);
        <br/>
        (B) any
        <a href="/wiki/Work_for_hire" title="Work for hire">
         work made for hire
        </a>
        ; or
        <br/>
        (C) any work not subject to copyright protection under this title.
       </p>
      </blockquote>

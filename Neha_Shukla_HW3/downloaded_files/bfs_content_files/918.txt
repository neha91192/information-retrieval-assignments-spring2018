      <p>
       <b>
        Guerrilla filmmaking
       </b>
       refers to a form of
       <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">
        independent filmmaking
       </a>
       characterized by low budgets,
       <a href="/wiki/Skeleton_crew" title="Skeleton crew">
        skeleton crews
       </a>
       , and simple props using whatever is available. Often scenes are shot quickly in
       <a href="/wiki/Filming_location" title="Filming location">
        real locations
       </a>
       without any warning, and without obtaining
       <a href="/wiki/Filming_permit" title="Filming permit">
        filming permits
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Guerrilla filmmaking is usually done by independent filmmakers because they don't have the budget or time to obtain permits, rent out locations, or build expensive sets. Larger and more "mainstream" film studios tend to avoid guerrilla filmmaking tactics because of the risk of being sued, fined or having their reputation damaged due to negative
       <a href="/wiki/Public_relations" title="Public relations">
        PR
       </a>
       publicity.
      </p>
      <p>
       According to Yukon Film Commission Manager Mark Hill, "Guerrilla filmmaking is driven by passion with whatever means at hand".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Guerrilla_films">
        Guerrilla films
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guerrilla_filmmaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Guerrilla films">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Janet_Maslin" title="Janet Maslin">
        Janet Maslin
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
         The New York Times
        </a>
       </i>
       characterized
       <a href="/wiki/Ed_Wood" title="Ed Wood">
        Ed Wood
       </a>
       as a guerrilla filmmaker. As depicted in the biopic
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ed_Wood_(film)" title="Ed Wood (film)">
         Ed Wood
        </a>
       </i>
       , Wood stole a fake octopus for one of the scenes in his low budget films.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Film critic
       <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">
        Roger Ebert
       </a>
       described
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sweet_Sweetback%27s_Baadasssss_Song" title="Sweet Sweetback's Baadasssss Song">
         Sweet Sweetback's Baadasssss Song
        </a>
       </i>
       , directed by
       <a href="/wiki/Melvin_Van_Peebles" title="Melvin Van Peebles">
        Melvin Van Peebles
       </a>
       , as "a textbook on guerrilla filmmaking" in his review of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Baadasssss!" title="Baadasssss!">
         Baadasssss!
        </a>
       </i>
       , a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biopic" title="Biopic">
        biopic
       </a>
       about the making of
       <i>
        Sweet Sweetback
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ben Sisario of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
         The New York Times
        </a>
       </i>
       called Van Peebles "a hero of guerrilla filmmaking" who has suffered for his uncompromising vision.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Spike_Lee" title="Spike Lee">
        Spike Lee
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/She%27s_Gotta_Have_It" title="She's Gotta Have It">
         She's Gotta Have It
        </a>
       </i>
       was a guerrilla film on a budget of $175,000 which made $7,137,502 at the box office.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was Spike Lee's first feature-length film and inspired him to write the book
       <i>
        Spike Lee's Gotta Have It: Inside Guerrilla Filmmaking
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/New_Queer_Cinema" title="New Queer Cinema">
        New Queer Cinema
       </a>
       director
       <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Araki" title="Gregg Araki">
        Gregg Araki
       </a>
       shot his first two films,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Three_Bewildered_People_in_the_Night" title="Three Bewildered People in the Night">
         Three Bewildered People in the Night
        </a>
       </i>
       (1987) and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Long_Weekend_(O%27_Despair)" title="The Long Weekend (O' Despair)">
         The Long Weekend (O' Despair)
        </a>
       </i>
       (1989) using a spring-wound
       <a href="/wiki/Bolex" title="Bolex">
        Bolex
       </a>
       camera and scrap film stock, on a budget of $5,000 each.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rodriguez" title="Robert Rodriguez">
        Robert Rodriguez
       </a>
       shot the action film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/El_Mariachi" title="El Mariachi">
         El Mariachi
        </a>
       </i>
       in Spanish.
       <i>
        El Mariachi
       </i>
       , which was shot for around $7,000 with money partially raised by volunteering in medical research studies, won the Audience Award at the
       <a href="/wiki/Sundance_Film_Festival" title="Sundance Film Festival">
        Sundance Film Festival
       </a>
       in 1992. The film, originally intended for the Spanish-language low-budget home-video market, was distributed by
       <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">
        Columbia Pictures
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       . Rodriguez described his experiences making the film in his book
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Rebel_Without_a_Crew" title="Rebel Without a Crew">
         Rebel Without a Crew
        </a>
       </i>
       . The book and film would inspire other filmmakers to pick up cameras and make no-budget movies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pi_(film)" title="Pi (film)">
         Pi
        </a>
       </i>
       , directed by
       <a href="/wiki/Darren_Aronofsky" title="Darren Aronofsky">
        Darren Aronofsky
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       was made on a budget of $68,000. It proved to be a financial success at the box office ($4.6 million gross worldwide).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Aronofsky raised money for the project by selling $100 shares in the film to family and friends, and was able to pay them all back with a $50 profit per-share when the film was sold to Artisan.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Troma_Entertainment" title="Troma Entertainment">
        Troma Entertainment
       </a>
       is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_production" title="Film production">
        film production
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Film_distribution" title="Film distribution">
        distribution
       </a>
       company founded by
       <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Kaufman" title="Lloyd Kaufman">
        Lloyd Kaufman
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_Herz_(producer)" title="Michael Herz (producer)">
        Michael Herz
       </a>
       in 1974. The company produces low-budget independent films, many of which have developed
       <a href="/wiki/Cult_film" title="Cult film">
        cult followings
       </a>
       . Kaufman has been outspoken about their use of
       <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_marketing" title="Guerrilla marketing">
        guerrilla marketing
       </a>
       and tolerance of piracy, and he has written the books
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/All_I_Need_to_Know_about_Filmmaking_I_Learned_from_the_Toxic_Avenger" title="All I Need to Know about Filmmaking I Learned from the Toxic Avenger">
         All I Need to Know about Filmmaking I Learned from the Toxic Avenger
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Make_Your_Own_Damn_Movie!" title="Make Your Own Damn Movie!">
         Make Your Own Damn Movie!
        </a>
       </i>
       , which outline his philosophy of quick and inexpensive independent film.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Paranormal_Activity" title="Paranormal Activity">
         Paranormal Activity
        </a>
       </i>
       , directed by first time director
       <a href="/wiki/Oren_Peli" title="Oren Peli">
        Oren Peli
       </a>
       , was shot for approximately $10,000.
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_Cieply" title="Michael Cieply">
        Michael Cieply
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
         The New York Times
        </a>
       </i>
       described the production and release as "guerrilla style". After being well received at film festivals, Paramount put the film on a tour where fans could request a screening.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Escape_From_Tomorrow_(film)" title="Escape From Tomorrow (film)">
         Escape From Tomorrow
        </a>
       </i>
       , made for $650,000, was "shot in a guerrilla-style manner at
       <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World" title="Walt Disney World">
        Walt Disney World
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Disneyland" title="Disneyland">
        Disneyland
       </a>
       without the permission of the parks," according to Jason Guerrasio of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indiewire" title="Indiewire">
        Indiewire
       </a>
       . The film was originally expected to not be released due to fears of a lawsuit from Disney, but it was released on
       <a href="/wiki/Video_on_demand" title="Video on demand">
        video on demand
       </a>
       in October 2013.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clark:_A_gonzomentary" title="Clark: A gonzomentary">
        Clark: A Gonzomentary
       </a>
       ' was a 2012
       <a href="/wiki/Gonzo_journalism" title="Gonzo journalism">
        gonzo journalism
       </a>
       -styled mockumentary about an amateur filmmaker documenting a
       <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">
        Philadelphian
       </a>
       eccentric artist and his creative process. The guerrilla-style techniques implemented were used as part of the story itself, to represent the amateur production within the story. It was shot with a budget of less than $3,000 with a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canon_XL2" title="Canon XL2">
        Canon XL2
       </a>
       and a
       <a href="/wiki/Panasonic_AG-DVX100" title="Panasonic AG-DVX100">
        Panasonic AG-DVX100
       </a>
       . The director opted out of using a
       <a href="/wiki/Steadicam" title="Steadicam">
        steadicam
       </a>
       purposefully to achieve more shakiness. It was awarded Outstanding Lead Actor in a comedy or mockumentary by The 2013
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=LA_Web_Series_Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="LA Web Series Festival (page does not exist)">
        LA Web Series Festival
       </a>
       and deemed "a gonzomentary truly realized" by Mark Bell of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Film_Threat" title="Film Threat">
         Film Threat
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight_Rider_(film)" title="Midnight Rider (film)">
        Midnight Rider
       </a>
       ", a biopic of
       <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Allman" title="Gregg Allman">
        Gregg Allman
       </a>
       , attempted to use guerilla filmmaking methods to illegally film a sequence on a railroad bridge in active use. A train coming shattered a metal bed placed on the tracks, killing camera operator Sarah Jones. Director
       <a href="/wiki/Randall_Miller" title="Randall Miller">
        Randall Miller
       </a>
       was sentenced to two years imprisonment for involuntary manslaughter while producer Jay Sedrish received a suspended sentence of ten years' probation. Both were prohibited from working in any directorial or safety-related role for a decade.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Super_Demetrios" title="Super Demetrios">
         Super Demetrios
        </a>
       </i>
       (2011), the first
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_cinema" title="Greek cinema">
        Greek
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Superhero_film" title="Superhero film">
        superhero
       </a>
       film, made on a budget of €2,000, won the audience award at the 52nd
       <a href="/wiki/Thessaloniki_International_Film_Festival" title="Thessaloniki International Film Festival">
        Thessaloniki International Film Festival
       </a>
       with the highest number of votes in the history of the festival and became an instant cult classic,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flix_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Flix-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       "proving that Greek guerrilla cinema can survive without state funding injections" according to Giannis Zoumboulakis of
       <i>
        To Vima
       </i>
       newspaper.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-To_Vima_guerilla_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-To_Vima_guerilla-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The advent of digital cameras and home computer editing systems such as
       <a href="/wiki/Final_Cut_Pro" title="Final Cut Pro">
        Final Cut Pro
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avid_Technology" title="Avid Technology">
        Avid Media Composer
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Premiere_Pro" title="Adobe Premiere Pro">
        Premiere
       </a>
       are a contributing factor to the increase in guerrilla filmmaking. Digital editing is a cheap and easy form of editing that allows the filmmaker to edit anywhere and at a low budget.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many guerrilla filmmakers are now using professional quality digital cameras because of their cheap cost, and the ability to set up shots quickly.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many films have used digital cameras in the place of film cameras such as:
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/28_Days_Later" title="28 Days Later">
         28 Days Later
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Miami_Vice_(film)" title="Miami Vice (film)">
         Miami Vice
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sin_City_(film)" title="Sin City (film)">
         Sin City
        </a>
       </i>
       . One recent film shot on consumer camcorders that has received major attention and awards is
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/My_Date_with_Drew" title="My Date with Drew">
         My Date with Drew
        </a>
       </i>
       , directed by Brian Herzlinger. The first crowdfunded Indian
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Karnataka" title="Cinema of Karnataka">
        Kannada
       </a>
       movie "Lucia" also was effectively shot using Canon 5D.
      </p>

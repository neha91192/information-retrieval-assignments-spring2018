      <p>
       A
       <b>
        screenplay writer
       </b>
       (also called
       <b>
        screenwriter
       </b>
       for short),
       <b>
        scriptwriter
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        scenarist
       </b>
       is a writer who practices the craft of
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriting" title="Screenwriting">
        screenwriting
       </a>
       , writing
       <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">
        screenplays
       </a>
       on which
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">
        mass media
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        films
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">
        television programs
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Comics" title="Comics">
        comics
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_games" title="Video games">
        video games
       </a>
       , are based.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Profession">
        Profession
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Profession">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Screenwriting is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freelance" title="Freelance">
        freelance
       </a>
       profession. No education is required to become a professional screenwriter, just good
       <a href="/wiki/Storytelling" title="Storytelling">
        storytelling
       </a>
       abilities and
       <a href="/wiki/Imagination" title="Imagination">
        imagination
       </a>
       . Screenwriters are not hired employees but contracted freelancers. Most, if not all, screenwriters start their careers writing on speculation (spec) and so write without being hired or paid for it. If such a script is sold, it is called a
       <a href="/wiki/Spec_script" title="Spec script">
        spec script
       </a>
       . What separates a
       <a href="/wiki/Professional" title="Professional">
        professional
       </a>
       screenwriter from an
       <a href="/wiki/Amateur" title="Amateur">
        amateur
       </a>
       screenwriter is that professional screenwriters are usually represented by a
       <a href="/wiki/Talent_agent" title="Talent agent">
        talent agency
       </a>
       . Also, professional screenwriters do not often work for free, but amateur screenwriters will often work for free and are considered "writers in training." Spec scripts are usually penned by unknown professional screenwriters and amateur screenwriters.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are a legion of would-be screenwriters who attempt to enter the
       <a href="/wiki/Film_industry" title="Film industry">
        film industry
       </a>
       , but it often takes years of trial-and-error, failure, and gritty persistence to achieve success. In
       <i>
        Writing Screenplays that Sell
       </i>
       , Michael Hague writes, "Screenplays have become, for the last half of [the twentieth] century, what the
       <a href="/wiki/Great_American_Novel" title="Great American Novel">
        Great American Novel
       </a>
       was for the first half. Closet writers who used to dream of the glory of getting into print now dream of seeing their story on the big or small screen."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hauge_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hauge-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Film_industry">
        Film industry
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Film industry">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Every screenplay and teleplay begins with a thought or idea, and screenwriters use their ideas to write scripts, with the intention of selling them and having them produced.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferguson_2009_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ferguson_2009-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In some cases, the script is based on an existing property, such as a book or person's life story, which is adapted by the screenwriter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The majority of the time, a film project gets initiated by a screenwriter. The initiator of the project gets the exclusive writing assignment.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferguson_2009_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ferguson_2009-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are referred to as "exclusive" assignments or "pitched" assignments. Screenwriters who often pitch new projects, whether original or an adaptation, often do not have to worry about competing for assignments and are often more successful. When word is put out about a project a
       <a href="/wiki/Film_studio" title="Film studio">
        film studio
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Production_company" title="Production company">
        production company
       </a>
       , or producer wants done, they are referred to as "open" assignments. Open assignments are more competitive. If screenwriters are competing for an open assignment, more-established writers usually win the assignments. A screenwriter can also be approached and personally offered a writing assignment.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Script_doctoring">
        Script doctoring
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Script doctoring">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Many screenwriters also work as full or part-time
       <a href="/wiki/Script_doctor" title="Script doctor">
        script doctors
       </a>
       , attempting to better a script to suit the desires of a
       <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">
        director
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movie_studio" title="Movie studio">
        studio
       </a>
       . For instance, studio management may have a complaint that the motivations of the characters are unclear or that the dialogue is weak.
      </p>
      <p>
       Script-doctoring can be quite lucrative, especially for the better-known writers.
       <a href="/wiki/David_Mamet" title="David Mamet">
        David Mamet
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/John_Sayles" title="John Sayles">
        John Sayles
       </a>
       , for instance, fund the movies that they direct themselves, usually from their own screenplays, by writing and doctoring scripts for others. In fact, some writers make very profitable careers out of being the ninth or tenth writer to work on a piece, and they often work on projects that never see exposure to an audience of any size. Many up-and-coming screenwriters also
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ghostwrite" title="Ghostwrite">
        ghostwrite
       </a>
       projects and allow more-established screenwriters to take public credit for the project to increase the chances of it getting picked up.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Development_process">
        Development process
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development process">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       After a screenwriter finishes a project, he or she pairs with an industry-based representative, such as a
       <a href="/wiki/Film_producer" title="Film producer">
        producer
       </a>
       , director,
       <a href="/wiki/Literary_agent" title="Literary agent">
        literary agent
       </a>
       , entertainment lawyer, or entertainment executive. The partnerships often pitch their project to investors or others in a position to further a project. Once the script is sold, the writer has only the rights that were agreed with the purchaser.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferguson_2009_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ferguson_2009-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A screenwriter becomes credible by having work that is recognized, which gives the writer the opportunity to earn a higher income.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferguson_2009_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ferguson_2009-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As more films are produced independently (outside the studio system), many up-and-coming screenwriters are turning to pitch fests, screenplay contests, and independent development services to gain access to established and credible independent producers. Many development executives are now working independently to incubate their own pet projects.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Production_involvement">
        Production involvement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production involvement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Screenwriters are rarely involved in the development of a film. Sometimes they come on as advisors, or if they are established, as a producer. Some screenwriters also direct. Although many scripts are sold each year, many do not make it into production because the number of scripts that are purchased every year exceeds the number of professional directors that are working in the film and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV_industry" title="TV industry">
        TV industry
       </a>
       . When a screenwriter finishes a project and sells it to a
       <a href="/wiki/Film_studio" title="Film studio">
        film studio
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Production_company" title="Production company">
        production company
       </a>
       , TV network, or producer, he or she often has to continue networking, mainly with directors or executives, and push to have their projects "chosen" and turned into films or TV shows. If interest in a script begins to fade, a project can go dead.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Union">
        Union
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Union">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Most professional screenwriters in the U.S. are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unionized" title="Unionized">
        unionized
       </a>
       and are represented by the
       <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America" title="Writers Guild of America">
        Writers Guild of America
       </a>
       . Although membership in the WGA is recommended, it is not required of a screenwriter to join. The WGA is the final arbiter on awarding
       <a href="/wiki/WGA_screenwriting_credit_system" title="WGA screenwriting credit system">
        writing credit
       </a>
       for projects under its jurisdiction. The WGA also looks upon and verifies film copyright materials.
      </p>

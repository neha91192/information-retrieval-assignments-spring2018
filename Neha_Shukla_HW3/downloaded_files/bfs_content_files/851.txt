   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Film producer
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Film producers
       </b>
       fill a variety of roles depending upon the type of producer.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Either employed by a
       <a href="/wiki/Production_company" title="Production company">
        production company
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">
        independent
       </a>
       , producers plan and coordinate various aspects of film production, such as selecting script, coordinating writing, directing and editing, and arranging financing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During the "discovery stage", the producer finds and acknowledges promising material.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_London_Film_School_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-The_London_Film_School-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Then, unless the film is supposed to be based on an original script, the producer has to find an appropriate
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">
        screenwriter
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For various reasons, producers cannot always supervise all of the production. In this case, the main producer may appoint
       <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">
        executive producers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Line_producer" title="Line producer">
        line producers
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Unit_production_manager" title="Unit production manager">
        unit production managers
       </a>
       who represent the main producer's interests.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt22_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt22-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The producer has the last word on whether sounds or music have to be changed, including deciding if scenes have to be cut. They are in charge of selling the film or arranging distribution rights as well. The producer's role can vary significantly from project to project, based on the circumstances and funding. They generally are in charge of things like hiring staff, creating filming schedules, checking and approving locations, and much more.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:14_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:14-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_term_producers">
        The term
        <i>
         producers
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The term producers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Producers are present in every single genre of television and film and play major roles in each type of production. Although each genre might be widely different from each other, a producer's role and job is the same throughout each and every genre. A producer from a sports program can be compared to a producer of a film despite how different each genre can be.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although the primary role of the
       <a href="/wiki/Television_producer" title="Television producer">
        producer
       </a>
       can be compared to that of a filmmaker, the role is something that isn't as cut and dried as a lot of people might expect it to be. The roles are very wide-ranging, due to the fact that this occupation has gone through an enormous amount of stages and phases as time went on.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Today, in most cases the roles range from supervising the film's editing process to hiring the main staff.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The producer has a great amount of power in every single aspect of the production process. They are never left out and are heavily involved in every single stage of the production. There are times when a producer can try to find promising material to create either a feature film or a television program. If a producer decides to do this, it is usually done in the "discovery stage". The discovery stage is when a producer is actively looking for a variety of ideas, concepts, or promising scripts that could be made into a show or film. In this stage, a producer's power may even stretch to gathering workers for the selected project.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Even though a producer today can have a wide range of roles within the field, there are roles that only fit into different types of "producers". Within the industry, there are many different types of producers who are only designated to specific roles such as controlling the staff and handling the finances.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, to oversee and manage all aspects of
       <a href="/wiki/Video_production" title="Video production">
        video production
       </a>
       the role may be filled by the
       <a href="/wiki/Line_producer" title="Line producer">
        line producer
       </a>
       , but an
       <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">
        executive producer
       </a>
       would be selected to oversee all other producers work.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">
        Types
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Different types of producers and their roles within the industry today include (in no order of seniority):
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">
         Executive producer
        </a>
       </dt>
       <dd>
        They oversee all of the other producers working on the same project. They make sure that the producers are fulfilling their roles on the given production. They can also be in charge of managing the film's finances and the handling of all other business aspects of the film.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_11-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-:2-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
       <dt>
        <a href="/wiki/Line_producer" title="Line producer">
         Line producer
        </a>
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Manages the staff and day-to-day operations, and the overseeing of each and every physical aspect that is involved in the making of a film or television program. The line producer can be credited as "produced by" in certain cases.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_11-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-:2-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
       <dt>
        Supervising producer
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Supervises the creative process of screenplay development and often aids in script re-writes. They can also serve in place of the Executive producers' role of overseeing other producers.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
       <dt>
        Producer
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Within the production process they can oversee, arrange, manage and begin every single aspect. They are involved in every single stage of the overall production process.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_11-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-:2-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
       <dt>
        Co-producer
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Is a part of a team of producers that perform all of the functions and roles that a single producer would have in a single given project.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
       <dt>
        Coordinating producer or
        <a href="/wiki/Production_coordinator" title="Production coordinator">
         production coordinator
        </a>
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Coordinates the work/role of multiple producers that are trying to achieve a shared result.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
       <dt>
        Associate producer or Assistant Producer
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Helps the producer during the production process. They can sometimes be involved in coordinating others jobs, such as creating peoples schedules and hiring the main talent.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_11-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-:2-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
       <dt>
        Segment producer
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Produces one or more single specific segments of a multi segment film or television production.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
       <dt>
        Field producer
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Helps the producer by overseeing all of the production that takes place outside of the studio in specific locations for the film.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_11-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-:2-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Responsibilities">
        Responsibilities
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Responsibilities">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span id="Development_.28film_rights.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Development_(film_rights)">
        Development (film rights)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development (film rights)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       During this stage of the production process, producers bring together people like the
       <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">
        film director
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">
        cinematographer
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Production_designer" title="Production designer">
        production designer
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:03_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:03-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Unless the film is supposed to be based on an original script, the producer has to find an appropriate
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">
        screenwriter
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes.com2_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes.com2-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If an existing script is considered flawed, they are able to order a new version or make the decision to hire a
       <a href="/wiki/Script_doctor" title="Script doctor">
        script doctor
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The producer also has the final say on which
       <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">
        film director
       </a>
       , cast members, or other staff get hired.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In some cases, they also have the last word when it comes to casting questions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A producer's role will also consist of approving locations, the studio hire, the final
       <a href="/wiki/Shooting_script" title="Shooting script">
        shooting script
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Production_schedule" title="Production schedule">
        production schedule
       </a>
       , and the budget. More time and money spent in pre-production can reduce the time and money wasted during production time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:03_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:03-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-production">
        Pre-production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pre-production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       During production, the producer's job is to make sure the film stays on schedule and under budget.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:14_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:14-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They will always be in contact with directors and other key creative team members.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:03_12-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:03-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In addition to this, cast and
       <a href="/wiki/Film_crew" title="Film crew">
        film crew
       </a>
       often work at different times or places, and certain films even require a
       <a href="/wiki/Second_unit" title="Second unit">
        second unit
       </a>
       . Consequently, it is normal that the main producer will appoint
       <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">
        executive producers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Line_producer" title="Line producer">
        line producers
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Unit_production_manager" title="Unit production manager">
        unit production managers
       </a>
       who represent the main producer's interests and vision.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt22_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt22-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The executive producer for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars_(film)" title="Star Wars (film)">
         Star Wars Episode IV: A New Hope
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Empire_Strikes_Back" title="The Empire Strikes Back">
         The Empire Strikes Back
        </a>
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Jedi" title="Return of the Jedi">
         Return of the Jedi
        </a>
       </i>
       was
       <a href="/wiki/George_Lucas" title="George Lucas">
        George Lucas
       </a>
       himself, the creator of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">
         Star Wars
        </a>
       </i>
       universe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
        Production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       For various reasons, producers cannot always personally supervise all parts of their production. For example, some producers run a company which also deals with film distribution.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Also, cast and
       <a href="/wiki/Film_crew" title="Film crew">
        film crew
       </a>
       often work at different times and places, and certain films even require a
       <a href="/wiki/Second_unit" title="Second unit">
        second unit
       </a>
       . Consequently, it is normal that the main producer will appoint
       <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">
        executive producers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Line_producer" title="Line producer">
        line producers
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Unit_production_manager" title="Unit production manager">
        unit production managers
       </a>
       who represent the main producer's interests.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Post-production">
        Post-production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       During post-production, the producer has the last word on whether sounds, music, or scenes have to be changed or cut. Even if the shooting has been finished, the producers can still demand that additional scenes be filmed. In the case of a negative
       <a href="/wiki/Test_screening" title="Test screening">
        test screening
       </a>
       , producers may even demand and get an alternative film ending. This happened, for example, with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/First_Blood" title="First Blood">
         First Blood
        </a>
        .
       </i>
       The test audience reacted very negatively when Rambo died, so the producers re-shot a new ending.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In addition to this, producers work with marketing and distribution companies in order to sell the film or arrange its distribution rights.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:14_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:14-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_union">
        The union
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The union">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Within the industry, all of the producers union contracts are negotiated by
       <a href="/wiki/Alliance_of_Motion_Picture_and_Television_Producers" title="Alliance of Motion Picture and Television Producers">
        The Alliance of Motion Picture and Television Producers (AMPTP)
       </a>
       . It was founded in 1924 by the U.S Trade Association as the Association of Motion Picture Producers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:4-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was originally created to only negotiate labor contracts, but during the mid-1930s they took over all contract negotiation responsibilities that were once controlled by the
       <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Motion_Picture_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences">
        Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_28-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:4-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This alliance negotiates with a wide range of other associations when dealing with producers union contracts. These associations include, the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Federation_of_Television_and_Radio_Artists" title="American Federation of Television and Radio Artists">
        American Federation of Television and Radio Artists
       </a>
       (AFTRA), the
       <a href="/wiki/Directors_Guild_of_America" title="Directors Guild of America">
        Directors Guild of America
       </a>
       (DGA), and the
       <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild" title="Screen Actors Guild">
        Screen Actors Guild
       </a>
       (SAG).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:3-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They negotiate over eighty industry wide union agreements and on behalf of 350 producers within the industry. It has been responsible for negotiating all of these union contracts within the industry since 1982. Today, it is considered the industry's official contract negotiation representative for everyone within the industry.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Career_process">
        Career process
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Career process">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are many different ways to become a film producer.
       <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kramer" title="Stanley Kramer">
        Stanley Kramer
       </a>
       started as an
       <a href="/wiki/Film_editing" title="Film editing">
        editor
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Writer" title="Writer">
        writer
       </a>
       , while other producers started as actors or directors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       However, most producers start in a college, university or film school. On the occasion of announcing his own film school, 'École de la Cité, film producer
       <a href="/wiki/Luc_Besson" title="Luc Besson">
        Luc Besson
       </a>
       admitted that at the beginning of his career, he would have appreciated the chance to attend a film school.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Film_school" title="Film school">
        Film schools
       </a>
       and many
       <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">
        universities
       </a>
       offer
       <a href="/wiki/Academic_degree" title="Academic degree">
        degree courses
       </a>
       that include film production knowledge, with some courses that are especially designed for future film producers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These courses focus on key topics like
       <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(filmmaking)" title="Pitch (filmmaking)">
        pitching
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Script_development" title="Script development">
        script development
       </a>
       , script assessment, shooting schedule design, and budgeting.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Filmakademie2_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Filmakademie2-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_London_Film_School2_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-The_London_Film_School2-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Students can also expect practical training regarding post-production.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Training at a top producing school is one of the most efficient ways a student can show professionals they are not a rookie.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:22-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       While education is one way to begin a career as a film producer, experience is also required to land a job.
       <a href="/wiki/Internship" title="Internship">
        Internships
       </a>
       are a great way to gain experience while in school and give students a solid foundation on which to build their career. Many internships are paid, which enable students to earn money while gaining hands-on skills from industry professionals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Through internships, students get to network with people in the film industry as well. This pays off in the end when looking for jobs after school. Once an internship is over, the next step typically will be to land a junior position, such as a
       <a href="/wiki/Production_assistant" title="Production assistant">
        production assistant
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_41-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:22-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although rates can vary based on a producer's role and the location of filming, the average salary can start anywhere from $20,000 to $70,000, even doubling when working in Los Angeles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The average annual salary for a producer in the U.S. is $109,844. When examining more than 15,000 producers in the Los Angeles metropolitan area, the average annual salary is $138,640.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-entertainment.howstuffworks.com2_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-entertainment.howstuffworks.com2-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Producers can also have an agreement to take a percentage of a movie's sales.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-entertainment.howstuffworks.com2_45-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-entertainment.howstuffworks.com2-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There is no average work day for a film producer, since their tasks are changing from day to day. A producer's work hours are often irregular and can consist of very long days with the possibility of working nights and weekends.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

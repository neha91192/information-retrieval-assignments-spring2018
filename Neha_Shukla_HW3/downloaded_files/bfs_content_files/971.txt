      <p>
       <b>
        Hollywood accounting
       </b>
       (also known as Hollywood
       <a href="/wiki/Bookkeeping" title="Bookkeeping">
        bookkeeping
       </a>
       ) refers to the opaque or
       <a href="/wiki/Creative_accounting" title="Creative accounting">
        creative accounting
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accounting_methods" title="Accounting methods">
        methods
       </a>
       used by the film, video, and television industry to budget and record profits for film projects. Expenditures can be inflated to reduce or eliminate the reported
       <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">
        profit
       </a>
       of the project, thereby reducing the amount which the corporation must pay in taxes and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royalties" title="Royalties">
        royalties
       </a>
       or other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Profit-sharing_agreement" title="Profit-sharing agreement">
        profit-sharing agreements
       </a>
       , as these are based on the
       <a href="/wiki/Net_profit" title="Net profit">
        net profit
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Hollywood accounting gets its name from its prevalence in the entertainment industry—that is, in the
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        movie
       </a>
       studios of
       <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">
        Hollywood
       </a>
       at a time when most studios were located in Hollywood. Those affected can include writers and actors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <p>
       Hollywood accounting can take several forms. In one form, a subsidiary is formed to perform a given activity and the parent entity will extract money out of the subsidiary not in terms of profits but in the form of charges for certain "services".
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2015)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The specific schemes can range from the simple and obvious to the extremely complex.
      </p>
      <p>
       Three main factors in Hollywood accounting reduce the reported profit of a movie, and all have to do with the calculation of
       <a href="/wiki/Overhead_(business)" title="Overhead (business)">
        overhead
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Production overhead: Studios, on average, calculate production overhead by using a figure around 15% of total production costs.
       </li>
       <li>
        Distribution overhead:
        <a href="/wiki/Film_distributor" title="Film distributor">
         Film distributors
        </a>
        typically keep 30% of what they receive from movie theaters ("gross rentals").
       </li>
       <li>
        Marketing overhead: To determine this number, studios usually choose about 10% of all advertising costs.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       All of the above means of calculating overhead are highly controversial, even within the accounting profession. Namely, these percentages are assigned without much regard to how, in reality, these estimates relate to actual overhead costs. In short, this method does not, by any rational standard, attempt to adequately trace overhead costs.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because of this, net points,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2015)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       which are a percentage of the net income (i.e. gross income minus expenses), as opposed to a percentage of the gross income are sometimes referred to as "monkey points". The term is attributed to
       <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Murphy" title="Eddie Murphy">
        Eddie Murphy
       </a>
       , who is said to have also stated that only a fool would accept net points in their contract.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Actress
       <a href="/wiki/Lynda_Carter" title="Lynda Carter">
        Lynda Carter
       </a>
       on
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Late_Show_(1986_TV_series)" title="The Late Show (1986 TV series)">
         The Late Show with Joan Rivers
        </a>
       </i>
       commented "Don't ever settle for net profits. It's called 'creative accounting'."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Many insist on "gross points" (a percentage of some definition of
       <a href="/wiki/Gross_(economics)" title="Gross (economics)">
        gross
       </a>
       revenue) rather than net profit participation. This practice reduces the likelihood of a project showing a profit, as a production company will claim a portion of the reported box-office revenue was diverted directly to gross point participants.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hollywood_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to
       <a href="/wiki/Lucasfilm" title="Lucasfilm">
        Lucasfilm
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Jedi" title="Return of the Jedi">
         Return of the Jedi
        </a>
       </i>
       , despite having earned $475 million at the box office against a budget of $32.5 million, "has never gone into profit".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Art_Buchwald" title="Art Buchwald">
        Art Buchwald
       </a>
       received a settlement from Paramount after his lawsuit
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Buchwald_v._Paramount" title="Buchwald v. Paramount">
         Buchwald v. Paramount
        </a>
       </i>
       . The court found Paramount's actions "unconscionable", noting that it was impossible to believe that Eddie Murphy’s 1988 comedy
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Coming_to_America" title="Coming to America">
         Coming to America
        </a>
       </i>
       , which grossed US$350 million, failed to make a profit, especially since the actual production costs were less than a tenth of that. Paramount settled for $900,000,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       rather than have its accounting methods closely scrutinized.
      </p>
      <p>
       Producers
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_Uslan" title="Michael Uslan">
        Michael Uslan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Melniker" title="Benjamin Melniker">
        Benjamin Melniker
       </a>
       filed a
       <a href="/wiki/Breach_of_contract" title="Breach of contract">
        breach of contract
       </a>
       lawsuit in
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County_Board_of_Supervisors" title="Los Angeles County Board of Supervisors">
        Los Angeles County Superior Court
       </a>
       on March 26, 1992. Uslan and Melniker claimed to be "the victims of a sinister campaign of fraud and coercion that has cheated them out of continuing involvement in the production of the 1989 film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Batman_(1989_film)" title="Batman (1989 film)">
         Batman
        </a>
       </i>
       and its sequels. We were denied proper credits, and deprived of any financial rewards for our indispensable creative contribution to the success of
       <i>
        Batman
       </i>
       ."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guber_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guber-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Superior_court" title="Superior court">
        superior court
       </a>
       judge rejected the lawsuit. Total revenues of
       <i>
        Batman
       </i>
       have topped $2 billion, with Uslan claiming to have "not seen a penny more than that since our net profit participation has proved worthless."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guber_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guber-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Warner Bros. offered the pair an out-of-court pay-off, a sum described by Uslan and Melniker's attorney as "two popcorns and two
       <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">
        Cokes
       </a>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The estate of
       <a href="/wiki/Jim_Garrison" title="Jim Garrison">
        Jim Garrison
       </a>
       sued
       <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">
        Warner Bros.
       </a>
       for their share of the profits from the movie
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/JFK_(film)" title="JFK (film)">
         JFK
        </a>
       </i>
       (1991), which was based on Garrison's book
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/On_the_Trail_of_the_Assassins" title="On the Trail of the Assassins">
         On the Trail of the Assassins
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The case was settled in 1999, with Garrison's estate receiving a "very small settlement."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Winston_Groom" title="Winston Groom">
        Winston Groom
       </a>
       's price for the screenplay rights to his novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Forrest_Gump_(novel)" title="Forrest Gump (novel)">
         Forrest Gump
        </a>
       </i>
       included a 3% share of the profits; however, due to Hollywood accounting, the film's commercial success was converted into a net loss, and Groom received only $350,000 for the rights and an additional $250,000 from the studio.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">
        Stan Lee
       </a>
       , co-creator of the character
       <a href="/wiki/Spider-Man" title="Spider-Man">
        Spider-Man
       </a>
       , had a contract awarding him 10% of the net profits of anything based on his characters. The film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Spider-Man_(2002_film)" title="Spider-Man (2002 film)">
         Spider-Man
        </a>
       </i>
       (2002) made more than $800 million in revenue, but the producers claim that it did not make any profit as defined in Lee's contract, and Lee received nothing. In 2002 he filed a
       <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">
        lawsuit
       </a>
       against
       <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">
        Marvel Comics
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The 2002 film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/My_Big_Fat_Greek_Wedding" title="My Big Fat Greek Wedding">
         My Big Fat Greek Wedding
        </a>
       </i>
       was considered hugely successful for an independent film, yet according to the studio, the film lost money.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Accordingly, the cast (with the exception of
       <a href="/wiki/Nia_Vardalos" title="Nia Vardalos">
        Nia Vardalos
       </a>
       who had a separate deal) sued the studio for their part of the profits. The original producers of the film have sued
       <a href="/wiki/Gold_Circle_Films" title="Gold Circle Films">
        Gold Circle Films
       </a>
       due to Hollywood accounting practices because the studio has claimed the film, which cost less than $6 million to make and made over $350 million at the box office, lost $20 million.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">
        Peter Jackson
       </a>
       , director of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(film_series)" title="The Lord of the Rings (film series)">
         The Lord of the Rings
        </a>
       </i>
       , and his studio
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wingnut_Films" title="Wingnut Films">
        Wingnut Films
       </a>
       , brought a lawsuit against
       <a href="/wiki/New_Line_Cinema" title="New Line Cinema">
        New Line Cinema
       </a>
       after an audit. Jackson stated this is regarding "certain accounting practices". In response, New Line stated that their rights to a film of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit" title="The Hobbit">
         The Hobbit
        </a>
       </i>
       were time-limited, and since Jackson would not work with them again until the suit was settled, he would not be asked to direct
       <i>
        The Hobbit
       </i>
       , as had been anticipated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Fifteen actors are suing New Line Cinema, claiming that they have never received their 5% of revenue from merchandise sold in relation to the movie, which contains their likenesses.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Similarly, the Tolkien estate sued New Line, claiming that their contract entitled them to 7.5% of the gross receipts of the $6 billion hit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to New Line's accounts, the trilogy made "horrendous losses" and no profit at all.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_Moore" title="Michael Moore">
        Michael Moore
       </a>
       sued
       <a href="/wiki/Bob_Weinstein" title="Bob Weinstein">
        Bob
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Weinstein" title="Harvey Weinstein">
        Harvey Weinstein
       </a>
       for creative accounting to deprive him of his share of profits for the film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit_9/11" title="Fahrenheit 9/11">
         Fahrenheit 9/11
        </a>
       </i>
       . Eventually, Moore reached a settlement with the Weinsteins and the lawsuit was dropped.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A WB receipt was leaked online, showing that the hugely successful movie
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Order_of_the_Phoenix_(film)" title="Harry Potter and the Order of the Phoenix (film)">
         Harry Potter and the Order of the Phoenix
        </a>
       </i>
       ended up with a $167 million loss on paper.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is especially unusual, given that, without inflation adjustment, the
       <i>
        Harry Potter
       </i>
       film series is the second highest-grossing film series of all time both domestic and international, behind only the
       <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Cinematic_Universe" title="Marvel Cinematic Universe">
        Marvel Cinematic Universe
       </a>
       .
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Deathly_Hallows_%E2%80%93_Part_2" title="Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows – Part 2">
         Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows – Part 2
        </a>
       </i>
       remains Warner Bros.' highest-grossing movie ever.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

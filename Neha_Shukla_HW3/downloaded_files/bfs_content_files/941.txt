      <p>
       A
       <b>
        film studio
       </b>
       (also known as
       <b>
        movie studio
       </b>
       or simply
       <b>
        studio
       </b>
       ) is a major entertainment company or motion picture company that has its own privately owned
       <a href="/wiki/Studio" title="Studio">
        studio
       </a>
       facility or facilities that are used to make
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        films
       </a>
       , which is handled by the
       <a href="/wiki/Production_company" title="Production company">
        production company
       </a>
       . The majority of firms in the entertainment industry have never owned their own studios, but have rented space from other companies.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are also
       <a href="/wiki/Privately_held_company" title="Privately held company">
        independently owned
       </a>
       studio facilities, who have never produced a motion picture of their own because they are not entertainment companies or motion picture companies; they are companies who sell only studio space.
      </p>
      <p>
       The largest film studio in the world is
       <a href="/wiki/Ramoji_Film_City" title="Ramoji Film City">
        Ramoji Film City
       </a>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/Hyderabad" title="Hyderabad">
        Hyderabad
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings">
        Beginnings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_studio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Beginnings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Filmstudio_Babelsberg_Eingang.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Filmstudio_Babelsberg_Eingang.jpg/220px-Filmstudio_Babelsberg_Eingang.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Filmstudio_Babelsberg_Eingang.jpg/330px-Filmstudio_Babelsberg_Eingang.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Filmstudio_Babelsberg_Eingang.jpg/440px-Filmstudio_Babelsberg_Eingang.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Filmstudio_Babelsberg_Eingang.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a href="/wiki/Babelsberg_Studio" title="Babelsberg Studio">
          Babelsberg Studio
         </a>
         near
         <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">
          Berlin
         </a>
         was the first large-scale film studio in the world and the forerunner to
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_film_industry" title="American film industry">
          Hollywood
         </a>
         . It still produces movies every year.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1893,
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">
        Thomas Edison
       </a>
       built the first movie studio in the United States when he constructed the
       <a href="/wiki/Edison%27s_Black_Maria" title="Edison's Black Maria">
        Black Maria
       </a>
       , a tarpaper-covered structure near his laboratories in
       <a href="/wiki/West_Orange,_New_Jersey" title="West Orange, New Jersey">
        West Orange
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">
        New Jersey
       </a>
       , and asked circus, vaudeville, and dramatic actors to perform for the camera. He distributed these
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movies" title="Movies">
        movies
       </a>
       at vaudeville theaters, penny arcades, wax museums, and fairgrounds. The pioneering
       <a href="/wiki/Thanhouser_Company" title="Thanhouser Company">
        Thanhouser
       </a>
       film studio was founded in
       <a href="/wiki/New_Rochelle,_New_York" title="New Rochelle, New York">
        New Rochelle, New York
       </a>
       in 1909 by American theatrical impresario
       <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Thanhouser" title="Edwin Thanhouser">
        Edwin Thanhouser
       </a>
       . The company produced and released 1,086 films between 1910 and 1917, successfully distributing them around the world. The first
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serial_(film)" title="Serial (film)">
        film serial
       </a>
       ever,
       <a href="/wiki/The_Million_Dollar_Mystery" title="The Million Dollar Mystery">
        The Million Dollar Mystery
       </a>
       , was released by the Thanhouser company in 1914.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the early 1900s, companies started moving to
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">
        Los Angeles, California
       </a>
       . Although
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_light" title="Electric light">
        electric lights
       </a>
       were by then widely available, none were yet powerful enough to adequately expose film; the best source of illumination for motion picture production was natural sunlight. Some movies were shot on the roofs of buildings in
       <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Los_Angeles" title="Downtown Los Angeles">
        Downtown Los Angeles
       </a>
       . Early movie producers also relocated to
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">
        Southern California
       </a>
       to escape Edison's
       <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Patents_Company" title="Motion Picture Patents Company">
        Motion Picture Patents Company
       </a>
       , which controlled almost all the
       <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">
        patents
       </a>
       relevant to movie production at the time.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first movie studio in the
       <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">
        Hollywood
       </a>
       area was
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nestor_Studios" title="Nestor Studios">
        Nestor Studios
       </a>
       , opened in 1911 by
       <a href="/wiki/Al_Christie" title="Al Christie">
        Al Christie
       </a>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/David_Horsley" title="David Horsley">
        David Horsley
       </a>
       . In the same year, another 15
       <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">
        independents
       </a>
       settled in Hollywood. Other production companies eventually settled in the Los Angeles area in places such as
       <a href="/wiki/Culver_City,_California" title="Culver City, California">
        Culver City
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Burbank,_California" title="Burbank, California">
        Burbank
       </a>
       , and what would soon become known as
       <a href="/wiki/Studio_City,_Los_Angeles" title="Studio City, Los Angeles">
        Studio City
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/San_Fernando_Valley" title="San Fernando Valley">
        San Fernando Valley
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="The_.22majors.22">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='The_"majors"'>
        The "majors"
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_studio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title='Edit section: The "majors"'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Major_film_studio" title="Major film studio">
        Major film studio
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        The Big 5
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       By the mid-1920s, the evolution of a handful of American production companies into wealthy motion picture industry conglomerates that owned their own studios,
       <a href="/wiki/Film_distributor" title="Film distributor">
        distribution divisions
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Movie_theater" title="Movie theater">
        theaters
       </a>
       , and contracted with performers and other filmmaking personnel, led to the sometimes confusing equation of "studio" with "production company" in industry slang. Five large companies,
       <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">
        20th Century Fox
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/RKO_Pictures" title="RKO Pictures">
        RKO Pictures
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">
        Paramount Pictures
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">
        Warner Bros.
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">
        Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer
       </a>
       came to be known as the "Big Five," the "majors," or "the Studios" in trade publications such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">
         Variety
        </a>
       </i>
       , and their management structures and practices collectively came to be known as the "
       <a href="/wiki/Studio_system" title="Studio system">
        studio system
       </a>
       ."
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        The Little 3
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although they owned few or no theaters to guarantee sales of their films,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">
        Universal Pictures
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">
        Columbia Pictures
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/United_Artists" title="United Artists">
        United Artists
       </a>
       also fell under these rubrics, making a total of eight generally recognized "major studios". United Artists, although its controlling partners owned not one but two production studios during the Golden Age, had an often-tenuous hold on the title of "major" and operated mainly as a backer and distributor of independently produced films.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_minors">
        The minors
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_studio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The minors">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Smaller studios operated simultaneously with "the majors." These included operations such as
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_Pictures" title="Republic Pictures">
        Republic Pictures
       </a>
       , active from 1935, which produced films that occasionally matched the scale and ambition of the larger studio, and
       <a href="/wiki/Monogram_Pictures" title="Monogram Pictures">
        Monogram Pictures
       </a>
       , which specialized in series and genre releases. Together with smaller outfits such as
       <a href="/wiki/Producers_Releasing_Corporation" title="Producers Releasing Corporation">
        PRC
       </a>
       TKO and Grand National, the minor studios filled the demand for
       <a href="/wiki/B_movie" title="B movie">
        B movies
       </a>
       and are sometimes collectively referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Poverty_Row" title="Poverty Row">
        Poverty Row
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_independents">
        The independents
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_studio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The independents">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Big Five's ownership of movie theaters was eventually opposed by eight independent producers, including
       <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Goldwyn" title="Samuel Goldwyn">
        Samuel Goldwyn
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/David_O._Selznick" title="David O. Selznick">
        David O. Selznick
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">
        Walt Disney
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hal_Roach" title="Hal Roach">
        Hal Roach
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Walter_Wanger" title="Walter Wanger">
        Walter Wanger
       </a>
       . In 1948, the federal government won a
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Paramount_Pictures,_Inc." title="United States v. Paramount Pictures, Inc.">
        case against Paramount
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">
        Supreme Court
       </a>
       , which ruled that the
       <a href="/wiki/Vertical_integration" title="Vertical integration">
        vertically integrated
       </a>
       structure of the movie industry constituted an illegal
       <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">
        monopoly
       </a>
       . This decision, reached after twelve years of litigation, hastened the end of the studio system and Hollywood's "Golden Age".
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Typical_major_film_studio_components">
        Typical major film studio components
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_studio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Typical major film studio components">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       By the 1950s, the physical components of a typical major film studio had become standardized. Since then, a major film studio has usually been housed inside a
       <a href="/wiki/Physical_security" title="Physical security">
        physically secure
       </a>
       compound with a high wall, which protects filmmaking operations from unwanted interference from
       <a href="/wiki/Paparazzi" title="Paparazzi">
        paparazzi
       </a>
       and crazed
       <a href="/wiki/Fan_(person)" title="Fan (person)">
        fans
       </a>
       of leading
       <a href="/wiki/Movie_star" title="Movie star">
        movie stars
       </a>
       . Movement in and out of the studio is normally limited to specific
       <a href="/wiki/Gate" title="Gate">
        gates
       </a>
       (often capped with grand decorative arches), where visitors must stop at a
       <a href="/wiki/Boom_barrier" title="Boom barrier">
        boom barrier
       </a>
       and explain the purpose of their visit to a
       <a href="/wiki/Security_guard" title="Security guard">
        security guard
       </a>
       . Studio premises generally feature multiple
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_stage" title="Sound stage">
        sound stages
       </a>
       along with an outside
       <a href="/wiki/Backlot" title="Backlot">
        backlot
       </a>
       , as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Office" title="Office">
        offices
       </a>
       for studio executives and production companies. There is normally a studio "commissary", which is the traditional term in the film industry for what other industries call a company
       <a href="/wiki/Cafeteria" title="Cafeteria">
        cafeteria
       </a>
       . Early
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">
        nitrate film
       </a>
       was notoriously flammable, and sets were and are still very flammable, which is why film studios built in the early-to-mid 20th century have
       <a href="/wiki/Water_tower" title="Water tower">
        water towers
       </a>
       to facilitate
       <a href="/wiki/Firefighting" title="Firefighting">
        firefighting
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Film_to_television">
        Film to television
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_studio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Film to television">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Halfway through the 1950s, with
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television
       </a>
       proving to be a lucrative enterprise not destined to disappear any time soon—as many in the film industry had once hoped—movie studios were increasingly being used to produce programming for the burgeoning medium. Some midsize film companies, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_Pictures" title="Republic Pictures">
        Republic Pictures
       </a>
       , eventually sold their studios to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_production_company" title="Television production company">
        TV production concerns
       </a>
       , which were eventually bought by larger studios, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">
        American Broadcasting Company
       </a>
       which was purchased by
       <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">
        The Walt Disney Company
       </a>
       in 1996.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Today">
        Today
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_studio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Today">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       With the growing diversification of studios into such fields as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_games" title="Video games">
        video games
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_parks" title="Theme parks">
        theme parks
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Home_video" title="Home video">
        home video
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Publishing" title="Publishing">
        publishing
       </a>
       , they have become multi-national corporations. As the studios increased in size they began to rely on production companies, like
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.J._Abrams" title="J.J. Abrams">
        J.J. Abrams
       </a>
       '
       <a href="/wiki/Bad_Robot_Productions" title="Bad Robot Productions">
        Bad Robot Productions
       </a>
       , to handle many of the creative and physical production details of their feature films. Instead the studios transformed into financing and distribution entities for the films made by their affiliated production companies. With the decreasing cost of CG and
       <a href="/wiki/Visual_effects" title="Visual effects">
        visual effects
       </a>
       , many studios sold large chunks of their once massive studio spaces or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backlots" title="Backlots">
        backlots
       </a>
       to private real-estate developers.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Century_City" title="Century City">
        Century City
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">
        Los Angeles
       </a>
       was once part of the
       <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">
        20th Century Fox
       </a>
       backlot, which was among the largest and most famous of the studio lots. In most cases portions of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backlots" title="Backlots">
        backlots
       </a>
       were retained and are available for rental by various
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        film
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television
       </a>
       productions. Some studios offer tours of their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backlots" title="Backlots">
        backlots
       </a>
       , while
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Pictures" title="Universal Pictures">
        Universal Pictures
       </a>
       allows visitors to its adjacent
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios_Hollywood" title="Universal Studios Hollywood">
        Universal Studios Hollywood
       </a>
       theme park to take a tram tour of the
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios_Lot" title="Universal Studios Lot">
        backlot
       </a>
       where films such as
       <a href="/wiki/Psycho_(1960_film)" title="Psycho (1960 film)">
        <i>
         Psycho
        </i>
       </a>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Back_to_the_Future" title="Back to the Future">
         Back to the Future
        </a>
       </i>
       were once shot.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_film_and_the_studios">
        Independent film and the studios
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_studio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Independent film and the studios">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the 1980s and 90s, as the cost of professional 16mm film equipment decreased, along with the emergence of non-film innovations such as
       <a href="/wiki/S-VHS" title="S-VHS">
        S-VHS
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mini-DV" title="Mini-DV">
        Mini-DV
       </a>
       cameras, many young filmmakers began to make films outside the "studio system". Filmmakers such as
       <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jarmusch" title="Jim Jarmusch">
        Jim Jarmusch
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rodriguez" title="Robert Rodriguez">
        Robert Rodriguez
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Steven_Soderbergh" title="Steven Soderbergh">
        Steven Soderbergh
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Tarantino" title="Quentin Tarantino">
        Quentin Tarantino
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Smith" title="Kevin Smith">
        Kevin Smith
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Linklater" title="Richard Linklater">
        Richard Linklater
       </a>
       made films that pushed boundaries in ways the studios were then reluctant to do. In response to these films, many distributed by "mini-studios" like
       <a href="/wiki/Miramax" title="Miramax">
        Miramax
       </a>
       the "majors" created their own in-house mini-studios meant to focus on edgier "independent" content.
       <a href="/wiki/Focus_Features" title="Focus Features">
        Focus Features
       </a>
       was created by
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Pictures" title="Universal Pictures">
        Universal Pictures
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_Searchlight" title="Fox Searchlight">
        Fox Searchlight
       </a>
       was created by
       <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">
        20th Century Fox
       </a>
       for this purpose.
      </p>

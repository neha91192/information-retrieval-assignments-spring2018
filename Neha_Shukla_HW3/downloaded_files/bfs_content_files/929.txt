      <p>
       A
       <b>
        freelancer
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        freelance worker
       </b>
       is a term commonly used for a person who is
       <a href="/wiki/Self-employment" title="Self-employment">
        self-employed
       </a>
       and is not necessarily committed to a particular employer long-term. Freelance workers are sometimes represented by a company or a
       <a href="/wiki/Employment_agency" title="Employment agency">
        temporary agency
       </a>
       that resells freelance labor to clients; others work independently or use professional associations or websites to get work.
      </p>
      <p>
       While the term "
       <a href="/wiki/Independent_contractor" title="Independent contractor">
        independent contractor
       </a>
       " would be used in a higher
       <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">
        register
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       to designate the tax and employment classes of this type of worker, the term freelancing is most common in culture and creative industries and this term specifically motions to participation therein.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Fields, professions, and industries where freelancing is predominant include:
       <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">
        music
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">
        writing
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Acting" title="Acting">
        acting
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">
        computer programming
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Web_design" title="Web design">
        web design
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">
        translating
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illustrating" title="Illustrating">
        illustrating
       </a>
       , film and video production, and other forms of
       <a href="/wiki/Piece_work" title="Piece work">
        piece work
       </a>
       which some cultural theorists consider as central to the
       <a href="/wiki/Cognitive-cultural_economy" title="Cognitive-cultural economy">
        cognitive-cultural economy
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Freelance_practices_and_compensation">
        Freelance practices and compensation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freelancer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Freelance practices and compensation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to the 2012 Freelance Industry Report compiled primarily about
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North America
       </a>
       freelancing, nearly half of freelancers do writing work, with 18% of freelancers listing writing as a primary skill, 10% editing/copy-editing, and 10% as copy-writing. 20% of freelancers listed their primary skills as design. Next on the list was translating (8%), web development (5.5%), and marketing (4%).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freelance_Industry_Report_2012_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Freelance_Industry_Report_2012-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Elance" title="Elance">
        Elance
       </a>
       , now
       <a href="/wiki/Upwork" title="Upwork">
        Upwork
       </a>
       a web platform that connects contract freelancers with businesses, surveyed its members and 39% listed writing and editing as their main skill set.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Depending on the industry, freelance work practices vary and have changed over time. In some industries such as consulting, freelancers may require clients to sign written
       <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">
        contracts
       </a>
       . While in journalism or writing, freelancers may work for free or do work "on spec" to build their reputations or a relationship with a publication. Some freelancers may provide written estimates of work and request deposits from clients.
      </p>
      <p>
       Payment for freelance work also depends on industry, skills, and experience. Freelancers may charge by the day, hour, a piece rate, or on a per-project basis. Instead of a flat rate or fee, some freelancers have adopted a
       <a href="/wiki/Value-based_pricing" title="Value-based pricing">
        value-based pricing
       </a>
       method based on the perceived value of the results to the client. By custom, payment arrangements may be upfront, percentage upfront, or upon completion. For more complex projects, a contract may set a payment schedule based on milestones or outcomes. One of the drawbacks of freelancing is that there is no guaranteed payment, and the work can be highly
       <a href="/wiki/Precarious_work" title="Precarious work">
        precarious
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In writing and other artistic fields, "freelance" and its derivative terms are often reserved for workers who create works on their own initiative and then seek a publisher. They typically retain the copyright to their works and sell the rights to publishers in time-limited contracts. Traditionally, works would be submitted to publishers, where they would become part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slushpile" title="Slushpile">
        slushpile
       </a>
       , and would either elicit an offer to buy (an "acceptance letter") or a
       <a href="/wiki/Rejection_slip" title="Rejection slip">
        rejection slip
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       People who create intellectual property under a
       <a href="/wiki/Work_for_hire" title="Work for hire">
        work for hire
       </a>
       situation (according to the publishers' or other customers' specifications) are sometimes referred to as "independent contractors" or other similar terms. Creators give up their rights to their works in a "works made for hire" situation, a category of intellectual property defined in U.S. copyright law — Section 101, Copyright Act of 1976 (17 USC §101). The protection of the intellectual property rights that give the creator of the work are considered to have been sold into a work for hire agreement. of employees, however in a contractual rather than employment relationship.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">
        Demographics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freelancer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Demographics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The total number of freelancers in USA is inexact, as the most recent governmental report on independent contractors was published in 2005 by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Labor" title="U.S. Department of Labor">
        U.S. Department of Labor
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Labor_Statistics" title="Bureau of Labor Statistics">
        Bureau of Labor Statistics
       </a>
       . At that time, there were approximately 10.3 million United States workers (7.4% of the workforce) employed as independent contractors of all sorts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indie2005_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-indie2005-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2011,
       <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Eisenach" title="Jeffrey Eisenach">
        Jeffrey Eisenach
       </a>
       , an economist at George Mason University, estimated that number of freelancers had grown by one million.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       While in 2012, the
       <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen_Group" title="Aberdeen Group">
        Aberdeen Group
       </a>
       , a private research company, estimated that 26% (approx. 81 million) of the United States population was is a part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contingent_workforce" title="Contingent workforce">
        contingent workforce
       </a>
       , a category of casual labor that includes freelancing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2013, the
       <a href="/wiki/Freelancers_Union" title="Freelancers Union">
        Freelancers Union
       </a>
       estimated that 1 in 3 workers in the United States was self-employed (approximately 42 million), with more than four million (43%) of those self-employed workers members of the
       <a href="/wiki/Creative_class" title="Creative class">
        creative class
       </a>
       , a stratum of work specifically associated with freelance industries, such as knowledge workers, technologists, professional writers, artists, entertainers, and media workers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2016, the Freelancers Union estimated that 35% of the workforce in the United States was self-employed (approximately 55 million). This workforce earned an estimated $1 trillion from freelancing in 2016—a significant share of the U.S. economy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2017, a study by MBO Partners estimated the total number of self-employed Americans aged 21 and above to be 40.9 million.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The total number of freelancers in UK is also inexact; however, figures from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Office_of_National_Statistics" title="Office of National Statistics">
        Office of National Statistics
       </a>
       show that people working mainly at or from home rose from 9.2% in 2001 to 10.7% in 2011.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It has been estimated, however, that there are approximately 1.7 million freelancers in the UK.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Freelancing is a gendered form of work.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freelance_Industry_Report_2012_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Freelance_Industry_Report_2012-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 2012 Freelance Industry Report estimates that more than 71% of freelancers are women between the ages of 30 and 50. Surveys of other specific areas of freelancing have similar trends. Demographic research on
       <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Mechanical_Turk" title="Amazon Mechanical Turk">
        Amazon Mechanical Turk
       </a>
       reveals that the majority of North American Mechanical Turk workers are women.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Catherine McKercher's research on journalism as a profession has showcased that while media organizations are still male-dominated, the reverse is true for freelance journalists and editors, whose ranks are mainly women.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">
        Benefits
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freelancer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Benefits">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Freelancers have a variety of reasons for freelancing, the perceived benefits differ by gender, industry, and lifestyle. For instance, the 2012 Freelance Industry Report reported that men and women freelance for different reasons. Female survey respondents indicated that they prefer the scheduling freedom and flexibility that freelancing offers, while male survey respondents indicated they freelance to follow or pursue personal passions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freelance_Industry_Report_2012_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Freelance_Industry_Report_2012-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Freelancing also enables people to obtain higher levels of employment in isolated communities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Freelancing is also taken up by workers who have been laid-off, who cannot find full-time employment,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freelance_Industry_Report_2012_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Freelance_Industry_Report_2012-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or for those industries such as journalism which are relying increasingly on contingent labor rather than full-time staff.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKercher_2009_370-374_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-McKercher_2009_370-374-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Freelancers also consist of students trying to make ends meet during the semester. In interviews and on blogs about freelancing, freelancers list choice and flexibility as a benefit.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Drawbacks">
        Drawbacks
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freelancer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Drawbacks">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Freelancing, like other forms of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casual_employment" title="Casual employment">
        casual labor
       </a>
       , can be
       <a href="/wiki/Precarious_work" title="Precarious work">
        precarious work
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Websites, books, portals and organizations for freelancers often feature advice on getting and keeping a steady work stream.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Beside the lack of job security, many freelancers also report the ongoing hassle of dealing with employers who don't pay on time and the possibility of long periods without work. Additionally, freelancers do not receive employment benefits such as a
       <a href="/wiki/Pension" title="Pension">
        pension
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sick_leave" title="Sick leave">
        sick leave
       </a>
       , paid holidays, bonuses or
       <a href="/wiki/Health_insurance" title="Health insurance">
        health insurance
       </a>
       , which can be a serious hardship for freelancers residing in countries such as the US without
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_health_care" title="Universal health care">
        universal health care
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Freelancers often earn less than their employed counterparts. While most freelancers have at least ten years of experience prior to working independently,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freelance_Industry_Report_2012_3-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Freelance_Industry_Report_2012-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       experienced freelancers do not always earn an income equal to that of full-time employment. Feedback from members suggests that web portals such as
       <a href="/wiki/Freelancer.com" title="Freelancer.com">
        Freelancer.com
       </a>
       tend to attract low paying clients that, although demanding very high standards, pay ~$10 per hour or less. Low-cost suppliers frequently offer to work at rates as low as $1–$2 per hour. Because most projects require bidding, professionals will not bid because they refuse to work at such rates. This has the effect of reducing the overall quality of the services provided.
      </p>
      <p>
       According to research conducted in 2005 by the Professional Writers Association of Canada on Canadian journalists and editors, there is a wage gap between staff and freelance journalists. While the typical Canadian full-time freelancer is female, between 35-55, holding a college diploma and often a graduate degree, she typically earns about $29,999 Canadian dollars before taxes. Meanwhile, a staff journalist of similar age and experience level working full-time at outlets such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Citizen" title="Ottawa Citizen">
        Ottawa Citizen
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Gazette" title="Montreal Gazette">
        Montreal Gazette
       </a>
       newspapers, earned at least $63,500 Canadian dollars that year, the top scale rate negotiated by the union,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Newspaper_Guild" title="The Newspaper Guild">
        The Newspaper Guild
       </a>
       -Communications Workers of America.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKercher_2009_370-374_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-McKercher_2009_370-374-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Given the gendered stratification of journalism, with more women working as freelancers than men, this disparity in income can be interpreted as a form of
       <a href="/wiki/Gender_pay_gap" title="Gender pay gap">
        gender pay gap
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Professional_Writers_Association_of_Canada" title="Professional Writers Association of Canada">
        Professional Writers Association of Canada
       </a>
       report showed no significant difference between the earnings of male and female freelancers, though part-time freelancers generally earned less than full-time freelancers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Working_from_home" title="Working from home">
        Working from home
       </a>
       is often cited as an attractive feature of freelancing, yet research suggests working from home introduces new sets of constraints for the process of doing work, particularly for married women with families, who continue to bear the brunt of household chores and child care despite increases in their paid work time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For instance, three years of ethnographic research about teleworkers in Australia conducted by Melissa Gregg, a Principal Engineer and Researcher in Residence for the Intel Science and Technology Center for Social Computing at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UC_Irvine" title="UC Irvine">
        UC Irvine
       </a>
       , raises concerns over how both physical isolation and continuous access enabled with networked digital media puts pressure on homeworkers to demonstrate their commitments through continual responses by email and to conceal their family or home life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Internet_and_online_marketplaces">
        Internet and online marketplaces
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freelancer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Internet and online marketplaces">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">
        Internet
       </a>
       has opened up many freelance opportunities, expanded available markets, and has contributed to service sector growth in many economies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIA_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CIA-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Offshore_outsourcing" title="Offshore outsourcing">
        Offshore outsourcing
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Online_outsourcing" title="Online outsourcing">
        online outsourcing
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing" title="Crowdsourcing">
        crowdsourcing
       </a>
       are heavily reliant on the Internet to provide economical access to remote workers, and frequently leverage technology to manage workflow to and from the employer. Much computer freelance work is being outsourced to developing countries outside the United States and Europe.
      </p>
      <p>
       Freelance marketplaces provide a
       <a href="/wiki/Marketplace" title="Marketplace">
        marketplace
       </a>
       for freelancers and buyers. Service providers or sellers create a profile where they include a description of the services they offer, examples of their work, and, in some cases, information about their rates. Buyers register and complete a basic profile, and then post projects outlining their requirements. Buyers will then bid for these projects on a fixed price or hourly basis.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many of these websites have
       <a href="/wiki/User_review" title="User review">
        user review
       </a>
       sections that affect the reputation of freelancers who list there, and which may be manipulated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Freelance marketplaces have globalized competition for some jobs, allowing workers in high- and low-income countries to compete with one another.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to a 2016 study by the McKinsey Global Institute, 15% of independent workers used online marketplaces to find work.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       These marketplaces, including
       <a href="/wiki/Fiverr" title="Fiverr">
        Fiverr
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lyft" title="Lyft">
        Lyft
       </a>
       , have been criticized as exploiting workers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_aspects">
        Legal aspects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freelancer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legal aspects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Many periodicals and newspapers offer the option of ghost signing, when a freelance writer signs with an editor but their name is not listed on the byline of their article(s). This allows the writer to receive benefits while still being classified as a freelancer, and independent of any set organization. In some countries this can lead to taxation issues (e.g., so-called
       <a href="/wiki/IR35" title="IR35">
        IR35
       </a>
       violations in the UK). Ghost signing has little bearing on whether a writer is a freelancer or employee in the US.
      </p>
      <p>
       Freelancers often must handle contracts, legal issues, accounting, marketing, and other business functions by themselves. If they do choose to pay for professional services, they can sometimes turn into significant
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Out-of-pocket_expenses" title="Out-of-pocket expenses">
        out-of-pocket expenses
       </a>
       . Working hours can extend beyond the standard working day and working week.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">
        European Commission
       </a>
       does not define “freelancers” in any legislative text. However, the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">
        European Commission
       </a>
       defines a self-employed person as someone: “pursuing a gainful activity for their own account, under the conditions laid down by national law”. In the exercise of such an activity, the personal element is of special importance and such exercise always involves a large measure of independence in the accomplishment of the professional activities. This definition comes from Directive (2010/41/EU) on the application of the principle of equal treatment between men and women engaged in an activity in a self-employed capacity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Directive_(2010/41/EU)_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Directive_(2010/41/EU)-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The European Forum of Independent Professionals defines freelancers as: “a highly-skilled subset of self-employed workers, without employers nor employees, offering specialised services of an intellectual and knowledge-based nature”. Independent professionals work on a flexible basis in a range of creative, managerial, scientific and technical occupations; they are not a homogeneous group and as such, they cannot be considered or investigated as a whole. They are generally characterised by a large portion of autonomy, a high labour productivity, knowledge intensive performance, social commitment and a large dose of entrepreneurship and specialisation.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       , the perceived disadvantages of being freelance have led the European Union to research the area, producing draft papers
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       that would, if enforced, make it illegal for companies or organizations to employ freelancers directly, unless the freelancer was entitled to benefits such as pension contributions and holiday pay. In the UK, where the terms of integration into the EU have and are being hotly debated, this would lead to a significant reshaping of the way freelance work is dealt with and have a major impact on industry; employers would be required either to give freelancers the contractual rights of employees or employ only freelancers already being employed by agencies or other organizations granting them these rights. However, the White Papers that recommend such moves have not yet been adopted in the EU, and the potential impact on UK employment laws is being opposed by key UK organizations lobbying the government to negotiate over the acceptance of EU legislation in such areas.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The legal definition of a sole trader requires that he/she must have more than one client or customer which promotes the freelancing ethos.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the U.S. in 2009, federal and state agencies began increasing their oversight of freelancers and other workers whom employers classify as
       <a href="/wiki/Independent_contractor" title="Independent contractor">
        independent contractors
       </a>
       . The U.S. Government Accountability Office (GAO)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GAO_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-GAO-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       recommended that the Secretary of Labor have its Wage and Hour Division "focus on misclassification of employees as independent contractors during targeted investigations." The increased regulation is meant to ensure workers are treated fairly and that companies are not misclassifying workers as independent contractors to avoid paying appropriate employment taxes and contributions to workers’ compensation and unemployment compensation.
      </p>
      <p>
       At the same time, this increased enforcement is affecting companies whose business models are based on using non-employee workers, as well as independent professionals who have chosen to work as independent contractors. For example, book publishing companies have traditionally outsourced certain tasks like indexing and proofreading to individuals working as independent contractors. Self-employed accountants and attorneys have traditionally hired out their services to accounting and law firms needing assistance. The U.S. Internal Revenue Service
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IRS_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IRS-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       offers some guidance on what constitutes
       <a href="/wiki/Self-employment" title="Self-employment">
        self-employment
       </a>
       , but states have enacted stricter laws to address how independent contractors should be defined. For example, a Massachusetts law
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mass_A.G._32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mass_A.G.-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       states that companies can hire independent contractors only to perform work that is "outside the usual course of business of the employer," meaning workers working on the company's core business must be classified as employees. According to this statute,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MGL_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MGL-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a software engineering firm cannot outsource work to a software engineering consultant, without hiring the consultant as an employee. The firm could, however, hire an independent contractor working as an electrician, interior decorator, or painter. This raises questions about the common practice of consulting, because a company would typically hire a
       <a href="/wiki/Management_consulting" title="Management consulting">
        management consulting
       </a>
       firm or self-employed
       <a href="/wiki/Consultant" title="Consultant">
        consultant
       </a>
       to address business-specific needs that are not "outside the usual course of business of the employer."
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freelancer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although it is commonly attributed to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Walter_Scott" title="Sir Walter Scott">
        Sir Walter Scott
       </a>
       (1771–1832) in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ivanhoe" title="Ivanhoe">
         Ivanhoe
        </a>
       </i>
       (1820) to describe a "medieval
       <a href="/wiki/Mercenary" title="Mercenary">
        mercenary
       </a>
       warrior" or "free-
       <a href="/wiki/Lance" title="Lance">
        lance
       </a>
       " (indicating that the lance is not sworn to any lord's services, not that the lance is available free of charge),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a previous appearance occurs in
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_N._Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas N. Brown (page does not exist)">
        Thomas N. Brown
       </a>
       in
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Life_and_Times_of_Hugh_Miller&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Life and Times of Hugh Miller (page does not exist)">
         The Life and Times of Hugh Miller
        </a>
       </i>
       (1809).,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       p 185. It changed to a figurative noun around the 1860s and was recognized as a verb in 1903 by authorities in
       <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">
        etymology
       </a>
       such as the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">
         Oxford English Dictionary
        </a>
        .
       </i>
       Only in modern times has the term morphed from a noun (a freelance) into an adjective (a freelance journalist), a verb (a journalist who freelances) and an adverb (she worked freelance), as well as into the noun "freelancer".
      </p>

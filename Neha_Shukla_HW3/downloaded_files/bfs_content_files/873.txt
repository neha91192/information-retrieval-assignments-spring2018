    Scriptment
   </h1>

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        scriptment
       </b>
       is a written work by a movie or television
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">
        screenwriter
       </a>
       that combines elements of a
       <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">
        script
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Film_treatment" title="Film treatment">
        treatment
       </a>
       , especially the dialogue elements, which are formatted the same as in a screenplay. It is a more elaborate document than a standard draft treatment. Some films have been shot using only a scriptment.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
        Origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scriptment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The term "scriptment" was originally coined by filmmaker
       <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">
        James Cameron
       </a>
       , possibly during his early involvement in the development of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Spider-Man_in_film" title="Spider-Man in film">
         Spider-Man
        </a>
       </i>
       film series. In that effort, after the success of his 1984 film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Terminator" title="The Terminator">
         The Terminator
        </a>
       </i>
       , Cameron wrote a 57-page scriptment for the first proposed
       <i>
        Spider-Man
       </i>
       film, which was used by screenwriter
       <a href="/wiki/David_Koepp" title="David Koepp">
        David Koepp
       </a>
       to write the first draft, incorporating it nearly word for word.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TotalFilmJan10_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TotalFilmJan10-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Cameron's scriptment for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Titanic_(1997_film)" title="Titanic (1997 film)">
         Titanic
        </a>
       </i>
       (1997) was 131 pages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The term became more widely known when Cameron's 1994 scriptment for the 2009 film
       <a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">
        <i>
         Avatar
        </i>
       </a>
       was leaked on the internet during pre-production, although other directors, such as
       <a href="/wiki/John_Hughes_(filmmaker)" title="John Hughes (filmmaker)">
        John Hughes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Zak_Penn" title="Zak Penn">
        Zak Penn
       </a>
       , had written scriptments before. The scriptment for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">
         Avatar
        </a>
       </i>
       (2009) and its notoriety caused the spread of the term.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Elements">
        Elements
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scriptment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Elements">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A scriptment borrows characteristics from both a regular
       <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">
        screenplay
       </a>
       and a film treatment and is comparable to a
       <a href="/wiki/Step_outline" title="Step outline">
        step outline
       </a>
       : the main text body is similar to an elaborate draft treatment, while usually only major sequences receive scene location headings (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Screenplay_slug_line" title="Screenplay slug line">
        slug lines
       </a>
       ), which is different from the extensive slug line formatting in standard screenplays, where every new scene or shot begins with an INT./EXT. DAY/NIGHT slug line set above the description or dialogue. However, just as a treatment can be short or long, a scriptment can exist in various degrees of completion depending on how much time the writer has devoted to it and a more fully developed one could have all slug lines in place, a great deal of dialogue, and only require the producer's (or a writing partner's) okay on the direction the finished script should take before proceeding further.
      </p>
      <p>
       In a scriptment, scenes and shots may be separated as paragraphs or sentences and, if it is the writer's style, can also include an occasional explanatory note, such as might be important in an adaptation or a sequel. As with standard treatments, much of the dialogue is summarized in action. The longer the scriptment, however, the more likely it contains dialog scenes that are fully developed. Single words or brief phrases of dialogue can be included within the description and lengthier exchanges are formatted exactly as a regular screenplay, which is the main reason for the "script" part of the term.
      </p>
      <p>
       The longer the scriptment, the more likely it is written shot to shot as opposed to scene by scene; thus, a long, detailed scriptment does not necessarily equate to a longer movie, as a typical 90-120 page screenplay written with master scenes contains many more individual shots than are immediately apparent.
      </p>
      <p>
       A scriptment can begin with FADE IN: top left and conclude with a centered THE END. It can have a title page like a script and lengthier treatment. It is written single spaced with an empty space between paragraphs and other elements and the pages are numbered in the upper right corner, just as in a screenplay.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Work-in-progress_manuscript">
        Work-in-progress manuscript
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scriptment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Work-in-progress manuscript">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Directors and screenwriters write scriptments as an intermediate stage in development from the draft treatment to the first draft of the screenplay. Like a draft treatment, a scriptment can be anywhere from 20 to 80 or more pages, while regular presentation treatments or
       <a href="/wiki/Step_outline" title="Step outline">
        outlines
       </a>
       only summarize the plot, typically in not more than 30 pages.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        For the
        <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">
         Batman
        </a>
        feature film
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Knight_(film)" title="The Dark Knight (film)">
          The Dark Knight
         </a>
        </i>
        (2008), writer
        <a href="/wiki/David_S._Goyer" title="David S. Goyer">
         David S. Goyer
        </a>
        and the film's director
        <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Nolan" title="Christopher Nolan">
         Christopher Nolan
        </a>
        wrote a scriptment that was then used by Nolan and his brother
        <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Nolan" title="Jonathan Nolan">
         Jonathan Nolan
        </a>
        to expand further into a finished screenplay. "I wrote what you'd call a 'scriptment' with Chris over an accelerated month long period, and then we handed it off to his brother [Jonathan], who did the first pass," explains Goyer.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The sci-fi movie
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Transformers:_Revenge_of_the_Fallen" title="Transformers: Revenge of the Fallen">
          Transformers: Revenge of the Fallen
         </a>
        </i>
        (2009) reportedly used a scriptment during the screenplay writing process by
        <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Orci" title="Roberto Orci">
         Roberto Orci
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Alex_Kurtzman" title="Alex Kurtzman">
         Alex Kurtzman
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Ehren_Kruger" title="Ehren Kruger">
         Ehren Kruger
        </a>
        and director
        <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bay" title="Michael Bay">
         Michael Bay
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Filmmaker
        <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Spitzer" title="Wayne Spitzer">
         Wayne Spitzer
        </a>
        used a scriptment while writing the adaptation of author Algernon Blackwood’s 1907 supernatural short story "
        <a href="/wiki/The_Willows_(story)" title="The Willows (story)">
         The Willows
        </a>
        ".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Comic book writer
        <a href="/wiki/Warren_Ellis" title="Warren Ellis">
         Warren Ellis
        </a>
        has written that he sometimes works in the scriptment style.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Presentation_manuscript">
        Presentation manuscript
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scriptment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Presentation manuscript">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A scriptment can also be a presentation document; that is, one that is sold or handed in as the finished work.
      </p>

       <li>

        <i>

        </i>

      <p>
       <b>
        Cinematography
       </b>
       (also called
       <i>
        Direction of Photography
       </i>
       ) is the science or art of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion-picture" title="Motion-picture">
        motion-picture
       </a>
       photography by recording light or other
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">
        electromagnetic radiation
       </a>
       , either electronically by means of an
       <a href="/wiki/Image_sensor" title="Image sensor">
        image sensor
       </a>
       , or chemically by means of a light-sensitive material such as
       <a href="/wiki/Film_stock" title="Film stock">
        film stock
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Typically,
       <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">
        cinematographers
       </a>
       use a lens to repeatedly focus the light reflected from objects into
       <a href="/wiki/Real_image" title="Real image">
        real images
       </a>
       on the light-sensitive surface inside a camera during a questioned
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Exposure_(photography)" title="Exposure (photography)">
        exposure
       </a>
       , creating multiple images. With an electronic image-sensor, this produces an
       <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">
        electrical charge
       </a>
       at each
       <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">
        pixel
       </a>
       , which is
       <a href="/wiki/Video_processing" title="Video processing">
        electronically processed
       </a>
       and stored in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_file" title="Video file">
        video file
       </a>
       for subsequent display or processing. The result with
       <a href="/wiki/Photographic_emulsion" title="Photographic emulsion">
        photographic emulsion
       </a>
       is a series of invisible
       <a href="/wiki/Latent_image" title="Latent image">
        latent images
       </a>
       on the film stock, which are later chemically "
       <a href="/wiki/Photographic_developer" title="Photographic developer">
        developed
       </a>
       " into a
       <a href="/wiki/Positive_(photography)" title="Positive (photography)">
        visible image
       </a>
       . The images on the film stock are played back at a rapid speed and projected onto a screen, creating the illusion of motion.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cinematography finds uses in many fields of
       <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">
        science
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Business_sector" title="Business sector">
        business
       </a>
       as well as for entertainment purposes and
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_communication" title="Mass communication">
        mass communication
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The word "cinematography" was coined
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (March 2018)">
           by whom?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       on the basis of the Greek words
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BA%CE%AF%CE%BD%CE%B7%CE%BC%CE%B1" title="wikt:κίνημα">
         κίνημα
        </a>
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        kinema
       </i>
       ), meaning "movement, motion" and
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%CF%81%CE%AC%CF%86%CE%B5%CE%B9%CE%BD" title="wikt:γράφειν">
         γράφειν
        </a>
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        graphein
       </i>
       ) meaning "to record", together meaning "recording motion". The word used to refer to the art, process, or job of filming movies, but later its meaning became restricted to "motion picture photography".
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Precursors">
        Precursors
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Precursors">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Muybridge_race_horse_animated.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="300" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Muybridge_race_horse_animated.gif/220px-Muybridge_race_horse_animated.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/dd/Muybridge_race_horse_animated.gif 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Muybridge_race_horse_animated.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Eadweard_Muybridge" title="Eadweard Muybridge">
          Muybridge
         </a>
         sequence of a horse galloping
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In the 1830s, moving images were produced on revolving drums and disks, with independent invention by Simon von Stampfer (
       <a href="/wiki/Stroboscope" title="Stroboscope">
        stroboscope
       </a>
       ) in Austria, Joseph Plateau (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenakistoscope" title="Phenakistoscope">
        phenakistoscope
       </a>
       ) in Belgium, and William Horner (
       <a href="/wiki/Zoetrope" title="Zoetrope">
        zoetrope
       </a>
       ) in Britain.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1845,
       <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ronalds" title="Francis Ronalds">
        Francis Ronalds
       </a>
       invented the
       <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ronalds#Continuously_recording_camera" title="Francis Ronalds">
        first successful camera
       </a>
       able to make
       <a href="/wiki/Photography#Science_and_forensics" title="Photography">
        continuous recordings
       </a>
       of the varying indications of
       <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">
        meteorological
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">
        geomagnetic
       </a>
       instruments over time. The cameras were supplied to numerous observatories around the world and some remained in use until well into the 20th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       William Lincoln patented a device, in 1867, that showed animated pictures called the "wheel of life" or "
       <a href="/wiki/Zoopraxiscope" title="Zoopraxiscope">
        zoopraxiscope
       </a>
       ". In it, moving drawings or photographs were watched through a slit.
      </p>
      <p>
       On 19 June 1873,
       <a href="/wiki/Eadweard_Muybridge" title="Eadweard Muybridge">
        Eadweard Muybridge
       </a>
       successfully photographed a horse named "
       <a href="/wiki/Sallie_Gardner_at_a_Gallop" title="Sallie Gardner at a Gallop">
        Sallie Gardner
       </a>
       " in fast motion using a series of 24 stereoscopic cameras. The cameras were arranged along a track parallel to the horse's, and each camera shutter was controlled by a trip wire triggered by the horse's hooves. They were 21 inches apart to cover the 20 feet taken by the horse stride, taking pictures at one thousandth of a second.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BC_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BC-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the end of the decade, Muybridge had adapted sequences of his photographs to a zoopraxiscope for short, primitive projected "movies," which were sensations on his lecture tours by 1879 or 1880.
      </p>
      <p>
       Nine years later, in 1882, French scientist
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne-Jules_Marey" title="Étienne-Jules Marey">
        Étienne-Jules Marey
       </a>
       invented a chronophotographic gun, which was capable of taking 12 consecutive frames a second, recording all the frames of the same picture.
      </p>
      <p>
       The late nineteenth to the early twentieth century brought rise to the use of film not only for entertainment purposes but for scientific exploration as well. French biologist and filmmaker Jean Painleve lobbied heavily for the use of film in the scientific field, as the new medium was more efficient in capturing and documenting the behavior, movement, and environment of microorganisms, cells, and bacteria, than the naked eye.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The introduction of film into scientific fields allowed for not only the viewing "new images and objects, such as cells and natural objects, but also the viewing of them in real time",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       whereas prior to the invention of moving pictures, scientists and doctors alike had to rely on hand drawn sketches of human anatomy and its microorganisms. This posed a great inconvenience in the science and medical worlds. The development of film and increased usage of cameras allowed doctors and scientists to grasp a better understanding and knowledge of their projects.
      </p>

      <p>
       The experimental film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Roundhay_Garden_Scene" title="Roundhay Garden Scene">
         Roundhay Garden Scene
        </a>
       </i>
       , filmed by
       <a href="/wiki/Louis_Le_Prince" title="Louis Le Prince">
        Louis Le Prince
       </a>
       on 14 October 1888, in
       <a href="/wiki/Roundhay" title="Roundhay">
        Roundhay
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">
        Leeds
       </a>
       , England, is the earliest surviving motion picture. This movie was shot on paper film.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Kennedy_Laurie_Dickson" title="William Kennedy Laurie Dickson">
        W. K. L. Dickson
       </a>
       , working under the direction of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Alva_Edison" title="Thomas Alva Edison">
        Thomas Alva Edison
       </a>
       , was the first to design a successful apparatus, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinetograph" title="Kinetograph">
        Kinetograph
       </a>
       , patented in 1891. This camera took a series of instantaneous photographs on standard Eastman Kodak photographic emulsion coated onto a transparent
       <a href="/wiki/Celluloid" title="Celluloid">
        celluloid strip
       </a>
       35 mm wide. The results of this work were first shown in public in 1893, using the viewing apparatus also designed by Dickson, the
       <a href="/wiki/Kinetoscope" title="Kinetoscope">
        Kinetoscope
       </a>
       . Contained within a large box, only one person at a time looking into it through a peephole could view the movie.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the following year,
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Francis_Jenkins" title="Charles Francis Jenkins">
        Charles Francis Jenkins
       </a>
       and his projector, the
       <a href="/wiki/Phantoscope" title="Phantoscope">
        Phantoscope
       </a>
       , made a successful audience viewing while
       <a href="/wiki/Auguste_and_Louis_Lumi%C3%A8re" title="Auguste and Louis Lumière">
        Louis and Auguste Lumière
       </a>
       perfected the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cin%C3%A9matographe" title="Cinématographe">
        Cinématographe
       </a>
       , an apparatus that took, printed, and projected film, in Paris in December 1895. The Lumière brothers were the first to present projected, moving, photographic, pictures to a paying audience of more than one person.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1896, movie theaters were open in France (
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">
        Lyon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">
        Bordeaux
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nice" title="Nice">
        Nice
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">
        Marseille
       </a>
       ); Italy (
       <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">
        Rome
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">
        Milan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">
        Naples
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">
        Genoa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">
        Venice
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">
        Bologna
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Forl%C3%AC" title="Forlì">
        Forlì
       </a>
       );
       <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">
        Brussels
       </a>
       ; and
       <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
        London
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1896, Edison showed his improved Vitascope projector, the first commercially successful projector in the U.S.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cooper Hewitt invented mercury lamps which made it practical to shoot films indoors without sunlight in 1905.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first animated cartoon was produced in 1906.
      </p>
      <p>
       Credits began to appear at the beginning of motion pictures in 1911.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Bell and Howell 2709 movie camera invented in 1915 allowed directors to make close-ups without physically moving the camera.
      </p>
      <p>
       By the late 1920s, most of the movies produced were sound films.
      </p>
      <p>
       Wide screen formats were first experimented with in the 1950s.
      </p>
      <p>
       By the 1970s, most movies were color films. IMAX and other 70mm formats gained popularity. Wide distribution of films became commonplace, setting the ground for "blockbusters."
      </p>
      <p>
       Film cinematography dominated the motion picture industry from its inception until the 2010s when digital cinematography became dominant. Film cinematography is still used by some directors, especially in specific applications or out of fondness of the format.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Black_and_white">
        Black and white
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Black and white">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       From its birth in the 1880s, movies were predominantly monochrome. Contrary to popular belief, monochrome doesn't always mean black and white; it means a movie shot in a single tone or color. Since the cost of tinted film bases was substantially higher, most movies were produced in black and white monochrome. Even with the advent of early color experiments, the greater expense of color meant films were mostly made in black and white until the 1950s, when cheaper color processes were introduced, and in some years the percentage of films shot on color film surpassed 51%. By the 1960s, color became by far the dominant film stock. In the coming decades, the usage of color film greatly increased while monochrome films became scarce.
      </p>

      <p>
       After the advent of motion pictures, a tremendous amount of energy was invested in the production of photography in natural color.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The invention of the talking picture further increased the demand for the use of color photography. However, in comparison to other technological advances of the time, the arrival of color photography was a relatively slow process.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Early movies were not actually color movies since they were shot monochrome and hand-colored or machine-colored afterwards. (Such movies are referred to as
       <i>
        colored
       </i>
       and not
       <i>
        color
       </i>
       .) The earliest such example is the hand-tinted
       <a href="/wiki/Annabelle_Serpentine_Dance" title="Annabelle Serpentine Dance">
        Annabelle Serpentine Dance
       </a>
       in 1895 by
       <a href="/wiki/Edison_Manufacturing_Company" title="Edison Manufacturing Company">
        Edison Manufacturing Company
       </a>
       . Machine-based tinting later became popular. Tinting continued until the advent of natural color cinematography in the 1910s. Many black and white movies have been colorized recently using digital tinting. This includes footage shot from both world wars, sporting events and political propaganda.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1902,
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Raymond_Turner" title="Edward Raymond Turner">
        Edward Raymond Turner
       </a>
       produced the first films with a natural color process rather than using colorization techniques.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1908,
       <a href="/wiki/Kinemacolor" title="Kinemacolor">
        kinemacolor
       </a>
       was introduced. In the same year, the short film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/A_Visit_to_the_Seaside" title="A Visit to the Seaside">
         A Visit to the Seaside
        </a>
       </i>
       became the first natural color movie to be publicly presented.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1917, the earliest version of
       <a href="/wiki/Technicolor" title="Technicolor">
        Technicolor
       </a>
       was introduced.
       <a href="/wiki/Kodachrome" title="Kodachrome">
        Kodachrome
       </a>
       was introduced in 1935.
       <a href="/wiki/Eastmancolor" title="Eastmancolor">
        Eastmancolor
       </a>
       was introduced in 1950 and became the color standard for the rest of the century.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 2010s, color films were largely superseded by color digital cinematography.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_cinematography">

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">

      <p>
       In digital cinematography, the movie is shot on digital medium such as flash storage, as well as distributed through a digital medium such as a hard drive.
      </p>
      <p>
       Beginning in the late 1980s,
       <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">
        Sony
       </a>
       began marketing the concept of "electronic cinematography," utilizing its analog
       <a href="/wiki/Sony_HDVS" title="Sony HDVS">
        Sony HDVS
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Professional_video_camera" title="Professional video camera">
        professional video cameras
       </a>
       . The effort met with very little success. However, this led to one of the earliest digitally shot feature movies,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Julia_and_Julia" title="Julia and Julia">

        </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">

        </a>
       </sup>

       <a href="/wiki/HDCAM" title="HDCAM">
        HDCAM
       </a>
       recorders and 1920 × 1080 pixel digital professional video cameras based on
       <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">
        CCD
       </a>
       technology, the idea, now re-branded as "digital cinematography," began to gain traction in the market.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Last_Broadcast_(film)" title="The Last Broadcast (film)">
         The Last Broadcast
        </a>
       </i>
       is believed by some to be the first feature-length video shot and edited entirely on consumer-level digital equipment.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In May 1999,
       <a href="/wiki/George_Lucas" title="George Lucas">
        George Lucas
       </a>
       challenged the supremacy of the movie-making medium of film for the first time by including footage filmed with high-definition digital cameras in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_Episode_I_%E2%80%93_The_Phantom_Menace" title="Star Wars: Episode I – The Phantom Menace">
         Star Wars: Episode I – The Phantom Menace
        </a>
       </i>
       . In late 2013, Paramount became the first major studio to distribute movies to theaters in digital format, eliminating 35mm film entirely. Since then the demand of movies to be developed onto digital format rather than 35mm has increased drastically.
      </p>
      <p>
       As digital technology improved, movie studios began increasingly shifting towards digital cinematography. Since the 2010s, digital cinematography has become the dominant form of cinematography after largely superseding film cinematography.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Aspects">
        Aspects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aspects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Cinematic_techniques" title="Cinematic techniques">
        Cinematic techniques
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Numerous aspects contribute to the art of cinematography, including:
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cinema_technique">
        Cinema technique
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cinema technique">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Melstud2.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="594" height="114" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Melstud2.gif/170px-Melstud2.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Melstud2.gif/255px-Melstud2.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Melstud2.gif/340px-Melstud2.gif 2x" width="170"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Melstud2.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Georges_M%C3%A9li%C3%A8s" title="Georges Méliès">
          Georges Méliès
         </a>
         (left) painting a backdrop in his studio
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The first film cameras were fastened directly to the head of a tripod or other support, with only the crudest kind of leveling devices provided, in the manner of the still-camera tripod heads of the period. The earliest film cameras were thus effectively fixed during the shot, and hence the first camera movements were the result of mounting a camera on a moving vehicle. The first known of these was a film shot by a Lumière cameraman from the back platform of a train leaving Jerusalem in 1896, and by 1898, there were a number of films shot from moving trains. Although listed under the general heading of "panoramas" in the sales catalogues of the time, those films shot straight forward from in front of a railway engine were usually specifically referred to as "
       <a href="/wiki/Phantom_ride" title="Phantom ride">
        phantom rides
       </a>
       ".
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1897,
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_W._Paul" title="Robert W. Paul">
        Robert W. Paul
       </a>
       had the first real rotating camera head made to put on a tripod, so that he could follow the passing processions of Queen Victoria's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamond_Jubilee" title="Diamond Jubilee">
        Diamond Jubilee
       </a>
       in one uninterrupted shot. This device had the camera mounted on a vertical axis that could be rotated by a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worm_gear" title="Worm gear">
        worm gear
       </a>
       driven by turning a crank handle, and Paul put it on general sale the next year. Shots taken using such a
       <a href="/wiki/Panning_(camera)" title="Panning (camera)">
        "panning"
       </a>
       head were also referred to as "panoramas" in the film catalogues of the first decade of the cinema. This eventually led to the creation of a panoramic photo as well.
      </p>
      <p>
       The standard pattern for early film studios was provided by the studio which Georges Méliès had built in 1897. This had a glass roof and three glass walls constructed after the model of large studios for still photography, and it was fitted with thin cotton cloths that could be stretched below the roof to diffuse the direct ray of the sun on sunny days. The soft overall light without real shadows that this arrangement produced, and which also exists naturally on lightly overcast days, was to become the basis for film lighting in film studios for the next decade.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Image_sensor_and_film_stock">
        Image sensor and film stock
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Image sensor and film stock">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Cinematography can begin with digital
       <a href="/wiki/Image_sensor" title="Image sensor">
        image sensor
       </a>
       or rolls of film. Advancements in film emulsion and grain structure provided a wide range of available
       <a href="/wiki/Film_stock" title="Film stock">
        film stocks
       </a>
       . The selection of a film stock is one of the first decisions made in preparing a typical film production.
      </p>
      <p>
       Aside from the
       <a href="/wiki/Film_gauge" title="Film gauge">
        film gauge
       </a>
       selection –
       <a href="/wiki/8_mm_film" title="8 mm film">
        8 mm
       </a>
       (amateur),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/16_mm" title="16 mm">
        16 mm
       </a>
       (semi-professional),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/35_mm" title="35 mm">
        35 mm
       </a>
       (professional) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/65_mm" title="65 mm">
        65 mm
       </a>
       (epic photography, rarely used except in special event venues) – the cinematographer has a selection of stocks in
       <a href="/wiki/Reversal_film" title="Reversal film">
        reversal
       </a>
       (which, when developed, create a positive image) and negative formats along with a wide range of
       <a href="/wiki/Film_speed" title="Film speed">
        film speeds
       </a>
       (varying sensitivity to light) from
       <a href="/wiki/Film_speed" title="Film speed">
        ISO
       </a>
       50 (slow, least sensitive to light) to 800 (very fast, extremely sensitive to light) and differing response to color (low
       <a href="/wiki/Colorfulness" title="Colorfulness">
        saturation
       </a>
       , high saturation) and contrast (varying levels between pure black (no exposure) and pure white (complete overexposure). Advancements and adjustments to nearly all gauges of film create the "super" formats wherein the area of the film used to capture a single frame of an image is expanded, although the physical gauge of the film remains the same.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_8_mm_film" title="Super 8 mm film">
        Super 8 mm
       </a>
       , Super 16 mm, and
       <a href="/wiki/Super_35" title="Super 35">
        Super 35
       </a>
       mm all utilize more of the overall film area for the image than their "regular" non-super counterparts. The larger the film gauge, the higher the overall image resolution clarity and technical quality. The techniques used by the
       <a href="/wiki/Film_laboratory" title="Film laboratory">
        film laboratory
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Photographic_processing" title="Photographic processing">
        process the film stock
       </a>
       can also offer a considerable variance in the image produced. By controlling the temperature and varying the duration in which the film is soaked in the development chemicals, and by skipping certain chemical processes (or partially skipping all of them), cinematographers can achieve very different looks from a single film stock in the laboratory. Some techniques that can be used are
       <a href="/wiki/Push_processing" title="Push processing">
        push processing
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bleach_bypass" title="Bleach bypass">
        bleach bypass
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Cross_processing" title="Cross processing">
        cross processing
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Most of modern cinema uses
       <a href="/wiki/Digital_cinematography" title="Digital cinematography">
        digital cinematography
       </a>
       and has no film stocks
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       , but the cameras themselves can be adjusted in ways that go far beyond the abilities of one particular film stock. They can provide varying degrees of color sensitivity, image contrast, light sensitivity and so on. One camera can achieve all the various looks of different emulsions. Digital image adjustments such as ISO and contrast are executed by estimating the same adjustments that would take place if actual film were in use, and are thus vulnerable to the camera's sensor designers perceptions of various film stocks and image adjustment parameters.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Filters">
        Filters
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Filters">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_(photography)" title="Filter (photography)">
        Filters
       </a>
       , such as diffusion filters or color effect filters, are also widely used to enhance mood or dramatic effects. Most photographic filters are made up of two pieces of optical glass glued together with some form of image or light manipulation material between the glass. In the case of color filters, there is often a translucent color medium pressed between two planes of optical glass. Color filters work by blocking out certain color
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavelengths" title="Wavelengths">
        wavelengths
       </a>
       of light from reaching the film. With color film, this works very intuitively wherein a blue filter will cut down on the passage of red, orange, and yellow light and create a blue tint on the film. In black-and-white photography, color filters are used somewhat counter intuitively; for instance a yellow filter, which cuts down on blue wavelengths of light, can be used to darken a daylight sky (by eliminating blue light from hitting the film, thus greatly underexposing the mostly blue sky) while not biasing most human flesh tone. Certain cinematographers, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Doyle" title="Christopher Doyle">
        Christopher Doyle
       </a>
       , are well known for their innovative use of filters. Filters can be used in front of the lens or, in some cases, behind the lens for different effects.
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Doyle" title="Christopher Doyle">
        Christopher Doyle
       </a>
       was a pioneer for increased usage of filters in movies. He was highly respected throughout the cinema world.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lens">
        Lens
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lens">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Camera_lens" title="Camera lens">
        Lenses
       </a>
       can be attached to the camera to give a certain look, feel, or effect by focus, color, etc.
      </p>
      <p>
       As does the
       <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">
        human eye
       </a>
       , the camera creates
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perspective_(visual)" title="Perspective (visual)">
        perspective
       </a>
       and spatial relations with the rest of the world. However, unlike one's eye, a cinematographer can select different lenses for different purposes. Variation in
       <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">
        focal length
       </a>
       is one of the chief benefits. The focal length of the lens determines the
       <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_view" title="Angle of view">
        angle of view
       </a>
       and, therefore, the
       <a href="/wiki/Field_of_view" title="Field of view">
        field of view
       </a>
       . Cinematographers can choose from a range of
       <a href="/wiki/Wide-angle_lens" title="Wide-angle lens">
        wide-angle lenses
       </a>
       , "normal" lenses and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_focus_lens" title="Long focus lens">
        long focus lenses
       </a>
       , as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Macro_photography" title="Macro photography">
        macro lenses
       </a>
       and other special effect lens systems such as
       <a href="/wiki/Borescope" title="Borescope">
        borescope
       </a>
       lenses. Wide-angle lenses have short focal lengths and make spatial distances more obvious. A person in the distance is shown as much smaller while someone in the front will loom large. On the other hand, long focus lenses reduce such exaggerations, depicting far-off objects as seemingly close together and flattening perspective. The differences between the perspective rendering is actually not due to the focal length by itself, but by the distance between the subjects and the camera. Therefore, the use of different focal lengths in combination with different camera to subject distances creates these different rendering. Changing the focal length only while keeping the same camera position doesn't affect perspective but the
       <a href="/wiki/Camera_angle" title="Camera angle">
        camera angle
       </a>
       of view only.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Zoom_lens" title="Zoom lens">
        zoom lens
       </a>
       allows a camera operator to change his focal length within a shot or quickly between setups for shots. As
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_lens" title="Prime lens">
        prime lenses
       </a>
       offer greater optical quality and are "faster" (larger aperture openings, usable in less light) than zoom lenses, they are often employed in professional cinematography over zoom lenses. Certain scenes or even types of filmmaking, however, may require the use of zooms for speed or ease of use, as well as shots involving a zoom move.
      </p>
      <p>
       As in other photography, the control of the exposed image is done in the lens with the control of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diaphragm_aperture" title="Diaphragm aperture">
        diaphragm aperture
       </a>
       . For proper selection, the cinematographer needs that all lenses be engraved with
       <a href="/wiki/F-number#T-stop" title="F-number">
        T-Stop
       </a>
       , not
       <a href="/wiki/F-number" title="F-number">
        f-stop
       </a>
       so that the eventual light loss due to the glass doesn't affect the exposure control when setting it using the usual meters. The choice of the aperture also affects image quality (aberrations) and depth of field.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Depth_of_field_and_focus">
        Depth of field and focus
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Depth of field and focus">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Citizen_Kane_deep_focus.jpg">
         <img alt="A stern looking man and a woman sit on the right side of a table with documents on the table. A top hat is on the table. An unkempt man stands to the left of the picture. In the background a boy can be seen through a window playing in the snow." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="273" data-file-width="365" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/68/Citizen_Kane_deep_focus.jpg/220px-Citizen_Kane_deep_focus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/68/Citizen_Kane_deep_focus.jpg/330px-Citizen_Kane_deep_focus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/6/68/Citizen_Kane_deep_focus.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Citizen_Kane_deep_focus.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A
         <a href="/wiki/Deep_focus" title="Deep focus">
          deep focus
         </a>
         shot from
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Citizen_Kane" title="Citizen Kane">
           Citizen Kane
          </a>
         </i>
         (1941): everything, including the hat in the foreground and the boy (young
         <a href="/wiki/Charles_Foster_Kane" title="Charles Foster Kane">
          Charles Foster Kane
         </a>
         ) in the distance, is in sharp focus.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Focal length and diaphragm aperture affect the
       <a href="/wiki/Depth_of_field" title="Depth of field">
        depth of field
       </a>
       of a scene – that is, how much the background, mid-ground and foreground will be rendered in "acceptable focus" (only one exact plane of the image is in precise focus) on the film or video target. Depth of field (not to be confused with
       <a href="/wiki/Depth_of_focus" title="Depth of focus">
        depth of focus
       </a>
       ) is determined by the aperture size and the focal distance. A large or deep depth of field is generated with a very small iris aperture and focusing on a point in the distance, whereas a shallow depth of field will be achieved with a large (open) iris aperture and focusing closer to the lens. Depth of field is also governed by the format size. If one considers the field of view and angle of view, the smaller the image is, the shorter the focal length should be, as to keep the same field of view. Then, the smaller the image is, the more depth of field is obtained, for the same field of view. Therefore, 70mm has less depth of field than 35mm for a given field of view, 16mm more than 35mm, and video cameras even more depth of field than 16mm. As videographers try to emulate the look of 35 mm film with digital cameras, this is one issue of frustration – excessive depth of field with digital cameras and using additional optical devices to reduce that depth of field.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Citizen_Kane" title="Citizen Kane">
         Citizen Kane
        </a>
       </i>
       (1941), cinematographer
       <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Toland" title="Gregg Toland">
        Gregg Toland
       </a>
       and director
       <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">
        Orson Welles
       </a>
       used tighter apertures to create every detail of the foreground and background of the sets in sharp focus. This practice is known as
       <a href="/wiki/Deep_focus" title="Deep focus">
        deep focus
       </a>
       . Deep focus became a popular cinematographic device from the 1940s onwards in Hollywood. Today, the trend is for more
       <a href="/wiki/Shallow_focus" title="Shallow focus">
        shallow focus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       To change the plane of focus from one object or character to another within a shot is commonly known as a
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rack_focus" title="Rack focus">
         rack focus
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Aspect_ratio_and_framing">
        Aspect ratio and framing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Aspect ratio and framing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio_(image)" title="Aspect ratio (image)">
        aspect ratio
       </a>
       of an image is the ratio of its width to its height. This can be expressed either as a ratio of 2 integers, such as 4:3, or in a decimal format, such as 1.33:1 or simply 1.33.
      </p>
      <p>
       Different ratios provide different aesthetic effects. Standards for aspect ratio have varied significantly over time.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the silent era, aspect ratios varied widely, from square
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_film_formats#Film_formats" title="List of film formats">
        1:1
       </a>
       , all the way up to the extreme widescreen 4:1
       <a href="/wiki/Polyvision" title="Polyvision">
        Polyvision
       </a>
       . However, from the 1910s, silent motion pictures generally settled on the ratio of 4:3 (1.33). The introduction of sound-on-film briefly narrowed the aspect ratio, to allow room for a sound stripe. In 1932, a new standard was introduced, the
       <a href="/wiki/Academy_ratio" title="Academy ratio">
        Academy ratio
       </a>
       of 1.37, by means of thickening the
       <a href="/wiki/Frame_line" title="Frame line">
        frame line
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For years, mainstream cinematographers were limited to using the Academy ratio, but in the 1950s, thanks to the popularity of
       <a href="/wiki/Cinerama" title="Cinerama">
        Cinerama
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Widescreen" title="Widescreen">
        widescreen
       </a>
       ratios were introduced in an effort to pull audiences back into the theater and away from their home
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_television" title="History of television">
        television
       </a>
       sets. These new widescreen formats provided cinematographers a wider frame within which to compose their images.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many different proprietary photographic systems were invented and utilized in the 1950s to create widescreen movies, but one dominated film: the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anamorphic" title="Anamorphic">
        anamorphic
       </a>
       process, which optically squeezes the image to photograph twice the horizontal area to the same size vertical as standard "spherical" lenses. The first commonly used anamorphic format was
       <a href="/wiki/CinemaScope" title="CinemaScope">
        CinemaScope
       </a>
       , which used a 2.35 aspect ratio, although it was originally 2.55. CinemaScope was used from 1953 to 1967, but due to technical flaws in the design and its ownership by Fox, several third-party companies, led by
       <a href="/wiki/Panavision" title="Panavision">
        Panavision
       </a>
       's technical improvements in the 1950s, dominated the anamorphic cine lens market. Changes to
       <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Motion_Picture_and_Television_Engineers" title="Society of Motion Picture and Television Engineers">
        SMPTE
       </a>
       projection standards altered the projected ratio from 2.35 to 2.39 in 1970, although this did not change anything regarding the photographic anamorphic standards; all changes in respect to the aspect ratio of anamorphic 35 mm photography are specific to camera or projector gate sizes, not the optical system. After the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_film_formats" title="List of film formats">
        "widescreen wars"
       </a>
       of the 1950s, the motion-picture industry settled into 1.85 as a standard for theatrical projection in the United States and the United Kingdom. This is a cropped version of 1.37. Europe and Asia opted for 1.66 at first, although 1.85 has largely permeated these markets in recent decades. Certain "epic" or adventure movies utilized the anamorphic 2.39.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 1990s, with the advent of
       <a href="/wiki/High-definition_video" title="High-definition video">
        high-definition video
       </a>
       , television engineers created the 1.78 (16:9) ratio as a mathematical compromise between the theatrical standard of 1.85 and television's 1.33, as it was not practical to produce a traditional CRT television tube with a width of 1.85. Until that point, nothing had ever been originated in 1.78. Today, this is a standard for high-definition video and for widescreen television.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lighting">
        Lighting
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Lighting">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Light is necessary to create an image exposure on a frame of film or on a digital target (CCD, etc.). The art of lighting for cinematography goes far beyond basic exposure, however, into the essence of visual storytelling. Lighting contributes considerably to the emotional response an audience has watching a motion picture. The increased usage of filters can greatly impact the final image and affect the lighting.
      </p>

      <p>
       Cinematography can not only depict a moving subject but can use a camera, which represents the audience's viewpoint or perspective, that moves during the course of filming. This movement plays a considerable role in the emotional language of film images and the audience's emotional reaction to the action. Techniques range from the most basic movements of
       <a href="/wiki/Panning_(camera)" title="Panning (camera)">
        panning
       </a>
       (horizontal shift in viewpoint from a fixed position; like turning your head side-to-side) and tilting (vertical shift in viewpoint from a fixed position; like tipping your head back to look at the sky or down to look at the ground) to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dolly_shot" title="Dolly shot">
        dollying
       </a>
       (placing the camera on a moving platform to move it closer or farther from the subject),
       <a href="/wiki/Tracking_shot" title="Tracking shot">
        tracking
       </a>
       (placing the camera on a moving platform to move it to the left or right),
       <a href="/wiki/Crane_shot" title="Crane shot">
        craning
       </a>
       (moving the camera in a vertical position; being able to lift it off the ground as well as swing it side-to-side from a fixed base position), and combinations of the above. Early cinematographers often faced problems that were not common to other graphic artists because of the element of motion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Filming_outdoor.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Filming_outdoor.jpg/220px-Filming_outdoor.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Filming_outdoor.jpg/330px-Filming_outdoor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Filming_outdoor.jpg/440px-Filming_outdoor.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Filming_outdoor.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Live filming for TV (2013)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Cameras have been mounted to nearly every imaginable form of transportation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Most cameras can also be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handheld_camera" title="Handheld camera">
        handheld
       </a>
       , that is held in the hands of the camera operator who moves from one position to another while filming the action. Personal stabilizing platforms came into being in the late 1970s through the invention of
       <a href="/wiki/Garrett_Brown" title="Garrett Brown">
        Garrett Brown
       </a>
       , which became known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Steadicam" title="Steadicam">
        Steadicam
       </a>
       . The Steadicam is a body harness and stabilization arm that connects to the camera, supporting the camera while isolating it from the operator's body movements. After the Steadicam patent expired in the early 1990s, many other companies began manufacturing their concept of the personal camera stabilizer. This invention is much more common throughout the cinematic world today. From feature-length films to the evening news, more and more networks have begun to use a personal camera stabilizer.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Special_effects">
        Special effects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Special effects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Special_effect" title="Special effect">
        Special effect
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The first special effects in the cinema were created while the film was being shot. These came to be known as "
       <a href="/wiki/In-camera_effect" title="In-camera effect">
        in-camera
       </a>
       " effects. Later,
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_printer" title="Optical printer">
        optical
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Digital_compositing" title="Digital compositing">
        digital effects
       </a>
       were developed so that editors and visual effects artists could more tightly control the process by manipulating the film in
       <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">
        post-production
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The 1896 movie
       <a href="/wiki/The_Execution_of_Mary_Stuart" title="The Execution of Mary Stuart">
        The Execution of Mary Stuart
       </a>
       shows an actor dressed as the queen placing her head on the execution block in front of a small group of bystanders in Elizabethan dress. The executioner brings his axe down, and the queen's severed head drops onto the ground. This trick was worked by stopping the camera and replacing the actor with a dummy, then restarting the camera before the axe falls. The two pieces of film were then trimmed and cemented together so that the action appeared continuous when the film was shown, thus creating an overall illusion and successfully laying the foundation for special effects.
      </p>
      <p>
       This film was among those exported to Europe with the first Kinetoscope machines in 1895 and was seen by Georges Méliès, who was putting on magic shows in his Theatre Robert-Houdin in Paris at the time. He took up filmmaking in 1896, and after making imitations of other films from Edison, Lumière, and Robert Paul, he made
       <i>
        Escamotage d'un dame chez Robert-Houdin (
        <a href="/wiki/The_Vanishing_Lady" title="The Vanishing Lady">
         The Vanishing Lady
        </a>
        )
       </i>
       . This film shows a woman being made to vanish by using the same
       <a href="/wiki/Stop_motion" title="Stop motion">
        stop motion
       </a>
       technique as the earlier Edison film. After this, Georges Méliès made many single shot films using this trick over the next couple of years.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Double_exposure">
        Double exposure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Double exposure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Santa_Claus,_1898.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="231" data-file-width="300" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5b/Santa_Claus%2C_1898.gif/220px-Santa_Claus%2C_1898.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5b/Santa_Claus%2C_1898.gif 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Santa_Claus,_1898.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A scene inset inside a circular vignette showing a "dream vision" in
         <i>
          Santa Claus
         </i>
         (1898).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The other basic technique for trick cinematography involves
       <a href="/wiki/Multiple_exposure" title="Multiple exposure">
        double exposure
       </a>
       of the film in the camera, which was first done by
       <a href="/wiki/George_Albert_Smith_(film_pioneer)" title="George Albert Smith (film pioneer)">
        George Albert Smith
       </a>
       in July 1898 in the UK. Smith's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Corsican_Brothers" title="The Corsican Brothers">
         The Corsican Brothers
        </a>
       </i>
       (1898) was described in the catalogue of the
       <a href="/wiki/Warwick_Trading_Company" title="Warwick Trading Company">
        Warwick Trading Company
       </a>
       , which took up the distribution of Smith's films in 1900, thus:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        "One of the twin brothers returns home from shooting in the Corsican mountains, and is visited by the ghost of the other twin. By extremely careful photography the ghost appears *quite transparent*. After indicating that he has been killed by a sword-thrust, and appealing for vengeance, he disappears. A 'vision' then appears showing the fatal duel in the snow. To the Corsican's amazement, the duel and death of his brother are vividly depicted in the vision, and overcome by his feelings, he falls to the floor just as his mother enters the room."
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The ghost effect was done by draping the set in black velvet after the main action had been shot, and then re-exposing the negative with the actor playing the ghost going through the actions at the appropriate point. Likewise, the vision, which appeared within a circular vignette or
       <a href="/wiki/Matte_(filmmaking)" title="Matte (filmmaking)">
        matte
       </a>
       , was similarly superimposed over a black area in the backdrop to the scene, rather than over a part of the set with detail in it, so that nothing appeared through the image, which seemed quite solid. Smith used this technique again in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Santa_Claus_(1898_film)" title="Santa Claus (1898 film)">
         Santa Claus
        </a>
       </i>
       (1898).
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Georges_M%C3%A9li%C3%A8s" title="Georges Méliès">
        Georges Méliès
       </a>
       first used superimposition on a dark background in
       <i>
        La Caverne maudite (The Cave of the Demons)
       </i>
       made a couple of months later in 1898, and elaborated it with multiple superimpositions in the one shot in
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Un_Homme_de_t%C3%AAtes" title="Un Homme de têtes">
         Un Homme de têtes
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/The_Four_Troublesome_Heads" title="The Four Troublesome Heads">
         The Four Troublesome Heads
        </a>
        )
       </i>
       . He created further variations in subsequent films.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Frame_rate_selection">
        Frame rate selection
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Frame rate selection">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Frame_rate" title="Frame rate">
        Frame rate
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Motion picture images are presented to an audience at a constant speed. In the theater it is 24
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frames_per_second" title="Frames per second">
        frames per second
       </a>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/NTSC" title="NTSC">
        NTSC
       </a>
       (US) Television it is 30 frames per second (29.97 to be exact), in
       <a href="/wiki/PAL" title="PAL">
        PAL
       </a>
       (Europe) television it is 25 frames per second. This speed of presentation does not vary.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, by varying the speed at which the image is captured, various effects can be created knowing that the faster or slower recorded image will be played at a constant speed. Giving the cinematographer even more freedom for creativity and expression to be made.
      </p>
      <p>
       For instance,
       <a href="/wiki/Time-lapse_photography" title="Time-lapse photography">
        time-lapse photography
       </a>
       is created by exposing an image at an extremely slow rate. If a cinematographer sets a camera to expose one frame every minute for four hours, and then that footage is projected at 24 frames per second, a four-hour event will take 10 seconds to present, and one can present the events of a whole day (24 hours) in just one minute.
      </p>
      <p>
       The inverse of this, if an image is captured at speeds above that at which they will be presented, the effect is to greatly slow down (
       <a href="/wiki/Slow_motion" title="Slow motion">
        slow motion
       </a>
       ) the image. If a cinematographer shoots a person diving into a pool at 96 frames per second, and that image is played back at 24 frames per second, the presentation will take 4 times as long as the actual event. Extreme slow motion, capturing many thousands of frames per second can present things normally invisible to the
       <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">
        human eye
       </a>
       , such as bullets in flight and shockwaves travelling through media, a potentially powerful cinematographical technique.
      </p>
      <p>
       In motion pictures, the manipulation of time and space is a considerable contributing factor to the narrative storytelling tools. Film editing plays a much stronger role in this manipulation, but frame rate selection in the photography of the original action is also a contributing factor to altering time. For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">
        Charlie Chaplin
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Modern_Times_(film)#Production" title="Modern Times (film)">
         Modern Times
        </a>
       </i>
       was shot at "silent speed" (18 fps) but projected at "sound speed" (24 fps), which makes the slapstick action appear even more frenetic.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speed_ramping" title="Speed ramping">
        Speed ramping
       </a>
       , or simply "ramping", is a process whereby the capture frame rate of the camera changes over time. For example, if in the course of 10 seconds of capture, the capture frame rate is adjusted from 60 frames per second to 24 frames per second, when played back at the standard movie rate of 24 frames per second, a unique time-manipulation effect is achieved. For example, someone pushing a door open and walking out into the street would appear to start off in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slow-motion" title="Slow-motion">
        slow-motion
       </a>
       , but in a few seconds later within the same shot, the person would appear to walk in "realtime" (normal speed). The opposite speed-ramping is done in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Matrix" title="The Matrix">
         The Matrix
        </a>
       </i>
       when Neo re-enters the Matrix for the first time to see the Oracle. As he comes out of the warehouse "load-point", the camera zooms into Neo at normal speed but as it gets closer to Neo's face, time seems to slow down,
       <a href="/wiki/Foreshadowing" title="Foreshadowing">
        foreshadowing
       </a>
       the manipulation of time itself within the Matrix later in the movie.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_special_techniques">
        Other special techniques
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Other special techniques">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       G.A. Smith initiated the technique of
       <a href="/wiki/Reverse_motion" title="Reverse motion">
        reverse motion
       </a>
       and also improved the quality of self-motivating images. This he did by repeating the action a second time while filming it with an inverted camera and then joining the tail of the second negative to that of the first. The first films using this were
       <i>
        Tipsy, Topsy, Turvy
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        The Awkward Sign Painter
       </i>
       , the latter which showed a sign painter lettering a sign, and then the painting on the sign vanishing under the painter's brush. The earliest surviving example of this technique is Smith's
       <i>
        The House That Jack Built
       </i>
       , made before September 1901. Here, a small boy is shown knocking down a castle just constructed by a little girl out of children's building blocks. A title then appears, saying "Reversed", and the action is repeated in reverse so that the castle re-erects itself under his blows.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cecil Hepworth improved upon this technique by printing the negative of the forwards motion backwards frame by frame, so that in the production of the print the original action was exactly reversed. Hepworth made
       <i>
        The Bathers
       </i>
       in 1900, in which bathers who have undressed and jumped into the water appear to spring backwards out of it, and have their clothes magically fly back onto their bodies.
      </p>
      <p>
       The use of different camera speeds also appeared around 1900. Robert Paul's
       <i>
        On a Runaway Motor Car through Piccadilly Circus
       </i>
       (1899), had the camera turn so slowly that when the film was projected at the usual 16 frames per second, the scenery appeared to be passing at great speed. Cecil Hepworth used the opposite effect in
       <i>
        The Indian Chief and the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seidlitz_powder" title="Seidlitz powder">
         Seidlitz powder
        </a>
       </i>
       (1901), in which a naïve
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">
        Red Indian
       </a>
       eats a lot of the fizzy stomach medicine, causing his stomach to expand and then he then leaps around balloon-like. This was done by cranking the camera faster than the normal 16 frames per second giving the first "
       <a href="/wiki/Slow_motion" title="Slow motion">
        slow motion
       </a>
       " effect.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Personnel">
        Personnel
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinematography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Personnel">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FMPU_camera_crew_1944.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="430" height="266" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/FMPU_camera_crew_1944.jpg/220px-FMPU_camera_crew_1944.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/FMPU_camera_crew_1944.jpg/330px-FMPU_camera_crew_1944.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8d/FMPU_camera_crew_1944.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FMPU_camera_crew_1944.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A camera crew from the
         <a href="/wiki/First_Motion_Picture_Unit" title="First Motion Picture Unit">
          First Motion Picture Unit
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In descending order of seniority, the following staff is involved:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Director of photography
        </b>
        also called
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">
          cinematographer
         </a>
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Camera operator
        </b>
        also called
        <b>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cameraman" title="Cameraman">
          cameraman
         </a>
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         First assistant camera
        </b>
        also called
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Focus_puller" title="Focus puller">
          focus puller
         </a>
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Second assistant camera
        </b>
        also called
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Clapper_loader" title="Clapper loader">
          clapper loader
         </a>
        </b>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In the film industry, the
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">
         cinematographer
        </a>
       </b>
       is responsible for the technical aspects of the images (lighting, lens choices, composition, exposure, filtration, film selection), but works closely with the director to ensure that the artistic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aesthetic" title="Aesthetic">
        aesthetics
       </a>
       are supporting the director's vision of the story being told. The cinematographers are the heads of the camera,
       <a href="/wiki/Grip_(job)" title="Grip (job)">
        grip
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lighting_technician" title="Lighting technician">
        lighting crew
       </a>
       on a set, and for this reason, they are often called
       <b>
        directors of photography
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        DPs
       </b>
       . The ASC defines cinematography as a creative and interpretive process that culminates in the authorship of an original work of art rather than the simple recording of a physical event. Cinematography is not a subcategory of photography. Rather, photography is but one craft that the cinematographer uses in addition to other physical, organizational, managerial, interpretive. and image-manipulating techniques to effect one coherent process.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In British tradition, if the DOP actually operates the camera him/herself they are called the
       <i>
        cinematographer
       </i>
       . On smaller productions, it is common for one person to perform all these functions alone. The career progression usually involves climbing up the ladder from seconding, firsting, eventually to operating the camera.
      </p>
      <p>
       Directors of photography make many creative and interpretive decisions during the course of their work, from pre-production to post-production, all of which affect the overall feel and look of the motion picture. Many of these decisions are similar to what a photographer needs to note when taking a picture: the cinematographer controls the film choice itself (from a range of available stocks with varying sensitivities to light and color), the selection of lens focal lengths, aperture
       <a href="/wiki/Exposure_(photography)" title="Exposure (photography)">
        exposure
       </a>
       and focus. Cinematography, however, has a temporal aspect (see
       <a href="/wiki/Persistence_of_vision" title="Persistence of vision">
        persistence of vision
       </a>
       ), unlike still photography, which is purely a single still image. It is also bulkier and more strenuous to deal with movie cameras, and it involves a more complex array of choices. As such a cinematographer often needs to work co-operatively with more people than does a photographer, who could frequently function as a single person. As a result, the cinematographer's job also includes personnel management and logistical organization. Given the in-depth knowledge. a cinematographer requires not only of his or her own craft but also that of other personnel, formal tuition in analogue or digital filmmaking can be advantageous.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

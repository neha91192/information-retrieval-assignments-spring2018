      <p>
       A
       <b>
        unit production manager
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        UPM
       </b>
       ) is the
       <a href="/wiki/Directors_Guild_of_America" title="Directors Guild of America">
        DGA
       </a>
       –approved title for the top
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Below_the_line_(filmmaking)" title="Below the line (filmmaking)">
        below-the-line
       </a>
       staff position, responsible for the administration of a
       <a href="/wiki/Feature_film" title="Feature film">
        feature film
       </a>
       or television production. Non-DGA productions might call it the
       <b>
        production manager
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        production supervisor
       </b>
       . They work closely with the
       <a href="/wiki/Line_producer" title="Line producer">
        line producer
       </a>
       . Sometimes the line producer is the UPM. A senior producer may assign a UPM more than one production at a time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Responsibility_to_a_senior_producer">
        Responsibility to a senior producer
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unit_production_manager&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Responsibility to a senior producer">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A UPM is usually hired by a
       <a href="/wiki/Film_producer" title="Film producer">
        film producer
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Television_producer" title="Television producer">
        television producer
       </a>
       , and is responsible for managing the production and regulating the costs of delivering the expected film or television show on budget at the end of principal photography. Based on a shooting script, the UPM will create a working budget during
       <a href="/wiki/Pre-production" title="Pre-production">
        pre-production
       </a>
       , prior to the start of
       <a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">
        principal photography
       </a>
       , thereby charting a recommended budgetary course. Typically, a producer will oversee all the cost-related decisions, including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_the_line_(film_production)" title="Above the line (film production)">
        above-the-line
       </a>
       expenditures (especially during
       <a href="/wiki/Pre-production" title="Pre-production">
        pre-production
       </a>
       ). However, the UPM is responsible for the more detailed planning and execution of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Below_the_line_(filmmaking)" title="Below the line (filmmaking)">
        below-the-line
       </a>
       costs (primarily for physical production). The film's
       <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">
        director
       </a>
       will select important department heads (keys) or support personnel related to his work, including the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casting_director" title="Casting director">
        casting director
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">
        cinematographer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Costume_designer" title="Costume designer">
        costume designer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Production_designer" title="Production designer">
        production designer
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_editor" title="Film editor">
        film editor
       </a>
       . For below-the-line matters, the UPM often negotiates deals (for location, equipment, etc.) and hires the remaining crew, typically on the recommendation of the keys and/or based on prior experience with trusted individuals.
      </p>
      <p>
       The UPM frequently does not have any direct responsibilities with the creative direction of the film's narrative, although in some cases he will and can be consulted. Many budget decisions affect the creative direction of the project. He or she is to support the director's artistic vision on a daily physical production level.
      </p>
      <p>
       Before principal photography begins both the producer and UPM work to secure as many of the production elements as possible, including a workable schedule and budget. Usually from a production office during pre-production, they may later divide their time between the office and the set while the film is being shot. An on-set trailer may also be used as a makeshift production office. Like nearly all members of a film crew, UPMs will typically remain on the shoot until the end of principal photography and can return if there are any reshoots. It is not atypical for a UPM to work 80-100 (or more) hours per week while a film is in production.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">
        US
       </a>
       , a union UPM is represented by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Directors_Guild_of_America" title="The Directors Guild of America">
        The Directors Guild of America
       </a>
       . Certain conditions must be adhered to on a union shoot in order to remain in good standing with the DGA. These include expected salary rates, working hours, overtime penalties, required meals and other related criteria.
      </p>
      <p>
       Highly experienced UPMs bring great value to a project for their strategic and creative problem-solving skills. These individuals will positively affect the film's budget as well as the final outcome and quality of the film on multiple levels.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Duties">
        Duties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unit_production_manager&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Duties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The UPM, under the supervision of the employer, is required to coordinate, facilitate and oversee the preparation of the production unit or units (to the extent herein provided) assigned to him or her, all off-set logistics, day-to-day production decisions, locations, budget schedules and personnel. Without limitation, among the duties which the Employer must assign to the UPM or First Assistant are the supervision of or participation in the following:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Prepare breakdown and preliminary shooting schedule.
       </li>
       <li>
        Prepare or coordinate the budget.
       </li>
       <li>
        Oversee preliminary search and survey of all locations and the completion of business arrangements for the same.
       </li>
       <li>
        Assist in the preparation of the production to ensure continuing efficiency.
       </li>
       <li>
        Supervise completion of the Production Report for each day's work, showing work covered and the status of the production, and arrange for the distribution of that report in line with the company's requirement.
       </li>
       <li>
        Coordinate arrangements for the transportation and housing of cast, crew and staff.
       </li>
       <li>
        Oversee the securing of releases and negotiate for locations and personnel.
       </li>
       <li>
        Maintain a liaison with local authorities regarding locations and the operation of the company.
       </li>
      </ul>

    Sync sound
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       <b>
        Sync sound
       </b>
       (synchronized sound recording) refers to sound recorded at the time of the
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        filming
       </a>
       of movies. It has been widely used in movies since the birth of
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_film" title="Sound film">
        sound movies
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sync_sound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Even in the silent film era, films were shown with sounds, often with musical accompaniment by a pianist or an orchestra keeping time with the screen action. The first synchronization was a turning recording device marked with a white spot. As the white spot rotated, the cameraman hand cranked the camera to keep it in sync with the recording. The method was then repeated for playback, but with the projectionist hand cranking the film projector. "
       <a href="/wiki/Single-system_recording" title="Single-system recording">
        Single-system
       </a>
       " sound recorded sound optically to part of the original camera film, or magnetically to a stripe of magnetic coating along the film edge.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/Double-system_recording" title="Double-system recording">
        Double-system
       </a>
       " sound used independent cameras and sound recorders. The first sync sound standard used recorders and cameras both powered by AC (alternating current) motors - essentially clock motors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Later the 50 Hz or 60 Hz sine wave, called a
       <a href="/wiki/Pilottone" title="Pilottone">
        Pilottone
       </a>
       , was recorded on a second parallel track of an audio recorder.
      </p>
      <p>
       In double-system film, speed variations of camera and recorder, as well as the elasticity of the magnetic recording tape, requires some positive means of keying the dialogue to its appropriate film frame. The inclusion on the sound recorder of a second, parallel, sync or "Pilotone" track, has been the most common method in use until today. In video recording, synchronism is electronically generated and generally called dual-system sound
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       If location, a camera is driven by a
       <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">
        DC
       </a>
       motor, with some sort of governor control to hold it fairly accurate at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/24_fps" title="24 fps">
        24 fps
       </a>
       , a sync pulse generator geared to the movement or motor shaft could be employed to provide the sync pulse output. A cable conducts the sync pulse from camera to sound recorder. The sync pulse is typically a sine wave of 50 or 60 Hz with an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RMS_amplitude" title="RMS amplitude">
        RMS amplitude
       </a>
       of approximately 1 volt.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This double-system audio recording could then be transferred or "resolved" to sprocketed magnetic film, with sprocket holes that match one to one with the original camera film. These two sprocketed media could be run through a "
       <a href="/wiki/Moviola" title="Moviola">
        Moviola
       </a>
       " or flat-bed editing table such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Steenbeck" title="Steenbeck">
        Steenbeck
       </a>
       for synchronous sound editing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       With the introduction of the
       <a href="/wiki/Bulova" title="Bulova">
        Bulova
       </a>
       "Accutron" watch that used a tuning fork as a time reference (watches later used an oscillating electronic crystal), the camera no longer needed to be connected to the sound recorder with a cable. The camera speed was controlled by one
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oscillator" title="Oscillator">
        oscillator
       </a>
       , and a second oscillator in the recorder generated the Pilotone.
      </p>
      <p>
       This method was developed in the 1960s by pioneering filmmaker
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Leacock" title="Richard Leacock">
        Richard Leacock
       </a>
       . It was called
       <a href="/wiki/Direct_Cinema" title="Direct Cinema">
        Direct Cinema
       </a>
       . Filmmakers abandoned the studio and went out on location to film, often with hand-held cameras.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1972,
       <a href="/wiki/Bell_%26_Howell" title="Bell &amp; Howell">
        Bell &amp; Howell
       </a>
       brought out a consumer version of a double-system
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super-8" title="Super-8">
        Super-8
       </a>
       sound filmmaking system called "Filmosound". A
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_cassette" title="Compact cassette">
        compact cassette
       </a>
       recorder was attached to the camera with a cable that transmitted a single pulse to the recorder every time a new frame of film was exposed in the camera. On playback, the cassette recorder pulse was used to control the projector speed.
      </p>
      <p>
       At that time,
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Leacock" title="Richard Leacock">
        Ricky Leacock
       </a>
       , a professor in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">
        MIT
       </a>
       architecture department film section, developed a Super-8 film production system with a crystal-controlled camera, a crystal-generated Pilotone cassette recorder, a sprocketed magnetic film recorder, a flatbed editing table, and a projector. The MIT/Leacock System was funded with a $300,000 grant from the founder of
       <a href="/wiki/Polaroid_Corporation" title="Polaroid Corporation">
        Polaroid
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edwin_Land" title="Edwin Land">
        Edwin Land
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1973, the one-pulse-per-frame technique was used to control recording directly onto sprocketed magnetic film in the Super8 Sound Recorder.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Super8 Sound Recorder could also "resolve" sound that had been recorded onto cassette tape with this new "digital" sync pulse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Today,
       <a href="/wiki/Digital_video" title="Digital video">
        digital video
       </a>
       cameras and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_sound" title="Digital sound">
        digital sound
       </a>
       recorders synchronize electronically, being used for double-system video production.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pioneering_films">
        Pioneering films
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>

        </a>
        by
        <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Rogosin" title="Lionel Rogosin">
         Lionel Rogosin
        </a>

       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronicle_of_a_Summer" title="Chronicle of a Summer">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sync_sound_in_Asia">
        Sync sound in Asia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sync_sound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sync sound in Asia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">
        Hong Kong
       </a>
       , sync sound was not widely used until the 1990s, as the generally noisy environment and lower production budgets made such a method impractical.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Most
       <a href="/wiki/Bollywood" title="Bollywood">
        Bollywood
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        films
       </a>
       from the 1960s onwards do not employ this technique and for that very reason the 2001 films
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lagaan" title="Lagaan">
         Lagaan
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dil_Chahta_Hai" title="Dil Chahta Hai">
         Dil Chahta Hai
        </a>
       </i>
       were noted for the use of sync sound. The common practice in Bollywood is to
       <a href="/wiki/Dubbing_(filmmaking)" title="Dubbing (filmmaking)">
        'dub'
       </a>
       over the dialogues at the post-production stage. Currently, Indian sound designers and sound mixers like
       <a href="/wiki/Resul_Pookutty" title="Resul Pookutty">
        Resul Pookutty
       </a>
       , Nakul Kamte, Dileep Subrahmaniam, Debashish Mishra, Indrajit Neogi, Arun Nambiar, Baylon Fonseca, Manas Choudhary and many others among the fresh talent are endeavoring to change this.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first Indian talkie,
       <i>
        Alam Ara
       </i>
       (1931), made the first use of sync sound in India and subsequent Indian films were regularly shot in sync sound with the silent Mitchell camera, until the 1960s. With the arrival of the Arri 2C, a noisy but more practical camera particularly for outdoor shoots, 'dubbing' became the norm and was never reversed.
      </p>
      <p>
       Use of this technique has increased in recent times with the development in film techniques and instrumentation like
       <a href="/wiki/Arri" title="Arri">
        Arri
       </a>
       Blimp
       <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">
        cameras
       </a>
       . This system has been used in the Hindi movie
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Jodhaa_Akbar" title="Jodhaa Akbar">
         Jodhaa Akbar
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

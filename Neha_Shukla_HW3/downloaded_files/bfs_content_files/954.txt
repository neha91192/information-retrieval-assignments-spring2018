   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Script (comics)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        script
       </b>
       is a document describing the narrative and dialogue of a
       <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">
        comic book
       </a>
       in detail. It is the comic book equivalent of a
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television program
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Teleplay" title="Teleplay">
        teleplay
       </a>
       or a
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        film
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">
        screenplay
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In comics, a script may be preceded by a plot outline, and is almost always followed by page sketches, drawn by a
       <a href="/wiki/Comics_artist" title="Comics artist">
        comics artist
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Inker" title="Inker">
        inked
       </a>
       , succeeded by the
       <a href="/wiki/Colorist" title="Colorist">
        coloring
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Letterer" title="Letterer">
        lettering
       </a>
       stages. There are no prescribed forms of comic scripts, but there are two dominant styles in the mainstream comics industry, the
       <i>
        full script
       </i>
       (commonly known as "
       <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">
        DC
       </a>
       style") and the
       <i>
        plot script
       </i>
       (or "
       <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">
        Marvel
       </a>
       style").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jones_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-jones-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Full_script">
        Full script
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Script_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Full script">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In this style, the
       <b>
        comics writer
       </b>
       (also
       <b>
        comics scripter
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        comic book writer
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        comics author
       </b>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <b>
        comic book author
       </b>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <b>
        graphic novel writer
       </b>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <b>
        graphic novel author
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <b>
        graphic novelist
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) breaks the story down in sequence, page-by-page and panel-by-panel, describing the action, characters, and sometimes backgrounds and "camera" points-of-view of each panel, as well as all captions and dialogue balloons. For decades, this was the preferred format for books published by
       <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">
        DC Comics
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_David" title="Peter David">
        Peter David
       </a>
       described his specific application of the full script method: "I break down each page on a panel by panel basis and label them as PANEL A, PANEL B, and so on. Then I describe what's in each panel, and then do the dialogue, numbering the balloons. I designate the panels with letters and the word balloons with numbers so as to minimize confusion for the letterer."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_script">
        Plot script
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Script_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot script">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In a plot script the artist works from a story synopsis from the writer (or plotter), rather than a full script. The artist creates page-by-page plot details on his or her own, after which the work is returned to the writer for the insertion of dialogue. Due to its widespread use at
       <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">
        Marvel Comics
       </a>
       beginning in the 1960s, primarily under writer-editor
       <a href="/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">
        Stan Lee
       </a>
       and artists
       <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">
        Jack Kirby
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Steve_Ditko" title="Steve Ditko">
        Steve Ditko
       </a>
       , this approach became commonly known as the "
       <b>
        Marvel method
       </b>
       " or "
       <b>
        Marvel house style
       </b>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCJ75_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TCJ75-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Comics historian
       <a href="/wiki/Mark_Evanier" title="Mark Evanier">
        Mark Evanier
       </a>
       writes that this "new means of collaboration . . . was born of necessity—Stan was overburdened with work—and to make use of Jack's great skill with storylines. . . . Sometimes Stan would type up a written plot outline for the artist. Sometimes, not."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As comic-book writer-editor
       <a href="/wiki/Dennis_O%27Neil" title="Dennis O'Neil">
        Dennis O'Neil
       </a>
       describes, the Marvel method ". . . requires the writer to begin by writing out a plot and add[ing] words when the penciled artwork is finished. . . .[I]n the mid-sixties, plots were seldom more than a typewritten page, and sometimes less," while writers in later times "might produce as many as twenty-five pages of plot for a twenty-two page story, and even include in them snatches of dialog. So a Marvel Method plot can run from a couple of paragraphs to something much longer and more elaborate."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Marvel method was in place with at least one artist by early 1961, as Lee described in 2009 when speaking of his and Ditko's "short, five-page filler strips ... placed in any of our comics that had a few extra pages to fill", most prominently in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Amazing_Fantasy" title="Amazing Fantasy">
         Amazing Fantasy
        </a>
       </i>
       but even previously in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Amazing_Adventures" title="Amazing Adventures">
         Amazing Adventures
        </a>
       </i>
       and other "
       <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Comics_(1950s)#Pre-superhero_Marvel" title="Atlas Comics (1950s)">
        pre-superhero Marvel
       </a>
       "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science-fiction" title="Science-fiction">
        science-fiction
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">
        fantasy
       </a>
       anthology titles. "I'd dream up odd fantasy tales with an
       <a href="/wiki/O._Henry" title="O. Henry">
        O. Henry
       </a>
       type twist ending. All I had to do was give Steve a one-line description of the plot and he'd be off and running. He'd take those skeleton outlines I had given him and turn them into classic little works of art that ended up being far cooler than I had any right to expect."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Advantages of the Marvel method over the full script method that have been cited by creators and industry professionals include (1) the artist is more visually-minded and thus often has a better notion of how a scene should play out, (2) it gives the artist more freedom,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FieldShad_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FieldShad-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and (3) it lightens the burden on the writer.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FieldShad_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FieldShad-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Cited disadvantages include (1) not all talented artists are talented writers, and some struggle over aspects such as plot ideas and pacing,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FieldShad_13-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FieldShad-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Back17_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Back17-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (2) it takes advantage of artists, who are typically paid for art alone even though they are essentially working as co-writers,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Back17_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Back17-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and (3) the artist's development of a story may clash with the writer's style.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Kurtzman_style">
        Kurtzman style
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Script_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Kurtzman style">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In a variation of the plot script, attributed to
       <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Kurtzman" title="Harvey Kurtzman">
        Harvey Kurtzman
       </a>
       , the writer breaks down the story into page roughs or thumbnail sketches, with captions and dialogue jotted down inside the roughs. The artist (who is often the comic's writer as well) then fleshes out the roughs onto full-size art board. Writer/artists
       <a href="/wiki/Frank_Miller_(comics)" title="Frank Miller (comics)">
        Frank Miller
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Smith_(cartoonist)" title="Jeff Smith (cartoonist)">
        Jeff Smith
       </a>
       favor this style, as did
       <a href="/wiki/Archie_Goodwin_(comics)" title="Archie Goodwin (comics)">
        Archie Goodwin
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jones_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-jones-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="EC_style">
        EC style
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Script_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: EC style">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Attributed to
       <a href="/wiki/William_Gaines" title="William Gaines">
        William Gaines
       </a>
       (Kurtzman’s publisher at
       <a href="/wiki/EC_Comics" title="EC Comics">
        EC Comics
       </a>
       ), the EC style is similar to the Kurtzman Style, except the writer submits a tight plot to an artist, who breaks it down into panels that are laid out on the art board. The writer writes all captions and dialogue, which are pasted inside these panels, and then the artist draws the story to fit all of this paste-up. This laborious and restrictive way of creating comics is no longer in general use; the last artist to use even a variation of EC style was the late
       <a href="/wiki/Jim_Aparo" title="Jim Aparo">
        Jim Aparo
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jones_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-jones-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

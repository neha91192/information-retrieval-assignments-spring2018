   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Pitch (filmmaking)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       For other uses, see
       <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Pitch_(disambiguation)" title="Pitch (disambiguation)">
        Pitch
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        pitch
       </b>
       is a concise verbal (and sometimes visual) presentation of an idea for a
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        film
       </a>
       or TV series generally made by a
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">
        screenwriter
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">
        film director
       </a>
       to a
       <a href="/wiki/Film_producer" title="Film producer">
        film producer
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Studio_executive" title="Studio executive">
        studio executive
       </a>
       in the hope of attracting
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Development_(film)" title="Development (film)">
        development
       </a>
       finance to pay for the writing of a
       <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">
        screenplay
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p58_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-p58-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       "Pitch" is a contraction of the phrase "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sales_pitch" title="Sales pitch">
        sales pitch
       </a>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A pitch is used throughout different stages of production, such as casting and distribution, as well as to urge film producers to further fund a project.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p58_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-p58-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Filmmakers who devise a pitch tend to manufacture a production package, which is handed out to each potential investor during the pitch. The package contains the basic information for the filmmaker's project, such as a plot synopsis and budgeting values.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sometimes, filmmakers will produce an independent
       <a href="/wiki/Pitch_trailer" title="Pitch trailer">
        pitch trailer
       </a>
       as a part of the package to help potential financiers better visualize the project and the filmmaker's vision.
      </p>
      <p>
       Though pitches are usually made on the basis of a full
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Script_(recorded_media)" title="Script (recorded media)">
        script
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Teleplay" title="Teleplay">
        teleplay
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Animated_cartoon" title="Animated cartoon">
        animated
       </a>
       productions for both film and television are often pitched on the basis of
       <a href="/wiki/Storyboard" title="Storyboard">
        storyboards
       </a>
       alone. For example, the animated television show
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Phineas_and_Ferb" title="Phineas and Ferb">
         Phineas and Ferb
        </a>
       </i>
       was pitched from a storyboard. Co-founders of the project,
       <a href="/wiki/Dan_Povenmire" title="Dan Povenmire">
        Dan Povenmire
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jeff_%22Swampy%22_Marsh" title='Jeff "Swampy" Marsh'>
        Jeff "Swampy" Marsh
       </a>
       , needed to convince overseas executives for
       <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">
        The Walt Disney Company
       </a>
       to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenlight" title="Greenlight">
        greenlight
       </a>
       the series, so they drew a storyboard and recorded it as a reel. They then mixed it and dubbed it over with sound effects, voices, and narrative, then sent the recording to the executives, who accepted it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OP_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-OP-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Television pitches can also be devised by the network or company that produces the program.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raise_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-raise-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Certain networks are pitched the idea of including a character in a series in order to boost ratings. Such pitches have been used with "Oliver" in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Brady_Bunch" title="The Brady Bunch">
         The Brady Bunch
        </a>
       </i>
       and "Luke" on
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Growing_Pains" title="Growing Pains">
         Growing Pains
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alberti,_p._144_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Alberti,_p._144-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Networks also try to force their ideas on series' producers through their pitches, though their approach is business-oriented and their ideas are generally not favored by writers and viewers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1992, the crew of the animated series
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Rugrats" title="Rugrats">
         Rugrats
        </a>
       </i>
       was approached by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickelodeon_(TV_channel)" title="Nickelodeon (TV channel)">
        Nickelodeon
       </a>
       , which pitched the idea of a
       <i>
        Rugrats
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hanukkah" title="Hanukkah">
        Hanukkah
       </a>
       special.
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Germain" title="Paul Germain">
        Paul Germain
       </a>
       , co-creator of the series, responded by suggesting a
       <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">
        passover
       </a>
       special, which he dubbed a "funny idea."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raise_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-raise-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After they closed production for
       <a href="/wiki/A_Rugrats_Passover" title="A Rugrats Passover">
        that special
       </a>
       , they began considering the Hanukkah special and eventually created it in 1996 as the episode "
       <a href="/wiki/A_Rugrats_Chanukah" title="A Rugrats Chanukah">
        A Rugrats Chanukah
       </a>
       ."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raise_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-raise-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

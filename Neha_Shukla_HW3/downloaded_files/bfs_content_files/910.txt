   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Film distribution
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Film distribution
       </b>
       is the process of making a movie available for viewing by an
       <a href="/wiki/Audience" title="Audience">
        audience
       </a>
       . This is normally the task of a professional
       <a href="/wiki/Film_distributor" title="Film distributor">
        film distributor
       </a>
       , who would determine the marketing strategy for the film, the media by which a film is to be exhibited or made available for viewing, and who may set the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_release" title="Film release">
        release date
       </a>
       and other matters. The film may be exhibited directly to the public either through a
       <a href="/wiki/Movie_theater" title="Movie theater">
        movie theater
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television
       </a>
       , or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_entertainment" title="Home entertainment">
        personal home viewing
       </a>
       (including
       <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">
        VHS
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Video_on_demand" title="Video on demand">
        video-on-demand
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Download" title="Download">
        download
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">
        television programs
       </a>
       through
       <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_syndication" title="Broadcast syndication">
        broadcast syndication
       </a>
       ). For commercial projects, film distribution is usually accompanied by
       <a href="/wiki/Film_promotion" title="Film promotion">
        film promotion
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       When a film is initially produced, a feature film is often shown to audiences in a movie theater. Typically, one film is the featured presentation (or
       <a href="/wiki/Feature_film" title="Feature film">
        feature film
       </a>
       ). Before the 1970s, there were "double features"; typically, a high-quality "
       <i>
        A
       </i>
       picture" rented by an independent theater for a lump sum, and a lower-quality "
       <i>
        B
       </i>
       picture" rented for a percentage of the gross receipts. Today, the bulk of the material shown before the feature film consists of previews for upcoming movies (also known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trailer_(movie)" title="Trailer (movie)">
        trailers
       </a>
       ) and paid advertisements.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The identity of the first theater designed specifically for cinema is a matter of debate; candidates include Tally's Electric Theatre, established 1902 in Los Angeles,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Pittsburgh's Nickelodeon, established 1905.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Thousands of such theaters were built or converted from existing facilities within a few years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       , these theaters came to be known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickelodeon_movie_theater" title="Nickelodeon movie theater">
        nickelodeons
       </a>
       , because admission typically cost a nickel (five cents).
      </p>
      <p>
       Historically, all mass-marketed feature films were made to be shown in movie theaters. The development of
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television
       </a>
       has allowed films to be broadcast to larger audiences, usually after the film is no longer being shown in theaters.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       In 1971
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U-Matic" title="U-Matic">
        U-Matic
       </a>
       became the first magnetic format in which movies could be enjoyed in institutions outside the theatre. Later that year, the first
       <a href="/wiki/Cartrivision" title="Cartrivision">
        cartrivision
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Videocassettes" title="Videocassettes">
        videocassettes
       </a>
       of movies became available to consumers to watch in their own homes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Recording technology has since enabled consumers to rent or buy copies of films on
       <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">
        VHS
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">
        DVD
       </a>
       (and the older formats of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laserdisc" title="Laserdisc">
        laserdisc
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Video_CD" title="Video CD">
        VCD
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/SelectaVision" title="SelectaVision">
        SelectaVision
       </a>
       – see also
       <a href="/wiki/Videodisc" title="Videodisc">
        videodisc
       </a>
       ), and
       <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">
        Internet
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Download" title="Download">
        downloads
       </a>
       may be available and are revenue sources for film production companies. Some films may be made specifically for non-theatrical formats, being released as a "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_movie" title="Television movie">
        television movie
       </a>
       " or "
       <a href="/wiki/Direct-to-video" title="Direct-to-video">
        direct-to-video
       </a>
       " movie. The production values on these films are often considered to be of inferior quality compared to theatrical releases in similar genres; some films that are rejected by their own
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movie_studio" title="Movie studio">
        movie studios
       </a>
       upon completion may be distributed through these markets.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The movie theater pays an average of about 50-55% of its ticket sales to the movie studio, as film rental fees.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-PBS-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The actual percentage starts with a number higher than that, and decreases as the duration of a film's showing continues, as an incentive to theaters to keep movies in the theater longer. However, today's barrage of highly marketed movies ensures that most movies are shown in first-run theaters for less than 8 weeks. There are a few movies every year that defy this rule, often limited-release movies that start in only a few theaters and actually grow their theater count through good word-of-mouth and reviews.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       According to a 2000 study by
       <a href="/wiki/ABN_AMRO" title="ABN AMRO">
        ABN AMRO
       </a>
       , about 26% of Hollywood movie studios' worldwide income came from box office ticket sales; 46% came from VHS and DVD sales to consumers; and 28% came from television (broadcast, cable, and pay-per-view).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-PBS-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Film_release">
        Film release
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Film release">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-studio_era">
        Pre-studio era
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pre-studio era">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Prior to the decline of the
       <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Patents_Company" title="Motion Picture Patents Company">
        Motion Picture Patents Company
       </a>
       (Edison Trust) in 1915, there were two main forms of film distribution:
       <i>
        State's Rights
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Road Show
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cobbles_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cobbles-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Under the
       <b>
        state's rights
       </b>
       system, films were sold on a local, territorial basis. The local salesperson would then play the film as often as they desired in an attempt to make as much profit as possible. Film copyright holders would sell rights of a movie directly to the theater or franchise salesperson,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epics_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Epics-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       typically on a
       <a href="/wiki/Film_stock" title="Film stock">
        foot-by-foot
       </a>
       basis for 10 cents a foot.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cobbles_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cobbles-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Absent major studios or national theater franchises, this system was generally the best way to ensure national release of a film, particularly for shorter films. However, in terms of profitability, the state's rights system wasn't the most effective way to screen feature-length films since the film's producers only made money on the initial sale of each film copy.
      </p>
      <p>
       This method also made it possible to screen films of various genre which may be illegal in one state but legal in another.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epics_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Epics-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       With the
       <b>
        road show
       </b>
       system, the producer would enter into an agreement with each theater, with priority given to large-seating and famous theaters. Money would be made via ticket sales. A movie's showing would be limited to drive up demand and to help create a sense of prestige.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cobbles_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cobbles-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although this method helped increase film earnings for the producer, given its nature, a movie's release would only be at the regional level. Some of the first road show films were the Italian film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cabiria" title="Cabiria">
         Cabiria
        </a>
       </i>
       (1914) and the American
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Birth_of_a_Nation" title="The Birth of a Nation">
         The Birth of a Nation
        </a>
       </i>
       (1915).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_release">
        Standard release
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Standard release">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The standard release routine for a movie is regulated by a
       <a href="/wiki/Business_model" title="Business model">
        business model
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wsj-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       called "release windows". The release windows system was first conceived in the early
       <a href="/wiki/1980s_in_film" title="1980s in film">
        1980s
       </a>
       , on the brink of the VHS
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_entertainment" title="Home entertainment">
        home entertainment
       </a>
       market, as a strategy to keep different instances of a movie from competing with each other,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slate_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-slate-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       allowing the movie to take advantage of different markets (cinema, home video, TV, etc.) at different times.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the standard process, a movie is first released through
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movie_theaters" title="Movie theaters">
        movie theaters
       </a>
       (theatrical window), then, after approximately 16 and one-half weeks,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-wsj-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       it is released to
       <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">
        VHS
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Video_on_demand" title="Video on demand">
        VOD
       </a>
       services (entering its video window). After an additional number of months, it is usually released to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pay_TV" title="Pay TV">
        Pay TV
       </a>
       , and approximately two years after its theatrical release date, it is made available for
       <a href="/wiki/Free-to-air" title="Free-to-air">
        free-to-air
       </a>
       TV.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Simultaneous_release">
        Simultaneous release
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Simultaneous release">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Simultaneous_release" title="Simultaneous release">
        simultaneous release
       </a>
       takes place when a movie is made available on many media (cinema, DVD, internet...) at the same time or with very little difference in timing.
      </p>
      <p>
       Simultaneous releases offer great advantages to both consumers, who can choose the
       <a href="/wiki/Media_(communication)" title="Media (communication)">
        medium
       </a>
       that most suits their needs, and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movie_studio" title="Movie studio">
        production studios
       </a>
       that only have to run one
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marketing_campaign" title="Marketing campaign">
        marketing campaign
       </a>
       for all releases. The flip side, though, is that such distribution efforts are often regarded as experimental and thus do not receive substantial investment or promotion.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the course of the years simultaneous release approaches have gained both praise, with
       <a href="/wiki/Mark_Cuban" title="Mark Cuban">
        Mark Cuban
       </a>
       claiming movies should simultaneously be made available on all media allowing viewers to choose whether to see it at home or at the theater,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slate2_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-slate2-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and disapproval, with director
       <a href="/wiki/M._Night_Shyamalan" title="M. Night Shyamalan">
        M. Night Shyamalan
       </a>
       claiming it could potentially destroy the "magic" of moviegoing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-latimes-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Cinema owners can be affected seriously in case they have to share their opportunity window, specially at the beginning of the movie lifecycle, since, according to Disney, about 95% of all box office tickets for a film are sold within the first six weeks after initial distribution.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daily_Telegraph_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Daily_Telegraph-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Among relevant simultaneous release attempts are
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Bubble_(film)" title="Bubble (film)">
         Bubble
        </a>
       </i>
       (2006) by Academy Award-winning director
       <a href="/wiki/Steven_Soderbergh" title="Steven Soderbergh">
        Steven Soderbergh
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=EMR_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="EMR (film) (page does not exist)">
         EMR
        </a>
       </i>
       (2005) by James Erskine &amp; Danny McCullough, and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Road_to_Guantanamo" title="The Road to Guantanamo">
         The Road to Guantanamo
        </a>
       </i>
       (2006).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Straight-to-video_release">
        Straight-to-video release
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Straight-to-video release">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Direct-to-video" title="Direct-to-video">
        Direct-to-video
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A straight to video (or straight-to-DVD or straight-to-Blu-ray depending on the media upon which the movie is made available) release occurs when a movie is released on
       <a href="/wiki/Home_video" title="Home video">
        home video
       </a>
       formats (such as VHS, DVD, etc.) without being released in theaters first, thereby not taking into consideration the "theatrical window".
      </p>
      <p>
       As a result of strong DVD sales, Direct-to-video releases also achieved higher success and have become a profitable market lately,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       especially for
       <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">
        independent
       </a>
       moviemakers and companies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Internet_release">
        Internet release
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Internet release">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">
        Internet
       </a>
       research is still new when it comes to the film distribution platform. The volume of downloaded movies is difficult to find but none compares to the even more problematical discovery of their origin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Shrinking_of_the_theatrical_window">
        Shrinking of the theatrical window
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_distribution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Shrinking of the theatrical window">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       While originally conceived for a six months duration, the theatrical window has today been reduced to little more than four months.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slate_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-slate-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-reuters-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Major_film_studio#Today's_Big_Six" title="Major film studio">
        Major movie studios
       </a>
       have reportedly been pushing to shrink the duration of the theatrical window in an attempt to make up for the substantial losses in the
       <a href="/wiki/DVD-Video" title="DVD-Video">
        DVD
       </a>
       market they've been suffering from since the 2004 sales peak.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_10-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-wsj-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj2_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wsj2-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       These attempts have encountered the firm opposition of
       <a href="/wiki/Movie_theater" title="Movie theater">
        theater
       </a>
       owners, whose profits depend solely upon attendance and therefore benefit from keeping the movie available on their screens.
      </p>
      <p>
       In early 2010,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney" title="Disney">
        Disney
       </a>
       announced it would be putting out the DVD and Blu-ray versions of
       <a href="/wiki/Tim_Burton" title="Tim Burton">
        Tim Burton
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(2010_film)" title="Alice in Wonderland (2010 film)">
         Alice in Wonderland
        </a>
       </i>
       14 weeks after the movie's release date (instead of the usual 17) in order to avoid competition from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010_World_Cup" title="2010 World Cup">
        2010 World Cup
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_10-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-wsj-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In response to such statements, theater owners made threats not to show the movie on their screens,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but later reconsidered their position before the movie was released.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters_19-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-reuters-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Other strategies are also being deployed in order to make up for slow DVD sales. Most major studios have considered making movies available to
       <a href="/wiki/Video_on_demand" title="Video on demand">
        VOD
       </a>
       services shortly after their theatrical release for a premium price.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj2_20-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-wsj2-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In July 2010
       <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">
        Netflix
       </a>
       secured a deal with
       <a href="/wiki/Relativity_Media" title="Relativity Media">
        Relativity Media
       </a>
       in which the latter agreed to distribute a number of major movies to the aforementioned VOD service before
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pay_TV" title="Pay TV">
        Pay TV
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Producers of relatively
       <a href="/wiki/Low-budget_film" title="Low-budget film">
        smaller-budget
       </a>
       movies are also utilizing new release strategies. In 2009, the movie
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_House_of_the_Devil" title="The House of the Devil">
         The House of the Devil
        </a>
       </i>
       premiered on VOD systems on October 1, and received a
       <a href="/wiki/Limited_release" title="Limited release">
        limited theatrical release
       </a>
       one month later. In August 2010, it was announced that the movie
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Freakonomics#Film_adaptation" title="Freakonomics">
         Freakonomics
        </a>
       </i>
       would be released on video on demand on September 3, one month before its theatrical release. The British
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sci-fi" title="Sci-fi">
        sci-fi
       </a>
       movie
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Monsters_(2010_film)" title="Monsters (2010 film)">
         Monsters
        </a>
       </i>
       has also undergone the same release timetable.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       After
       <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">
        Netflix
       </a>
       bought the worldwide distribution rights to
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Beasts_of_No_Nation_(film)" title="Beasts of No Nation (film)">
         Beasts of No Nation
        </a>
       </i>
       , the film was simultaneously released theatrically and online through its subscription video-on-demand (SVOD) service on October 16, 2015.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       <b>
        "Below-the-line"
       </b>
       is a term derived from the top sheet of a
       <a href="/wiki/Film_budgeting" title="Film budgeting">
        film budget
       </a>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        motion pictures
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">
        television programs
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sponsored_film" title="Sponsored film">
        industrial films
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">
        independent films
       </a>
       , student films and
       <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">
        documentaries
       </a>
       as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Advertisement_film" title="Advertisement film">
        commercials
       </a>
       . The "line" in "below-the-line" refers to the separation of production costs between
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">
        script and story writers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Film_producer" title="Film producer">
        producers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">
        directors
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Actor" title="Actor">
        actors,
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Casting_(performing_arts)" title="Casting (performing arts)">
        casting
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Above-the-line_(filmmaking)" title="Above-the-line (filmmaking)">
        "above the-line"
       </a>
       ) and the rest of the crew, or
       <a href="/wiki/Production_team" title="Production team">
        production team
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The top sheet of any creative project's budget serves only as an at-a-glance reference to a fully detailed and attached main budget document, which features total expenses including federal, state and local taxes, as well as insurance within the entire production, and or
       <a href="/wiki/Movie_production_incentives_in_the_United_States" title="Movie production incentives in the United States">
        production incentives
       </a>
       . This painstaking task is usually assigned to the Production Manager or UPM of a production
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and should be completed before
       <a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">
        principal photography
       </a>
       begins for any project.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Below-the-line_crew">
        Below-the-line crew
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Below-the-line_(filmmaking)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Below-the-line crew">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some below-the-line film and television film crews operate in
       <a href="/wiki/Pre-production" title="Pre-production">
        pre-production
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Filmmaking#Production" title="Filmmaking">
        production
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">
        post-production
       </a>
       stages of filmmaking. For example, the film editor may work solely in the post-production stage of filming but he/she might also work throughout the production, editing the film as it is shot and advising the director if and when additional shots or scenes are needed. The boom operator on the other hand is mostly on set during filming because he or she has to pick up the dialogue of the actors as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Ambience_(sound_recording)" title="Ambience (sound recording)">
        ambient noise
       </a>
       when the scene is being recorded.
      </p>
      <p>
       This particular type of
       <a href="/wiki/Film_crew" title="Film crew">
        film crew
       </a>
       usually belongs to a guild or union. Some of the unions and/or guilds that represent below-the-line crew include:
       <a href="/wiki/International_Alliance_of_Theatrical_Stage_Employees" title="International Alliance of Theatrical Stage Employees">
        I.A.T.S.E
       </a>
       ., The International Cinematographer's Guild (an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IATSE" title="IATSE">
        IATSE
       </a>
       organization), The
       <a href="/wiki/International_Brotherhood_of_Teamsters" title="International Brotherhood of Teamsters">
        Teamsters
       </a>
       , Motion Picture Editors Guild, Animation Guild, and the Motion Picture Sound Editors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is usually the case for the "below-the-line" crew to work for hourly wages, as opposed to a set wage, or negotiated contractual wage on the front, or back end of any given project. These set workers are responsible for a number of many other unsung, and demanding tasks that must be completed to achieve the common goal of creating a film, or television program. These positions could be considered "less glamorous", and the unmentionable part of filmmaking. However, many dedicated and hard working individuals have made a good living with "below-the-line" job positions, whether it's in
       <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">
        independent filmmaking
       </a>
       , and or for a
       <a href="/wiki/Major_film_studio" title="Major film studio">
        major film studio
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       Within the many entry-level positions available to gain knowledge of the industry, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Lighting_technician" title="Lighting technician">
        Lighting technicians
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grip_(job)" title="Grip (job)">
        grips
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Wrangler_(profession)" title="Wrangler (profession)">
        wranglers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Stunt_performer" title="Stunt performer">
        stunt performers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Property_master" title="Property master">
        property masters
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dubbing_(filmmaking)#ADR/post-sync" title="Dubbing (filmmaking)">
        ADR
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Foley_(filmmaking)" title="Foley (filmmaking)">
        foley
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scenic_painting" title="Scenic painting">
        set painters
       </a>
       or any
       <a href="/wiki/Stagehand" title="Stagehand">
        stagehand
       </a>
       etc., one could gain knowledge and skill of these, and many other key positions within the
       <a href="/wiki/Film_industry" title="Film industry">
        film industry
       </a>
       . Many schools and or
       <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_organization" title="Nonprofit organization">
        nonprofit organizations
       </a>
       are set up to train the youth and other interested individuals to better prepare and teach a trade that may not otherwise be available within the traditional school system. These organizations teach about film and video terminology, equipment, and
       <a href="/wiki/Life_skills" title="Life skills">
        life skills
       </a>
       , a must for today's highly completive
       <a href="/wiki/Show_business" title="Show business">
        entertainment
       </a>
       market.
      </p>

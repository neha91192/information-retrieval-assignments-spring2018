      <p>
       A
       <b>
        professional
       </b>
       is a member of a
       <a href="/wiki/Profession" title="Profession">
        profession
       </a>
       or any person who earns their living from a specified professional activity. The term also describes the standards of education and training that prepare members of the profession with the particular knowledge and skills necessary to perform their specific role within that profession. In addition, most professionals are subject to strict codes of conduct, enshrining rigorous
       <a href="/wiki/Professional_ethics" title="Professional ethics">
        ethical
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moral_obligation" title="Moral obligation">
        moral obligations
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Professional standards of practice and ethics for a particular field are typically agreed upon and maintained through widely recognized
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professional_associations" title="Professional associations">
        professional associations
       </a>
       , such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Electrical_and_Electronics_Engineers" title="Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers">
        IEEE
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some definitions of "professional" limit this term to those professions that serve some important aspect of public interest
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvey_1995_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harvey_1995-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the general good of society.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sullivan_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sullivan-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gardner_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gardner-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In some cultures, the term is used as shorthand to describe a particular social stratum of well-educated workers who enjoy considerable
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Work_autonomy" title="Work autonomy">
        work autonomy
       </a>
       and who are commonly engaged in creative and intellectually challenging work.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilbert_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gilbert-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beeghley_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Beeghley-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eichar_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Eichar-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehrenreich_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ehrenreich-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Trades">
        Trades
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Professional&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Trades">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In narrow usage, not all expertise is considered a profession. Although sometimes incorrectly referred to as professions, occupations such as skilled construction and maintenance work are more generally thought of as trades or
       <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">
        crafts
       </a>
       . The completion of an apprenticeship is generally associated with skilled labour, or trades such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpenter" title="Carpenter">
        carpenter
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Electrician" title="Electrician">
        electrician
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Stonemasonry" title="Stonemasonry">
        mason
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/House_painter_and_decorator" title="House painter and decorator">
        painter
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Plumber" title="Plumber">
        plumber
       </a>
       and other similar occupations. A related distinction would be that a professional does mainly
       <a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">
        mental
       </a>
       work, as opposed to engaging in physical work.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">
        Theory
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Professional&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theory">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although professional training appears to be ideologically neutral, it may be biased towards those with higher
       <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">
        class backgrounds
       </a>
       and a formal education. In his 2000 book,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Disciplined_Minds" title="Disciplined Minds">
         Disciplined Minds
        </a>
        : A Critical Look at Salaried Professionals and the Soul-Battering System that Shapes Their Lives
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Schmidt_(writer)" title="Jeff Schmidt (writer)">
        Jeff Schmidt
       </a>
       observes that qualified professionals are less creative and diverse in their opinions and habits than non-professionals, which he attributes to the subtle indoctrination and filtering which accompanies the process of professional training. His evidence is both qualitative and quantitative, including professional examinations, industry statistics and personal accounts of trainees and professionals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A key theoretical dispute arises from the observation that established professions (e.g. lawyers, medical doctors, architects, civil engineers) are subject to strict codes of conduct. Some have thus argued that these codes of conduct, agreed upon and maintained through widely recognized professional associations, are a key element of what constitutes any profession.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Others have argued that strict
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Codes_of_conduct" title="Codes of conduct">
        codes of conduct
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professional_associations" title="Professional associations">
        professional associations
       </a>
       that maintain them are merely a consequence of 'successful' professionalization, rather than an intrinsic element of the definition of professional(ism); this implies that a profession arises from the alignment between a shared purpose (connected to a 'greater good'), a
       <a href="/wiki/Body_of_knowledge" title="Body of knowledge">
        body of knowledge
       </a>
       , actual behavior in terms of actions and decisions, and expectations held by societal stakeholders.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Professional&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The etymology and historical meaning of the term professional is from Middle English, from
       <i>
        profes
       </i>
       , adjective, having professed one's vows, from Anglo-French, from Late Latin
       <i>
        professus
       </i>
       , from Latin, past participle of
       <i>
        profitēri
       </i>
       to profess, confess, from pro- before +
       <i>
        fatēri
       </i>
       to acknowledge; in other senses, from Latin
       <i>
        professus
       </i>
       , past participle. Thus, as people became more and more specialized in their trade, they began to 'profess' their skill to others, and 'vow' to perform their trade to the highest known standard. With a reputation to uphold, trusted workers of a society who have a specific trade are considered professionals. Ironically, the usage of the word 'profess' declined from the late 1800s to the 1950s, just as the term 'professional' was gaining popularity from 1900-2010.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

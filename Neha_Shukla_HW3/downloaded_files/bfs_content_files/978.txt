      <p>
       A
       <b>
        storyboard artist
       </b>
       (sometimes called a
       <b>
        story artist
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        visualizer
       </b>
       ) creates
       <a href="/wiki/Storyboard" title="Storyboard">
        storyboards
       </a>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Advertising_agency" title="Advertising agency">
        advertising agencies
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Filmmaking" title="Filmmaking">
        film productions
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Work">
        Work
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Storyboard_artist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Work">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A storyboard artist visualizes stories and sketches frames of the story.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Quick pencil drawings and marker renderings are two of the most common traditional techniques, although nowadays
       <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Flash" title="Adobe Flash">
        Adobe Flash
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Photoshop" title="Adobe Photoshop">
        Adobe Photoshop
       </a>
       , and other storyboard applications are often used. The digital camera is one of the latest techniques in creating storyboards.
      </p>
      <p>
       They are mostly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freelance" title="Freelance">
        freelance
       </a>
       artists, typically hired by
       <a href="/wiki/Art_director" title="Art director">
        art directors
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">
        film directors
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Typically freelance storyboard artists will belong to one or more storyboard agencies much like an illustration agency.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Many storyboard artists nowadays begin and finish their work on computers using software and digital pencils or a
       <a href="/wiki/Graphics_tablet" title="Graphics tablet">
        graphics tablet
       </a>
       . Storyboard artists may use photos to create visuals where
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stock_photos" title="Stock photos">
        stock photos
       </a>
       or photos taken specifically for the project are put together digitally to produce a photographic representation called a photovisual.
      </p>
      <p>
       For motion pictures, some filmmakers, directors, and producers choose to use clip-art computer programs designed to create storyboards, or use a dedicated 3D storyboarding software, or a more multi-purpose 3D program which can also be used to create elements of the storyboards.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Industry_specific_work">
        Industry specific work
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Storyboard_artist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Industry specific work">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Storyboard artists have different goals in different industries.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Advertising">
        Advertising
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Storyboard_artist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Advertising">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In advertising, the storyboard artist can be called upon to create a representation of what the finished TV commercial, or spot, will look like in order to persuade and engage the client to buy the concept being pitched. This can either be at the time the agency is trying to win the client's business or once the client has signed on with the agency. In either case, the important element is for the storyboards to visualize for the client what the agency's creative director or "creatives" are thinking will sell the client's product. A storyboard artist may also be asked to visually represent several versions of a campaign for print ads.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Live-action_film">
        Live-action film
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Storyboard_artist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Live-action film">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In live-action film, a storyboard artist is hired at the beginning of a project. When a storyboard artist is hired by a motion picture company, the artist must break down the scenes of the script into shots which can be filmed. This is done under the supervision of the film's director in order to ensure the director's vision from the start of the project. As the production proceeds, the storyboards are presented to the cinematographer who is then responsible for bringing that vision to the screen.
      </p>
      <p>
       Film production companies may also hire a storyboard artist to create polished presentation-style storyboards (which might also include sound) which can be used by an executive producer to raise the money to create the film.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Animation">
        Animation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Storyboard_artist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Animation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In animation, projects are often pitched on the basis of storyboards alone (that is, a screenplay may not be written until later), and storyboard artists continue to work throughout the production to develop particular sequences. After a sequence is edited the director and/or storyboard artist and team may need to rework the sequence as it becomes evident that changes need to be made for timing and story.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

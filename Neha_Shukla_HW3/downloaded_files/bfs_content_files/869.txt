             </abbr>
            </a>
           </li>
          </ul>
         </div>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       <b>
        Filmmaking
       </b>
       (or, in an academic context,
       <b>
        film production
       </b>
       ) is the process of making a
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        film
       </a>
       , generally in the sense of films intended for extensive theatrical exhibition. Filmmaking involves a number of discrete stages including an initial
       <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">
        story
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Idea" title="Idea">
        idea
       </a>
       , or commission, through
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriting" title="Screenwriting">
        screenwriting
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Casting_(performing_arts)" title="Casting (performing arts)">
        casting
       </a>
       , shooting,
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_recording_and_reproduction" title="Sound recording and reproduction">
        sound recording and reproduction
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Film_editing" title="Film editing">
        editing
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Film_screening" title="Film screening">
        screening
       </a>
       the finished product before an
       <a href="/wiki/Audience" title="Audience">
        audience
       </a>
       that may result in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_release" title="Film release">
        film release
       </a>
       and exhibition. Filmmaking takes place in many places around the world in a range of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic" title="Economic">
        economic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Social" title="Social">
        social
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political" title="Political">
        political
       </a>
       contexts, and using a variety of technologies and
       <a href="/wiki/Cinematic_techniques" title="Cinematic techniques">
        cinematic techniques
       </a>
       . Typically, it involves a large number of people, and can take from a few months to several years to complete.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stages_of_production">
        Stages of production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Filmmaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Stages of production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Film production consists of five major stages:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Development
        </b>
        : The first stage in which the ideas for the film are created, rights to books/plays are bought etc., and the screenplay is written.
        <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">
         Financing
        </a>
        for the project has to be sought and obtained.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Pre-production" title="Pre-production">
          Pre-production
         </a>
        </b>
        : Arrangements and preparations are made for the shoot, such as hiring cast and
        <a href="/wiki/Film_crew" title="Film crew">
         film crew
        </a>
        , selecting locations and constructing sets.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Production
        </b>
        : The raw footage and other elements for the film are recorded during the film shoot.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">
          Post-production
         </a>
        </b>
        : The images, sound, and visual effects of the recorded film are edited and combined into a finished product.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Film_distribution" title="Film distribution">
          Distribution
         </a>
        </b>
        : The completed film is distributed, marketed, and screened in cinemas and/or released to
        <a href="/wiki/Home_video" title="Home video">
         home video
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Development">
        Development
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Filmmaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In this stage, the project
       <a href="/wiki/Film_producer" title="Film producer">
        producer
       </a>
       selects a story, which may come from a
       <a href="/wiki/Book" title="Book">
        book
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Play_(theatre)" title="Play (theatre)">
        play
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Remake" title="Remake">
        another film
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_films_based_on_actual_events" title="List of films based on actual events">
        true story
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">
        video game
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">
        comic book
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Graphic_novel" title="Graphic novel">
        graphic novel
       </a>
       , or an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Original_idea" title="Original idea">
        original idea
       </a>
       , etc. After identifying a theme or underlying message, the producer works with writers to prepare a synopsis. Next they produce a
       <a href="/wiki/Step_outline" title="Step outline">
        step outline
       </a>
       , which breaks the story down into one-paragraph scenes that concentrate on dramatic structure. Then, they prepare a
       <a href="/wiki/Film_treatment" title="Film treatment">
        treatment
       </a>
       , a 25-to-30-page description of the story, its mood, and characters. This usually has little dialogue and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stage_directions" title="Stage directions">
        stage direction
       </a>
       , but often contains drawings that help visualize key points. Another way is to produce a
       <a href="/wiki/Scriptment" title="Scriptment">
        scriptment
       </a>
       once a synopsis is produced.
      </p>
      <p>
       Next, a
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">
        screenwriter
       </a>
       writes a
       <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">
        screenplay
       </a>
       over a period of several months. The screenwriter may rewrite it several times to improve dramatization, clarity, structure, characters, dialogue, and overall style. However, producers often skip the previous steps and develop submitted screenplays which investors, studios, and other interested parties assess through a process called
       <a href="/wiki/Script_coverage" title="Script coverage">
        script coverage
       </a>
       . A
       <a href="/wiki/Film_distributor" title="Film distributor">
        film distributor
       </a>
       may be contacted at an early stage to assess the likely market and potential financial success of the film. Hollywood distributors adopt a hard-headed no approach and consider factors such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Film_genre" title="Film genre">
        film genre
       </a>
       , the target audience and assumed audience, the historical success of similar films, the actors who might appear in the film, and potential directors. All these factors imply a certain appeal of the film to a possible audience. Not all films make a profit from the theatrical release alone, so film companies take DVD sales and worldwide distribution rights into account.
      </p>
      <p>
       The producer and screenwriter prepare a film
       <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(filmmaking)" title="Pitch (filmmaking)">
        pitch
       </a>
       , or treatment, and present it to potential financiers. They will also pitch the film to actors and directors (especially so-called
       <a href="/wiki/Bankable_star" title="Bankable star">
        bankable stars
       </a>
       ) in order to "attach" them to the project (that is, obtain a binding promise to work on the film if financing is ever secured). Many projects fail to move beyond this stage and enter so-called
       <a href="/wiki/Development_hell" title="Development hell">
        development hell
       </a>
       . If a pitch succeeds, a film receives a "
       <a href="/wiki/Green-light" title="Green-light">
        green light
       </a>
       ", meaning someone offers financial backing: typically a major
       <a href="/wiki/Film_studio" title="Film studio">
        film studio
       </a>
       , film council, or independent investor. The parties involved negotiate a deal and sign contracts.
      </p>
      <p>
       Once all parties have met and the deal has been set, the film may proceed into the pre-production period. By this stage, the film should have a clearly defined marketing strategy and target audience.
      </p>
      <p>
       Development of
       <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">
        animated
       </a>
       films differs slightly in that it is the director who develops and pitches a story to an executive producer on the basis of rough
       <a href="/wiki/Storyboard" title="Storyboard">
        storyboards
       </a>
       , and it is rare for a full-length screenplay to already exist at that point in time. If the film is green-lighted for further development and pre-production, then a screenwriter is later brought in to prepare the screenplay.
      </p>
      <p>
       Analogous to most any business venture, financing of a film project deals with the study of filmmaking as the management and procurement of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Investments" title="Investments">
        investments
       </a>
       . It includes the dynamics of
       <a href="/wiki/Asset" title="Asset">
        assets
       </a>
       that are required to fund the filmmaking and
       <a href="/wiki/Liability_(financial_accounting)" title="Liability (financial accounting)">
        liabilities
       </a>
       incurred during the filmmaking over the time period from early development through the management of profits and losses after distribution under conditions of different degrees of uncertainty and risk. The practical aspects of filmmaking finance can also be defined as the science of the money management of all phases involved in filmmaking. Film finance aims to price assets based on their risk level and their expected
       <a href="/wiki/Rate_of_return" title="Rate of return">
        rate of return
       </a>
       based upon anticipated profits and protection against losses.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-production">
        Pre-production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Filmmaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pre-production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Pre-production" title="Pre-production">
        Pre-production
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Pre-production" title="Pre-production">
        pre-production
       </a>
       , every step of actually creating the film is carefully designed and planned. The
       <a href="/wiki/Production_company" title="Production company">
        production company
       </a>
       is created and a
       <a href="/wiki/Production_office" title="Production office">
        production office
       </a>
       established. The film is pre-visualized by the director, and may be
       <a href="/wiki/Storyboard" title="Storyboard">
        storyboarded
       </a>
       with the help of
       <a href="/wiki/Illustrator" title="Illustrator">
        illustrators
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concept_artist" title="Concept artist">
        concept artists
       </a>
       . A
       <a href="/wiki/Production_budget" title="Production budget">
        production budget
       </a>
       is drawn up to plan expenditures for the film. For major productions,
       <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">
        insurance
       </a>
       is procured to protect against accidents.
      </p>
      <p>
       The nature of the film, and the budget, determine the size and type of crew used during filmmaking. Many Hollywood
       <a href="/wiki/Blockbuster_(entertainment)" title="Blockbuster (entertainment)">
        blockbusters
       </a>
       employ a cast and crew of hundreds, while a low-budget,
       <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">
        independent film
       </a>
       may be made by a skeleton crew of eight or nine (or fewer). These are typical crew positions:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Storyboard_artist" title="Storyboard artist">
         Storyboard artist
        </a>
        : creates visual images to help the director and production designer communicate their ideas to the production team.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">
         Director
        </a>
        : is primarily responsible for the storytelling, creative decisions and acting of the film.
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Assistant_director" title="Assistant director">
           Assistant director
          </a>
          (AD): manages the
          <a href="/wiki/Shooting_schedule" title="Shooting schedule">
           shooting schedule
          </a>
          and logistics of the production, among other tasks. There are several types of AD, each with different responsibilities.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Film_producer" title="Film producer">
         Film producer
        </a>
        : hires the film's crew.
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Unit_production_manager" title="Unit production manager">
           Unit production manager
          </a>
          : manages the
          <a href="/wiki/Production_budget" title="Production budget">
           production budget
          </a>
          and
          <a href="/wiki/Production_schedule" title="Production schedule">
           production schedule
          </a>
          . They also report, on behalf of the production office, to the
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_executives" title="Studio executives">
           studio executives
          </a>
          or financiers of the film.
          <ul>
           <li>
            <a href="/wiki/Location_manager" title="Location manager">
             Location manager
            </a>
            : finds and manages film locations. Nearly all pictures feature segments that are shot in the controllable environment of a studio
            <a href="/wiki/Sound_stage" title="Sound stage">
             sound stage
            </a>
            , while outdoor sequences call for filming
            <a href="/wiki/Location_shooting" title="Location shooting">
             on location
            </a>
            .
           </li>
          </ul>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Production_designer" title="Production designer">
         Production designer
        </a>
        : the one who creates the visual conception of the film, working with the
        <a href="/wiki/Art_director" title="Art director">
         art director
        </a>
        , who manages the art department, which makes
        <a href="/wiki/Production_sets" title="Production sets">
         production sets
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vincent_LoBrutto_1994_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Vincent_LoBrutto_1994-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Costume_designer" title="Costume designer">
           Costume designer
          </a>
          : creates the clothing for the characters in the film working closely with the actors, as well as other departments.
         </li>
         <li>
          Makeup and hair designer: works closely with the costume designer in order to create a certain look for a character.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casting_director" title="Casting director">
         Casting director
        </a>
        : finds actors to fill the parts in the script. This normally requires that actors audition.
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Choreographer" title="Choreographer">
           Choreographer
          </a>
          : creates and coordinates the movement and dance - typically for musicals. Some films also credit a
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fight_choreographer" title="Fight choreographer">
           fight choreographer
          </a>
          .
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">
         Director of photography (DP)
        </a>
        : the head of the
        <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">
         photography
        </a>
        of the entire film, supervises all
        <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">
         cinematographers
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Camera_operator" title="Camera operator">
         Camera Operators
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Production_sound_mixer" title="Production sound mixer">
         Production sound mixer
        </a>
        : the head of the sound department during the production stage of filmmaking. They record and mix the audio on set - dialogue,
        <a href="/wiki/Presence_(sound_recording)" title="Presence (sound recording)">
         presence
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_effects" title="Sound effects">
         sound effects
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Monaural" title="Monaural">
         mono
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Ambience_(sound_recording)" title="Ambience (sound recording)">
         ambience
        </a>
        in stereo.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        They work with the
        <a href="/wiki/Boom_operator_(media)" title="Boom operator (media)">
         boom operator
        </a>
        , Director, DA, DP, and First AD.
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_designer" title="Sound designer">
           Sound designer
          </a>
          : creates the aural conception of the film,
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vincent_LoBrutto_1994_2-1">
           <a href="#cite_note-Vincent_LoBrutto_1994-2">
            [2]
           </a>
          </sup>
          working with the
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supervising_Sound_Editor" title="Supervising Sound Editor">
           supervising sound editor
          </a>
          . On
          <a href="/wiki/Bollywood" title="Bollywood">
           Bollywood
          </a>
          -style Indian productions the sound designer plays the role of a
          <a href="/wiki/Director_of_audiography" title="Director of audiography">
           director of audiography
          </a>
          .
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
           <a href="#cite_note-5">
            [5]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">
           Composer
          </a>
          : creates new music for the film. (usually not until post-production)
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
        Production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Filmmaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:218px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Steven_Spielberg_with_Chandran_Rutnam_in_Sri_Lanka.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="295" data-file-width="216" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bc/Steven_Spielberg_with_Chandran_Rutnam_in_Sri_Lanka.jpg" width="216"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Steven_Spielberg_with_Chandran_Rutnam_in_Sri_Lanka.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">
          Steven Spielberg
         </a>
         (standing) with Chandran Rutnam in Sri Lanka, during the production of "Indiana Jones and the Temple of Doom" (released 1984)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Cinematography" title="Cinematography">
        Cinematography
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Audiography" title="Audiography">
        Audiography
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">
        Principal photography
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In production, the film is created and shot. More crew will be recruited at this stage, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Property_master" title="Property master">
        property master
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Script_supervisor" title="Script supervisor">
        script supervisor
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Assistant_director" title="Assistant director">
        assistant directors
       </a>
       , stills
       <a href="/wiki/Photographer" title="Photographer">
        photographer
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_editor" title="Film editor">
        picture editor
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_editor_(filmmaking)" title="Sound editor (filmmaking)">
        sound editors
       </a>
       . These are just the most common roles in filmmaking; the
       <a href="/wiki/Production_office" title="Production office">
        production office
       </a>
       will be free to create any unique blend of roles to suit the various responsibilities possible during the production of a film.
      </p>
      <p>
       A typical day's shooting begins with the crew arriving on the set/location by their call time. Actors usually have their own separate call times. Since set construction,
       <a href="/wiki/Set_dresser" title="Set dresser">
        dressing
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lighting" title="Lighting">
        lighting
       </a>
       can take many hours or even days, they are often set up in advance.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Grip_(job)" title="Grip (job)">
        grip
       </a>
       , electric and production design crews are typically a step ahead of the camera and sound departments: for efficiency's sake, while a scene is being filmed, they are already preparing the next one.
      </p>
      <p>
       While the crew prepare their equipment, the actors don their costumes and attend the hair and make-up departments. The actors rehearse the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_script" title="Film script">
        script
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Blocking_(stage)" title="Blocking (stage)">
        blocking
       </a>
       with the director, and the camera and sound crews rehearse with them and make final tweaks. Finally, the action is shot in as many
       <a href="/wiki/Take" title="Take">
        takes
       </a>
       as the director wishes. Most American productions follow a specific procedure:
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Assistant_director" title="Assistant director">
        assistant director
       </a>
       (AD) calls "picture is up!" to inform everyone that a take is about to be recorded, and then "quiet, everyone!" Once everyone is ready to shoot, the AD calls "roll sound" (if the take involves sound), and the
       <a href="/wiki/Production_sound_mixer" title="Production sound mixer">
        production sound mixer
       </a>
       will start their equipment, record a verbal slate of the take's information, and announce "sound speed", or just "speed", when they are ready. The AD follows with "roll camera", answered by "speed!" by the
       <a href="/wiki/Camera_operator" title="Camera operator">
        camera operator
       </a>
       once the camera is recording. The
       <a href="/wiki/Clapper_loader" title="Clapper loader">
        clapper
       </a>
       , who is already in front of the camera with the
       <a href="/wiki/Clapperboard" title="Clapperboard">
        clapperboard
       </a>
       , calls "marker!" and slaps it shut. If the take involves extras or background action, the AD will cue them ("action background!"), and last is the director, telling the actors "action!". The AD may echo "action" louder on large sets.
      </p>
      <p>
       A take is over when the director calls "cut!", and camera and sound stop recording. The script supervisor will note any continuity issues and the sound and camera teams log technical notes for the take on their respective report sheets. If the director decides additional takes are required, the whole process repeats. Once satisfied, the crew moves on to the next camera angle or "setup," until the whole scene is "covered." When shooting is finished for the scene, the assistant director declares a "wrap" or "moving on," and the crew will "strike," or dismantle, the set for that scene.
      </p>
      <p>
       At the end of the day, the director approves the next day's shooting schedule and a daily progress report is sent to the production office. This includes the report sheets from continuity, sound, and camera teams.
       <a href="/wiki/Daily_call_sheet" title="Daily call sheet">
        Call sheets
       </a>
       are distributed to the cast and crew to tell them when and where to turn up the next shooting day. Later on, the director, producer, other department heads, and, sometimes, the cast, may gather to watch that day or yesterday's footage, called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dailies" title="Dailies">
         dailies
        </a>
       </i>
       , and review their work.
      </p>
      <p>
       With workdays often lasting 14 or 18 hours in remote locations, film production tends to create a team spirit. When the entire film is
       <i>
        in the can,
       </i>
       or in the completion of the production phase, it is customary for the production office to arrange a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wrap_party" title="Wrap party">
        wrap party
       </a>
       , to thank all the cast and crew for their efforts.
      </p>
      <p>
       For the production phase on
       <a href="/wiki/Live_action" title="Live action">
        live-action films
       </a>
       , synchronizing work schedules of key cast and crew members is very important, since for many scenes, several cast members and most of the crew must be physically present at the same place at the same time (and bankable stars may need to rush from one project to another). Animated films have different workflow at the production phase, in that voice talent can record their takes in the
       <a href="/wiki/Recording_studio" title="Recording studio">
        recording studio
       </a>
       at different times and may not see one another until the film's premiere, while most physical live-action tasks are either unnecessary or are simulated by various types of
       <a href="/wiki/Animator" title="Animator">
        animators
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Post-production">
        Post-production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Filmmaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">
        Post-production
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Here the video/film is assembled by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_editor" title="Film editor">
        film editor
       </a>
       . The shot film material is edited. The production sound (dialogue) is also edited; music tracks and songs are composed and recorded if a film is sought to have a score; sound effects are designed and recorded. Any computer-graphic visual effects are digitally added by an
       <a href="/wiki/Artist" title="Artist">
        artist
       </a>
       . Finally, all sound elements are mixed into "stems", which are then married to picture, and the film is fully completed ("locked").
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">
        Distribution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Filmmaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distribution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Film_distribution" title="Film distribution">
        Film distribution
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Film_promotion" title="Film promotion">
        Film promotion
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       This is the final stage, where the film is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_release" title="Film release">
        released
       </a>
       to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinemas" title="Cinemas">
        cinemas
       </a>
       or, occasionally,
       <a href="/wiki/Direct-to-video" title="Direct-to-video">
        directly to consumer media
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">
        VHS
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VCD" title="VCD">
        VCD
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">
        DVD
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">
        Blu-ray
       </a>
       ) or direct download from a
       <a href="/wiki/Digital_media" title="Digital media">
        digital media
       </a>
       provider. The film is duplicated as required (either onto
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        film
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Hard_disk_drive" title="Hard disk drive">
        hard disk drives
       </a>
       ) and distributed to cinemas for exhibition (screening). Press kits, posters, and other advertising materials are published, and the film is advertised and
       <a href="/wiki/Film_promotion" title="Film promotion">
        promoted
       </a>
       . A
       <a href="/wiki/B-roll" title="B-roll">
        B-roll
       </a>
       clip may be released to the press based on raw footage shot for a "making of" documentary, which may include making-of clips as well as on-set interviews.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Film_distributor" title="Film distributor">
        Film distributors
       </a>
       usually release a film with a launch party, a
       <a href="/wiki/Red_carpet" title="Red carpet">
        red-carpet
       </a>
       premiere,
       <a href="/wiki/Press_release" title="Press release">
        press releases
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Interview" title="Interview">
        interviews
       </a>
       with the press, press preview screenings, and
       <a href="/wiki/Film_festival" title="Film festival">
        film festival
       </a>
       screenings. Most films are also promoted with their own special
       <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">
        website
       </a>
       separate from those of the production company or distributor. For major films, key personnel are often contractually required to participate in promotional tours in which they appear at premieres and festivals, and sit for interviews with many TV, print, and online journalists. The largest productions may require more than one promotional tour, in order to rejuvenate audience demand at each release window.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the advent of
       <a href="/wiki/Home_video" title="Home video">
        home video
       </a>
       in the early 1980s, most major films have followed a pattern of having several distinct release windows. A film may first be released to a few select cinemas, or if it
       <a href="/wiki/Test_screening" title="Test screening">
        tests well enough
       </a>
       , may go directly into
       <a href="/wiki/Wide_release" title="Wide release">
        wide release
       </a>
       . Next, it is released, normally at different times several weeks (or months) apart, into different market segments like
       <a href="/wiki/Video_rental_shop" title="Video rental shop">
        rental
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Retail" title="Retail">
        retail
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pay-per-view" title="Pay-per-view">
        pay-per-view
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/In-flight_entertainment" title="In-flight entertainment">
        in-flight entertainment
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">
        cable
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">
        satellite
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Free-to-air" title="Free-to-air">
        free-to-air
       </a>
       broadcast television. The distribution rights for the film are also usually sold for worldwide distribution. The distributor and the production company share profits and manage losses.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_filmmaking">
        Independent filmmaking
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Filmmaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Independent filmmaking">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">
        Independent film
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sound_recordist_Curtis_Choy.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1119" data-file-width="1412" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Sound_recordist_Curtis_Choy.jpg/220px-Sound_recordist_Curtis_Choy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Sound_recordist_Curtis_Choy.jpg/330px-Sound_recordist_Curtis_Choy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Sound_recordist_Curtis_Choy.jpg/440px-Sound_recordist_Curtis_Choy.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sound_recordist_Curtis_Choy.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Sound recordist
         <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Curtis_Choy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Curtis Choy (page does not exist)">
          Curtis Choy
         </a>
         (left) on location for
         <i>
          Dim Sum: a Little Bit of Heart
         </i>
         , an indie film by director Wayne Wang (center) on Clement Street in the Richmond District of San Francisco, California 1983. Photo by Nancy Wong
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Filmmaking also takes place outside of the mainstream and is commonly called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_filmmaking" title="Independent filmmaking">
        independent filmmaking
       </a>
       . Since the introduction of
       <a href="/wiki/DV" title="DV">
        DV
       </a>
       technology, the means of production have become more democratized. Filmmakers can conceivably shoot and edit a film, create and edit the sound and music, and mix the final cut on a home computer. However, while the means of production may be democratized, financing, traditional distribution, and marketing remain difficult to accomplish outside the traditional system. In the past, most independent filmmakers have relied on
       <a href="/wiki/Film_festival" title="Film festival">
        film festivals
       </a>
       (such as Sundance, Venice, Cannes and Toronto film festivals) to get their films noticed and sold for distribution and production. However, the
       <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">
        Internet
       </a>
       has allowed for relatively inexpensive distribution of independent films on websites such as YouTube. As a result, several companies have emerged to assist filmmakers in getting independent movies seen and sold via mainstream internet marketplaces, often adjacent to popular Hollywood titles. With internet movie distribution, independent filmmakers who choose to forgo a traditional distribution deal now have the ability to reach global audiences.
      </p>

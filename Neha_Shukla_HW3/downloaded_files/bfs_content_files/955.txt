   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Showrunner
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Showrunner
       </b>
       is the 21st-century term for the leading
       <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">
        executive producer
       </a>
       of a Hollywood television series in the United States.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The concept has since been adopted as well in the Canadian and British TV industries. A showrunner typically has creative control of a TV series production, through combining the responsibilities of the head
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">
        writer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">
        executive producer
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Script_editor" title="Script editor">
        script editor
       </a>
       . In films,
       <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">
        directors
       </a>
       typically have creative control of a production, but in television, the showrunner outranks the
       <a href="/wiki/Television_director" title="Television director">
        director
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVSR_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DVSR-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Showrunner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Traditionally, the executive producer of a television program was the
       <i>
        chief executive
       </i>
       , responsible for the show's creative direction and production. Over time, the title of executive producer was applied to a wider range of roles—from someone who arranges financing, to someone who holds the title as an
       <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">
        honorific
       </a>
       with no management duties. The term
       <i>
        showrunner
       </i>
       was created to identify the producer who holds ultimate management and creative authority for the program. The blog and book
       <i>
        Crafty Screenwriting
       </i>
       defines a showrunner as "the person responsible for all creative aspects of the show and responsible only to the network (and production company, if it's not [their] production company). The boss. Usually a writer."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crafty_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-crafty-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">
         Los Angeles Times
        </a>
       </i>
       columnist Scott Collins describes showrunners as:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-collins_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-collins-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        "Hyphenates", a curious hybrid of starry-eyed artists and tough-as-nails
        <a href="/wiki/Business_operations" title="Business operations">
         operational managers
        </a>
        . They're not just writers; they're not just producers. They hire and fire writers and crew members, develop
        <a href="/wiki/Plot_(narrative)" title="Plot (narrative)">
         story lines
        </a>
        , write scripts, cast actors, mind budgets and run interference with studio and network bosses. It's one of the most unusual and demanding,
        <a href="/wiki/Lateralization_of_brain_function" title="Lateralization of brain function">
         right-brain/left-brain
        </a>
        job descriptions in the entertainment world....[S]howrunners make – and often create – the show and now more than ever, shows are the only things that matter. In the "
        <a href="/wiki/Long_tail" title="Long tail">
         long tail
        </a>
        " entertainment economy, viewers don't watch networks. They don't even care about networks. They watch shows. And they don't care how they get them.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Shane_Brennan" title="Shane Brennan">
        Shane Brennan
       </a>
       , the showrunner for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/NCIS_(TV_series)" title="NCIS (TV series)">
         NCIS
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/NCIS:_Los_Angeles" title="NCIS: Los Angeles">
         NCIS: Los Angeles
        </a>
       </i>
       , states in an interview that:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        ... the moniker was created to identify the producer who actually held ultimate management and creative authority for the program, given the way the honorific "executive producer" was applied to a wider range of roles. There's also the fact that anyone with any power wanted a producer's credit, including the leading actors, who often did no more than say the writers' lines. "It had got to the stage where it was incredibly confusing; there were so many production credits no one knew who was responsible."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Typically, the showrunner is the creator or co-creator of the series, but this is not always the case. In long-running shows, often the creator of the show moves on, and day-to-day responsibilities of showrunning fall to other writers or writing teams.
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order" title="Law &amp; Order">
         Law &amp; Order
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/ER_(TV_series)" title="ER (TV series)">
         ER
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">
         The Simpsons
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_West_Wing" title="The West Wing">
         The West Wing
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation" title="Star Trek: The Next Generation">
         Star Trek: The Next Generation
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/NYPD_Blue" title="NYPD Blue">
         NYPD Blue
        </a>
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Supernatural_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Supernatural (U.S. TV series)">
         Supernatural
        </a>
       </i>
       are all examples of long-running shows that had successive, multiple showrunners.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">
        Canada
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Showrunner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Canada">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_Canada" title="Writers Guild of Canada">
        Writers Guild of Canada
       </a>
       , the union representing screenwriters in Canada, established the Showrunner Award in 2007, at the annual
       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Screenwriting_Awards" title="Canadian Screenwriting Awards">
        Canadian Screenwriting Awards
       </a>
       . The first Showrunner Award was presented in April 2007 to
       <a href="/wiki/Brad_Wright" title="Brad Wright">
        Brad Wright
       </a>
       , executive producer of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Stargate_Atlantis" title="Stargate Atlantis">
         Stargate Atlantis
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Stargate_SG-1" title="Stargate SG-1">
         Stargate SG-1
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wright-award_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wright-award-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">
        United Kingdom
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Showrunner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the first decade of the 21st century, the concept of a
       <i>
        showrunner
       </i>
       , specifically interpreted as a writer or presenter with overall responsibility for a television production, began to spread to the
       <a href="/wiki/Television_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Television in the United Kingdom">
        British television industry
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first British comedy series to use the term was
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/My_Family" title="My Family">
         My Family
        </a>
       </i>
       (2000–11), which had several showrunners in succession. Initially, the show was overseen by creator
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fred_Barron_(producer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fred Barron (producer) (page does not exist)">
        Fred Barron
       </a>
       from series 1–4. Ian Brown and James Hendrie took over for series 5, followed by American writer
       <a href="/wiki/Tom_Leopold" title="Tom Leopold">
        Tom Leopold
       </a>
       for series 6. Former
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cheers" title="Cheers">
         Cheers
        </a>
       </i>
       showrunner Tom Anderson was in charge from series 7 to the final series, series 11.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first writer appointed the role of showrunner on a British primetime drama was
       <a href="/wiki/Tony_McHale" title="Tony McHale">
        Tony McHale
       </a>
       , writer and creator of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Holby_City" title="Holby City">
         Holby City
        </a>
       </i>
       , in 2005.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Jed_Mercurio" title="Jed Mercurio">
        Jed Mercurio
       </a>
       had carried out a similar role on the less conspicuous medical drama
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Bodies_(TV_series)" title="Bodies (TV series)">
         Bodies
        </a>
       </i>
       (2004–2006).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guardian-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       But
       <a href="/wiki/Russell_T_Davies" title="Russell T Davies">
        Russell T Davies
       </a>
       ' work on the 2005 revival of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">
         Doctor Who
        </a>
       </i>
       brought the term to prominence in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_television" title="British television">
        British television
       </a>
       (to the extent that in 2009 a writer for
       <i>
        The Guardian
       </i>
       wrote that "Over here, the concept of 'showrunner' has only made it as far as
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       ").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In an interview, Davies said that he felt the role of the showrunner was to establish and maintain a consistent tone in a drama.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       remains the most prominent example of a British television programme with a showrunner, with
       <a href="/wiki/Steven_Moffat" title="Steven Moffat">
        Steven Moffat
       </a>
       having taken over the post from Davies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The term has also been used to refer to other writer-producers, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Tony_Jordan" title="Tony Jordan">
        Tony Jordan
       </a>
       on
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Moving_Wallpaper" title="Moving Wallpaper">
         Moving Wallpaper
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Echo_Beach_(soap)" title="Echo Beach (soap)">
         Echo Beach
        </a>
       </i>
       , Ann McManus on
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Waterloo_Road_(TV_series)" title="Waterloo Road (TV series)">
         Waterloo Road
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Hodges" title="Adrian Hodges">
        Adrian Hodges
       </a>
       on
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Primeval" title="Primeval">
         Primeval
        </a>
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jed_Mercurio" title="Jed Mercurio">
        Jed Mercurio
       </a>
       on
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Bodies_(TV_series)" title="Bodies (TV series)">
         Bodies
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guardian-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Line_of_Duty" title="Line of Duty">
         Line of Duty
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critical_(TV_Series)" title="Critical (TV Series)">
         Critical
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_10-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guardian-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">
        See also
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

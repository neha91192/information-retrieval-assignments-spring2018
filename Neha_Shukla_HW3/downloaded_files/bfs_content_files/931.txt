   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Imagination
   </h1>

      <p>
       <b>
        Imagination
       </b>
       , also called the faculty of
       <b>
        imagining
       </b>
       , is the creative ability to form images, ideas, and sensations in the
       <a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">
        mind
       </a>
       without any immediate input of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senses" title="Senses">
        senses
       </a>
       (such as seeing or hearing). Imagination helps make knowledge applicable in solving problems and is fundamental to integrating experience and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Learning_process" title="Learning process">
        learning process
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norman2000_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Norman2000-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sutton-Smith88_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sutton-Smith88-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Egan92_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Egan92-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A basic training for imagination is listening to
       <a href="/wiki/Storytelling" title="Storytelling">
        storytelling
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">
        narrative
       </a>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norman2000_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Norman2000-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in which the exactness of the chosen words is the fundamental factor to "evoke worlds".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Imagination is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive_process" title="Cognitive process">
        cognitive process
       </a>
       used in mental functioning and sometimes used in conjunction with psychological
       <a href="/wiki/Imagery" title="Imagery">
        imagery
       </a>
       . The cognate term of mental imagery may be used in
       <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">
        psychology
       </a>
       for denoting the process of reviving in the mind recollections of objects formerly given in sense perception. Since this use of the term conflicts with that of ordinary
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        language
       </a>
       , some psychologists have preferred to describe this process as "
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/imaging" title="wikt:imaging">
        imaging
       </a>
       " or "
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/imagery" title="wikt:imagery">
        imagery
       </a>
       " or to speak of it as "reproductive" as opposed to "productive" or "constructive" imagination. Constructive imagination is further divided into voluntary top-down imagination driven by the
       <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">
        prefrontal cortex
       </a>
       , that is called
       <a href="/wiki/Mental_synthesis" title="Mental synthesis">
        mental synthesis
       </a>
       , and spontaneous bottom up involuntary generation of novel images that occurs during dreaming. Imagined images, both novel and recalled, are seen with the "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mind%27s_eye" title="Mind's eye">
        mind's eye
       </a>
       ".
      </p>
      <p>
       Imagination can also be expressed through stories such as
       <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">
        fairy tales
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">
        fantasies
       </a>
       . Children often use such
       <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">
        narratives
       </a>
       and pretend play in order to exercise their imaginations. When children develop fantasy they play at two levels: first, they use
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Role_playing" title="Role playing">
        role playing
       </a>
       to act out what they have developed with their imagination, and at the second level they play again with their make-believe situation by acting as if what they have developed is an actual reality.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span id="The_mind.27s_eye">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_mind's_eye">
        The mind's eye
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imagination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The mind's eye">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The notion of a "mind's eye" goes back at least to
       <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">
        Cicero
       </a>
       's reference to
       <i>
        <b>
         mentis oculi
        </b>
       </i>
       during his discussion of the orator's appropriate use of
       <a href="/wiki/Simile" title="Simile">
        simile
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In this discussion, Cicero observed that allusions to "the
       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Gab%C3%A8s" title="Gulf of Gabès">
        Syrtis
       </a>
       of his patrimony" and "the
       <a href="/wiki/Charybdis" title="Charybdis">
        Charybdis
       </a>
       of his possessions" involved similes that were "too far-fetched"; and he advised the orator to, instead, just speak of "the rock" and "the gulf" (respectively) — on the grounds that "the eyes of the mind are more easily directed to those objects which we have seen, than to those which we have only heard".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The concept of "the mind's eye" first appeared in English in
       <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">
        Chaucer's
       </a>
       (c.1387)
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Man_of_Law%27s_Prologue_and_Tale" title="The Man of Law's Prologue and Tale">
        Man of Law's Tale
       </a>
       in his
       <a href="/wiki/The_Canterbury_Tales" title="The Canterbury Tales">
        Canterbury Tales
       </a>
       , where he tells us that one of the three men dwelling in a castle was blind, and could only see with "the eyes of his mind"; namely, those eyes "with which all men see after they have become blind".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imagination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The common use of the term is for the process of forming new images in the mind that have not been previously experienced with the help of what has been seen, heard, or felt before, or at least only partially or in different combinations. Some typical examples follow:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">
         Fairy tale
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Fiction" title="Fiction">
         Fiction
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        A form of
        <a href="/wiki/Verisimilitude" title="Verisimilitude">
         verisimilitude
        </a>
        often invoked in
        <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">
         fantasy
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
         science fiction
        </a>
        invites readers to pretend such stories are true by referring to
        <a href="/wiki/Object_of_the_mind" title="Object of the mind">
         objects of the mind
        </a>
        such as fictional books or years that do not exist apart from an
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imaginary_world" title="Imaginary world">
         imaginary world
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Imagination, not being limited to the acquisition of exact knowledge by the requirements of practical necessity is largely free from objective restraints. The ability to imagine one's self in another person's place is very important to social relations and understanding.
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
        Albert Einstein
       </a>
       said, "Imagination ... is more important than knowledge. Knowledge is limited. Imagination encircles the world."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The same limitations beset imagination in the field of
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">
        scientific
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">
        hypothesis
       </a>
       . Progress in scientific research is due largely to provisional explanations which are developed by imagination, but such hypotheses must be framed in relation to previously ascertained facts and in accordance with the principles of the particular science.
      </p>
      <p>
       Imagination is an experimental partition of the mind used to develop theories and ideas based on functions. Taking objects from real perceptions, the imagination uses complex IF-functions
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       to develop new or revised ideas. This part of the mind is vital to developing better and easier ways to accomplish old and new tasks. In sociology, Imagination is used to part ways with reality and have an understanding of social interactions derived from a perspective outside of society itself. This leads to the development of theories through questions that wouldn't usually be asked. These experimental ideas can be safely conducted inside a virtual world and then, if the idea is probable and the function is true, the idea can be actualized in reality. Imagination is the key to new development of the mind and can be shared with others, progressing collectively.
      </p>
      <p>
       Regarding the volunteer effort, imagination can be classified as:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        voluntary (the dream from the sleep, the daydream)
       </li>
       <li>
        involuntary (the reproductive imagination, the creative imagination, the dream of perspective)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Psychology">
        Psychology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imagination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Psychology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Psychologists have studied imaginative thought, not only in its exotic form of
       <a href="/wiki/Creativity" title="Creativity">
        creativity
       </a>
       and artistic expression but also in its mundane form of everyday imagination.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruth_M.J._Byrne" title="Ruth M.J. Byrne">
        Ruth M.J. Byrne
       </a>
       has proposed that everyday imaginative thoughts about
       <a href="/wiki/Counterfactual_thinking" title="Counterfactual thinking">
        counterfactual
       </a>
       alternatives to reality may be based on the same cognitive processes on which rational thoughts are also based.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Children can engage in the creation of imaginative alternatives to reality from their very early years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Cultural_psychology" title="Cultural psychology">
        Cultural psychology
       </a>
       is currently elaborating a view of imagination as a higher mental function involved in a number of everyday activities, both at the individual and collective level
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that enables people to manipulate complex meanings of both linguistic and iconic forms in the process of experiencing.
      </p>
      <p>
       The phenomenology of imagination is discussed In
       <i>
        The Imaginary: A Phenomenological Psychology of the Imagination
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         L'Imaginaire: Psychologie phénoménologique de l'imagination
        </span>
       </i>
       ), also published under the title
       <i>
        The Psychology of the Imagination
       </i>
       <b>
        ,
       </b>
       is a 1940 book by
       <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre" title="Jean-Paul Sartre">
        Jean-Paul Sartre
       </a>
       , in which he propounds his concept of the imagination and discusses what the existence of imagination shows about the nature of human consciousness.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The imagination is also active in our perception of photographic images in order to make them appear real.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Memory">
        Memory
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imagination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Memory">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Mental_image" title="Mental image">
        Mental image
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Imagery" title="Imagery">
        Imagery
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Memory and mental imagery, often seen as a part of the process of imagination, have been shown to be affected by one another.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Long_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Long-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       "Images made by functional magnetic resonance imaging technology show that remembering and imagining sends blood to identical parts of the brain."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Long_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Long-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Various psuchologicfal factors and influence the mental processing of and can heighten the chance of the brain to retain information as either long-term memories or short-term memories. John Sweller indicated that experiences stored as long-term memories are easier to recall, as they are ingrained deeper in the mind. Each of these forms require information to be taught in a specific manner so as to use various regions of the brain when being processed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This information can potentially help develop programs for young students to cultivate or further enhance their creative abilities from a young age. The
       <a href="/wiki/Neocortex" title="Neocortex">
        neocortex
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">
        thalamus
       </a>
       are responsible for controlling the brain's imagination, along with many of the brain's other functions such as consciousness and abstract thought.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since imagination involves many different brain functions, such as emotions, memory, thoughts, etc., portions of the brain where multiple functions occur—such as the thalamus and neocortex—are the main regions where imaginative processing has been documented.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The understanding of
       <i>
        how
       </i>
       memory and imagination are linked in the brain, paves the way to better understand one's ability to link significant past experiences with their imagination.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Perception">
        Perception
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imagination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Perception">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Piaget" title="Jean Piaget">
        Piaget
       </a>
       posited that
       <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">
        perceptions
       </a>
       depend on the world view of a person. The world view is the result of arranging perceptions into existing imagery by imagination. Piaget cites the example of a child saying that the moon is following her when she walks around the village at night. Like this, perceptions are integrated into the world view to make sense. Imagination is needed to make sense of perceptions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Versus_belief">
        Versus belief
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imagination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Versus belief">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Imagination is different from
       <a href="/wiki/Belief" title="Belief">
        belief
       </a>
       because the subject understands that what is personally invented by the mind does not necessarily affect the course of action taken in the apparently shared world, while beliefs are part of what one holds as truths about both the shared and personal worlds. The play of imagination, apart from the obvious limitations (e.g. of avoiding explicit self-contradiction), is conditioned only by the general trend of the mind at a given moment. Belief, on the other hand, is immediately related to practical activity: it is perfectly possible to imagine oneself a millionaire, but unless one believes it one does not, therefore, act as such. Belief endeavors to conform to the subject's experienced conditions or faith in the possibility of those conditions; whereas imagination as such is specifically free. The dividing line between imagination and belief varies widely in different stages of technological development. Thus in more extreme cases, someone from a primitive culture who ill frames an ideal reconstruction of the causes of his illness, and attributes it to the hostile magic of an enemy based on faith and tradition rather than science. In ignorance of the science of pathology the subject is satisfied with this explanation, and actually believes in it, sometimes to the point of death, due to what is known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Nocebo" title="Nocebo">
        nocebo
       </a>
       effect. It follows that the learned distinction between imagination and belief depends in practice on religion, tradition, and culture.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (September 2017)">
           according to whom?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Brain_activation">
        Brain activation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imagination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Brain activation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A study using
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FMRI" title="FMRI">
        fMRI
       </a>
       while subjects were asked to imagine precise visual figures, to mentally disassemble them, or mentally blend them, showed activity in the
       <a href="/wiki/Occipital_lobe" title="Occipital lobe">
        occipital
       </a>
       , frontoparietal,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posterior_parietal" title="Posterior parietal">
        posterior parietal
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Precuneus" title="Precuneus">
        precuneus
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorsolateral_prefrontal" title="Dorsolateral prefrontal">
        dorsolateral prefrontal
       </a>
       regions of the subject's brains.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_reality">
        As a reality
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imagination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: As a reality">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The world as experienced is an interpretation of data arriving from the senses; as such, it is perceived as real by contrast to most thoughts and imaginings. Users of hallucinogenic drugs are said to have a heightened imagination. This difference is only one of degree and can be altered by several historic causes, namely changes to brain chemistry,
       <a href="/wiki/Hypnosis" title="Hypnosis">
        hypnosis
       </a>
       or other
       <a href="/wiki/Altered_state_of_consciousness" title="Altered state of consciousness">
        altered states of consciousness
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">
        meditation
       </a>
       , many hallucinogenic drugs, and electricity applied directly to specific parts of the brain. The difference between imagined and perceived reality can be proven by
       <a href="/wiki/Psychosis" title="Psychosis">
        psychosis
       </a>
       . Many mental illnesses can be attributed to this inability to distinguish between the sensed and the internally created worlds. Some cultures and traditions even view the apparently shared world as an illusion of the mind as with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">
        Buddhist
       </a>
       maya, or go to the opposite extreme and accept the imagined and dreamed realms as of equal validity to the apparently shared world as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Aborigines" title="Australian Aborigines">
        Australian Aborigines
       </a>
       do with their concept of
       <a href="/wiki/Dreamtime" title="Dreamtime">
        dreamtime
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Imagination, because of having freedom from external limitations, can often become a source of real
       <a href="/wiki/Pleasure" title="Pleasure">
        pleasure
       </a>
       and unnecessary
       <a href="/wiki/Suffering" title="Suffering">
        suffering
       </a>
       . Consistent with this idea, imagining pleasurable and fearful events is found to engage emotional circuits involved in emotional perception and experience.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Costa_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Costa-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A person of vivid imagination often suffers acutely from the imagined perils besetting friends, relatives, or even strangers such as celebrities. Also crippling
       <a href="/wiki/Fear" title="Fear">
        fear
       </a>
       can result from taking an imagined painful future too seriously.
      </p>

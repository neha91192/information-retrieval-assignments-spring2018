       ) is a piece of prose, typically the step between scene cards (index cards) and the first draft of a
       <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">
        screenplay
       </a>
       for a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_picture" title="Motion picture">
        motion picture
       </a>
       , television program, or radio play. It is generally longer and more detailed than an
       <a href="/wiki/Step_outline" title="Step outline">
        outline
       </a>
       (or one-page
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/synopsis" title="wikt:synopsis">
        synopsis
       </a>
       ), and it may include details of directorial style that an outline omits. Treatments read like a short story, but are told in the present tense and describe events as they happen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A treatment may also be created in the process of
       <a href="/wiki/Film_adaptation" title="Film adaptation">
        adapting
       </a>
       a novel, play, or other pre-existing work into a screenplay.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Original_draft_treatment">
        Original draft treatment
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Original draft treatment">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The original draft treatment is created during the writing process, and is generally long and detailed. It consists of full-scene outlines put together. Usually there are between thirty and eighty standard
       <a href="/wiki/Letter_(paper_size)" title="Letter (paper size)">
        letter size
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A4_paper#The_A_series" title="A4 paper">
        A4
       </a>
       pages (
       <a href="/wiki/Courier_(typeface)" title="Courier (typeface)">
        Courier
       </a>
       New 12 point), with an average of about forty pages. For example,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Terminator" title="The Terminator">
         The Terminator
        </a>
       </i>
       is forty-eight pages.
      </p>
      <p>
       More elaborate forms of the draft treatment are the step outline and the
       <a href="/wiki/Scriptment" title="Scriptment">
        scriptment
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Presentation_treatment">
        Presentation treatment
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Presentation treatment">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The presentation treatment is created as presentation material. Generally the scene card descriptions are written out in order, and only have the essential and important story events that make up the scenes. It is the full story in its simplest form, moving from the
       <a href="/wiki/Concept" title="Concept">
        concept
       </a>
       , to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_(literature)" title="Theme (literature)">
        theme
       </a>
       , to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fictional_character" title="Fictional character">
        character
       </a>
       , to the detailed synopsis of about four to eight pages of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scene_(filming)#master_scenes" title="Scene (filming)">
        master scenes
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Presentation treatments are used to show how the production notes have been incorporated into the screenplay for the
       <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">
        director
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Film_producer" title="Film producer">
        production executives
       </a>
       to look over, or to leave behind as a presentation note after a sales
       <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(filmmaking)" title="Pitch (filmmaking)">
        pitch
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The presentation treatment is the appropriate treatment to submit if a script submission requires one. They are usually three to thirty pages in length, with an average of seven to twelve pages.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
        Usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Treatments are widely used within the motion picture industry as selling documents to outline story and character aspects of a planned screenplay, whereas outlines are generally produced as part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Screenplay#The_Development_Process" title="Screenplay">
        development process
       </a>
       . Screenwriters may use a treatment to initially pitch a screenplay, but may also use a treatment to sell a concept they are pitching without a completed screenplay.
      </p>

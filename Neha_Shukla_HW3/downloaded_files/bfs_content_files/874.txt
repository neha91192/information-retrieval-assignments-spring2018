      <p>
       A
       <b>
        screenplay
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        script
       </b>
       is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Written_work" title="Written work">
        written work
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">
        screenwriters
       </a>
       for a
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        film
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">
        video game
       </a>
       , or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">
        television program
       </a>
       . These screenplays can be original works or adaptations from existing pieces of writing. In them, the movement, actions, expression, and dialogues of the characters are also narrated. A screenplay written for television is also known as a
       <a href="/wiki/Teleplay" title="Teleplay">
        teleplay
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Format_and_style">
        Format and style
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenplay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Format and style">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The format is structured so that one page equates to roughly one minute of screen time, though this is only used as a ballpark estimate and often bears little resemblance to the running time of the final movie.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The standard font is 12 point, 10 pitch
       <a href="/wiki/Courier_(typeface)" title="Courier (typeface)">
        Courier
       </a>
       Typeface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The major components are action (sometimes called "screen direction") and
       <a href="/wiki/Dialogue" title="Dialogue">
        dialogue
       </a>
       . The action is written in the
       <a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">
        present tense
       </a>
       and is limited to what can be heard or seen by the audience, for example descriptions of settings, character movements, or sound effects. The dialogue is the words the characters speak, and is written in a center column.
      </p>
      <p>
       Unique to the screenplay (as opposed to a stage play) is the use of slug lines. A
       <i>
        slug line
       </i>
       , also called a
       <i>
        master scene heading
       </i>
       , occurs at the start of every scene and typically contains three pieces of information: whether the scene is set inside (interior/INT.) or outside (exterior/EXT.), the specific location, and the time of day. Each slug line begins a new scene. In a "
       <a href="/wiki/Shooting_script" title="Shooting script">
        shooting script
       </a>
       " the slug lines are numbered consecutively for ease of reference.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_format">
        Physical format
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenplay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical format">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       American screenplays are printed single-sided on three-hole-punched paper using the standard American
       <a href="/wiki/Paper_size" title="Paper size">
        letter size
       </a>
       (8.5 x 11 inch). They are then held together with two
       <a href="/wiki/Brass_fastener" title="Brass fastener">
        brass brads
       </a>
       in the top and bottom hole. The middle hole is left empty as it would otherwise make it harder to quickly read the script.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the United Kingdom, double-hole-punched
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A4_paper_size" title="A4 paper size">
        A4
       </a>
       paper is normally used, which is slightly taller and narrower than US letter size. Some UK writers format the scripts for use in the US letter size, especially when their scripts are to be read by American producers, since the pages would otherwise be cropped when printed on US paper. Because each country's standard paper size is difficult to obtain in the other country, British writers often send an electronic copy to American producers, or crop the A4 size to US letter.
      </p>
      <p>
       A British script may be bound by a single brad at the top left hand side of the page, making flicking through the paper easier during script meetings. Screenplays are usually bound with a light card stock cover and back page, often showing the logo of the production company or agency submitting the script, covers are there to protect the script during handling which can reduce the strength of the paper. This is especially important if the script is likely to pass through the hands of several people or through the post.
      </p>
      <p>
       Increasingly,
       <i>
        reading copies
       </i>
       of screenplays (that is, those distributed by producers and agencies in the hope of attracting finance or talent) are distributed printed on both sides of the paper (often professionally bound) to reduce paper waste. Occasionally they are reduced to half-size to make a small book which is convenient to read or put in a pocket; this is generally for use by the director or production crew during shooting.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although most writing contracts continue to stipulate physical delivery of three or more copies of a finished script, it is common for scripts to be delivered electronically via email.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Screenplay_formats">
        Screenplay formats
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenplay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Screenplay formats">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Screenplays and teleplays use a set of standardizations, beginning with proper formatting. These rules are in part to serve the practical purpose of making scripts uniformly readable "blueprints" of movies, and also to serve as a way of distinguishing a professional from an amateur.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Feature_film">
        Feature film
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenplay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Feature film">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Godfather_Screenplay.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/The_Godfather_Screenplay.JPG/220px-The_Godfather_Screenplay.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/The_Godfather_Screenplay.JPG/330px-The_Godfather_Screenplay.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/The_Godfather_Screenplay.JPG/440px-The_Godfather_Screenplay.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Godfather_Screenplay.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Screenplay for
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/The_Godfather_Part_II" title="The Godfather Part II">
           The Godfather Part II
          </a>
         </i>
         , Turin, Italy
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Motion picture screenplays intended for submission to mainstream studios, whether in the US or elsewhere in the world, are expected to conform to a standard
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typographical" title="Typographical">
        typographical
       </a>
       style known widely as
       <i>
        studio format
       </i>
       which stipulates how elements of the screenplay such as scene headings, action, transitions, dialog, character names, shots and parenthetical matter should be presented on the page, as well as the font size and line spacing.
      </p>
      <p>
       One reason for this is that, when rendered in studio format, most screenplays will transfer onto the screen at the rate of approximately one page per minute. This rule of thumb is widely contested — a page of dialogue usually occupies less screen time than a page of action, for example, and it depends enormously on the literary style of the writer — and yet it continues to hold sway in modern
       <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">
        Hollywood
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There is no single standard for studio format. Some studios have definitions of the required format written into the rubric of their writer's contract. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicholl_Fellowship" title="Nicholl Fellowship">
        Nicholl Fellowship
       </a>
       , a screenwriting competition run under the auspices of the
       <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Motion_Picture_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences">
        Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences
       </a>
       , has a guide to screenplay format.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A more detailed reference is
       <i>
        The Complete Guide to Standard Script Formats
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Screenplays are almost always written using a
       <a href="/wiki/Monospaced_font" title="Monospaced font">
        monospaced font
       </a>
       , often a variant of Courier or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Courier_New" title="Courier New">
        Courier New
       </a>
       , both mostly used as 12 pt font. This is preferred due to its clarity. Screenplays are usually 90-120 pages long.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spec_screenplay">
        Spec screenplay
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenplay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spec screenplay">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       A "
       <a href="/wiki/Spec_script" title="Spec script">
        spec script
       </a>
       " or speculative screenplay is a script written to be sold on the open market with no upfront payment, or promise of payment. The content is usually invented solely by the screenwriter, though spec screenplays can also be based on established works, or real people and events.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Television">
        Television
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenplay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Television">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       For American TV shows, the format rules for hour-long dramas, like
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/CSI:_Crime_Scene_Investigation" title="CSI: Crime Scene Investigation">
         CSI
        </a>
       </i>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Single-camera_setup" title="Single-camera setup">
        single-camera
       </a>
       sitcoms, like
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scrubs_(TV_Series)" title="Scrubs (TV Series)">
         Scrubs
        </a>
       </i>
       , are essentially the same as for motion pictures. The main difference is that TV scripts have act breaks.
       <a href="/wiki/Multiple-camera_setup" title="Multiple-camera setup">
        Multi-camera
       </a>
       sitcoms like
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/I_Love_Lucy" title="I Love Lucy">
         I Love Lucy
        </a>
       </i>
       use a different, specialized format that derives from radio and the stage play. In this format, dialogue is double-spaced, action lines are capitalized, and scene headings, character entrances and exits, and sound effects are capitalized and underlined.
      </p>
      <p>
       Drama series and sitcoms are no longer the only formats that require the skills of a writer. With reality-based programming crossing genres to create various hybrid programs, many of the so-called "reality" programs are in a large part scripted in format. That is, the overall skeleton of the show and its episodes are written to dictate the content and direction of the program. The Writers Guild of America has identified this as a legitimate writer's medium, so much so that they have lobbied to impose jurisdiction over writers and producers who "format" reality-based productions. Creating reality show formats involves storytelling structure similar to screenwriting, but much more condensed and boiled down to specific plot points or actions related to the overall concept and story.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Documentaries">
        Documentaries
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenplay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Documentaries">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The script format for documentaries and audio-visual presentations which consist largely of voice-over matched to still or moving pictures is different again and uses a two-column format which can be particularly difficult to achieve in standard word processors, at least when it comes to editing or rewriting. Many script-editing software programs include templates for documentary formats.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Screenwriting_software">
        Screenwriting software
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenplay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Screenwriting software">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriting_software" title="Screenwriting software">
        Screenwriting software
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Various
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriting_software" title="Screenwriting software">
        screenwriting software
       </a>
       packages are available to help screenwriters adhere to the strict formatting conventions. Detailed computer programs are designed specifically to format screenplays, teleplays, and stage plays. Such packages include BPC-Screenplay,
       <a href="/wiki/Celtx" title="Celtx">
        Celtx
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Fade_In" title="Fade In">
        Fade In
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Final_Draft_(software)" title="Final Draft (software)">
        Final Draft
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/FiveSprockets" title="FiveSprockets">
        FiveSprockets
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Montage_(software)" title="Montage (software)">
        Montage
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Movie_Magic_Screenwriter" title="Movie Magic Screenwriter">
        Movie Magic Screenwriter
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Movie_Outline" title="Movie Outline">
        Movie Outline
       </a>
       3.0, and
       <a href="/wiki/Scrivener_(software)" title="Scrivener (software)">
        Scrivener
       </a>
       , Movie Draft SE and Zhura. Software is also available as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Web_applications" title="Web applications">
        web applications
       </a>
       , accessible from any computer, and on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_devices" title="Mobile devices">
        mobile devices
       </a>
       , such as Fade In Mobile and Scripts Pro.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first screenwriting software was
       <a href="/wiki/SmartKey" title="SmartKey">
        SmartKey
       </a>
       , a macro program that sent strings of commands to existing word processing programs, such as
       <a href="/wiki/WordStar" title="WordStar">
        WordStar
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/WordPerfect" title="WordPerfect">
        WordPerfect
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Word" title="Microsoft Word">
        Microsoft Word
       </a>
       . SmartKey was popular with screenwriters from 1982–1987, after which word processing programs had their own macro features.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Script_coverage">
        Script coverage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenplay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Script coverage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Script_coverage" title="Script coverage">
        Script coverage
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Script coverage, is a filmmaking term for the analysis and grading of screenplays, often within the "script development" department of a production company. While coverage may remain entirely verbal, it usually takes the form of a written report, guided by a rubric that varies from company to company. The original idea behind coverage was that a producer's assistant could read a script and then give their producer a breakdown of the project and suggest whether they should consider producing the screenplay or not.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

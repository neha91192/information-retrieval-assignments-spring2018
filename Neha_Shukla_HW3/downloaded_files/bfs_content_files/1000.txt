      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Cinematography" title="Cinematography">
        cinematography
       </a>
       , the
       <b>
        dolly grip
       </b>
       is a dedicated technician trained to operate the
       <a href="/wiki/Camera_dolly" title="Camera dolly">
        camera dolly
       </a>
       . This technician places, levels, and moves the dolly track, then pushes and pulls the dolly and usually a
       <a href="/wiki/Camera_operator" title="Camera operator">
        camera operator
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camera_assistant" title="Camera assistant">
        camera assistant
       </a>
       as riders. If the dolly has a moveable vertical axis, such as a hydraulic arm, then the dolly grip also operates the "boom". If both axes are used simultaneously, this type of
       <a href="/wiki/Tracking_shot" title="Tracking shot">
        dolly shot
       </a>
       is known as a compound move.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A dolly grip must work closely with the camera crew to perfect these complex movements (
       <a href="/wiki/Cinematic_techniques" title="Cinematic techniques">
        cinematic techniques
       </a>
       ) during rehearsals. Focusing the lens is critical to capturing a sharp image, so a dolly grip must hit his or her marks in concert with a camera assistant who pulls focus. It is a skill that experience can hone to a point, but the best dolly grips are known for their "touch," and that makes them highly sought-after.
      </p>
      <p>
       A dolly grip is also employed when the camera is operated in handheld mode (on the operator's shoulders or literally in their hands). While the camera operator is moving with the camera, the dolly grip is responsible for the operator's safety, helping the operator to "blindly" negotiate sometimes complicated environments. The dolly grip silently directs the operator (through gentle touches, nudges, pulls and pushes) away from walls and other obstacles that the operator cannot see while concentrating on the image in the camera viewfinder. The same is true when the camera is operated with a
       <a href="/wiki/Steadicam" title="Steadicam">
        Steadicam
       </a>
       or similar body-mounted stabilization tool.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although dolly grips are hired by and under a
       <a href="/wiki/Key_grip" title="Key grip">
        key grip
       </a>
       , they are paid the same as (or more than) a
       <a href="/wiki/Best_boy" title="Best boy">
        best boy grip
       </a>
       , who is the second-in-command.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Dolly grips may also push a wheeled platform holding the microphone and boom operator.
      </p>
      <p>
       Crane operators in the film industry are specially trained film crew. They are normally
       <a href="/wiki/Grip_(job)" title="Grip (job)">
        grips
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Some
       <a href="/wiki/Shot_(filmmaking)" title="Shot (filmmaking)">
        shots
       </a>
       require the
       <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">
        camera
       </a>
       to move. This can be done several ways, one of which is to use a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camera_crane" title="Camera crane">
        camera crane
       </a>
       . There are many types of camera cranes, most being a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counterbalance" title="Counterbalance">
        counterbalanced
       </a>
       arm on a pivot, whilst others are hydraulic. Cranes can be used to lift the camera, and often the camera operator and assistant also, quickly into the air. The crane operator sets up and operates the camera crane so that the camera arrives at the right spot. This can be difficult since the camera may be a long distance from the operator.
      </p>

      <p>
       <b>
        David Alan Mamet
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ; born November 30, 1947) is an American playwright, film director, screenwriter and author. He won a
       <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">
        Pulitzer Prize
       </a>
       and received
       <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award" title="Tony Award">
        Tony
       </a>
       nominations for his plays
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Glengarry_Glen_Ross" title="Glengarry Glen Ross">
         Glengarry Glen Ross
        </a>
       </i>
       (1984) and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Speed-the-Plow" title="Speed-the-Plow">
         Speed-the-Plow
        </a>
       </i>
       (1988). He first gained critical acclaim for a trio of off-Broadway plays in 1976:
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Duck_Variations" title="The Duck Variations">
         The Duck Variations
        </a>
        ,
       </i>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sexual_Perversity_in_Chicago" title="Sexual Perversity in Chicago">
         Sexual Perversity in Chicago
        </a>
        ,
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/American_Buffalo_(play)" title="American Buffalo (play)">
         American Buffalo
        </a>
        .
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filmmakers1_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-filmmakers1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His play
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Race_(play)" title="Race (play)">
         Race
        </a>
       </i>
       opened on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadway_theater" title="Broadway theater">
        Broadway
       </a>
       on December 6, 2009, and his play
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Penitent_(play)" title="The Penitent (play)">
         The Penitent
        </a>
       </i>
       previewed off-Broadway on February 8, 2017.
      </p>
      <p>
       Feature films that Mamet both wrote and directed include
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/House_of_Games" title="House of Games">
         House of Games
        </a>
       </i>
       (1987),
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Spanish_Prisoner" title="The Spanish Prisoner">
         The Spanish Prisoner
        </a>
       </i>
       (1997),
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Winslow_Boy_(1999_film)" title="The Winslow Boy (1999 film)">
         The Winslow Boy
        </a>
       </i>
       (1999),
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heist_(film)" title="Heist (film)">
         Heist
        </a>
       </i>
       (2001),
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Redbelt" title="Redbelt">
         Redbelt
        </a>
       </i>
       (2008), and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phil_Spector_(2013_film)" title="Phil Spector (2013 film)">
         Phil Spector
        </a>
       </i>
       (2013). He wrote the screenplays for
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Verdict_(film)" title="The Verdict (film)">
         The Verdict
        </a>
       </i>
       (1982),
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Postman_Always_Rings_Twice_(1981_film)" title="The Postman Always Rings Twice (1981 film)">
         The Postman Always Rings Twice
        </a>
       </i>
       (1981),
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Untouchables_(film)" title="The Untouchables (film)">
         The Untouchables
        </a>
       </i>
       (1987),
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hoffa_(film)" title="Hoffa (film)">
         Hoffa
        </a>
       </i>
       (1992),
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wag_the_Dog" title="Wag the Dog">
         Wag the Dog
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Edge_(1997_film)" title="The Edge (1997 film)">
         The Edge
        </a>
       </i>
       (1997),
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ronin_(film)" title="Ronin (film)">
         Ronin
        </a>
       </i>
       (1998), and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Hannibal_(film)" title="Hannibal (film)">
         Hannibal
        </a>
       </i>
       (2001). Mamet himself wrote the screenplay for the
       <a href="/wiki/Glengarry_Glen_Ross_(film)" title="Glengarry Glen Ross (film)">
        1992 adaptation
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        Glengarry Glen Ross
       </i>
       , and wrote and directed the
       <a href="/wiki/Oleanna_(film)" title="Oleanna (film)">
        1994 adaptation
       </a>
       of his play
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Oleanna_(play)" title="Oleanna (play)">
         Oleanna
        </a>
       </i>
       (1992). He was the executive producer and frequent writer for the TV show
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Unit" title="The Unit">
         The Unit
        </a>
        .
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       Mamet's books include:
       <i>
        The Old Religion
       </i>
       (1997), a novel about the lynching of
       <a href="/wiki/Leo_Frank" title="Leo Frank">
        Leo Frank
       </a>
       ;
       <i>
        Five Cities of Refuge: Weekly Reflections on Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers and Deuteronomy
       </i>
       (2004), a
       <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">
        Torah
       </a>
       commentary with Rabbi
       <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Kushner" title="Lawrence Kushner">
        Lawrence Kushner
       </a>
       ;
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Wicked_Son" title="The Wicked Son">
         The Wicked Son
        </a>
       </i>
       (2006), a study of
       <a href="/wiki/Self-hating_Jew" title="Self-hating Jew">
        Jewish self-hatred
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">
        antisemitism
       </a>
       ;
       <i>
        Bambi vs. Godzilla
       </i>
       , a commentary on the movie business;
       <i>
        The Secret Knowledge: On the Dismantling of American Culture
       </i>
       (2011), a commentary on cultural and political issues; and
       <i>
        Three War Stories
       </i>
       (2013), a trio of novellas about the physical and psychological effects of war.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
        Early life
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Mamet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Mamet was born in 1947 in Chicago to Jewish parents, Lenore June (née Silver), a teacher, and Bernard Morris Mamet, a labor attorney.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One of his first jobs was as a busboy at Chicago's
       <a href="/wiki/London_House_(Chicago)" title="London House (Chicago)">
        London House
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/The_Second_City" title="The Second City">
        The Second City
       </a>
       . He also worked as an actor, editor for
       <a href="/wiki/Oui_(magazine)" title="Oui (magazine)">
        <i>
         Oui
        </i>
        magazine
       </a>
       and cab-driver.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was educated at the progressive
       <a href="/wiki/Francis_W._Parker_School_(Chicago)" title="Francis W. Parker School (Chicago)">
        Francis W. Parker School
       </a>
       and at
       <a href="/wiki/Goddard_College" title="Goddard College">
        Goddard College
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Plainfield,_Vermont" title="Plainfield, Vermont">
        Plainfield, Vermont
       </a>
       . At the Chicago Public Library Foundation 20th anniversary fundraiser in 2006, though, Mamet announced "My alma mater is the Chicago Public Library. I got what little educational foundation I got in the third-floor reading room, under the tutelage of a Coca-Cola sign".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       After a move to Chicago's North Side neighborhood, Mamet encountered theater director
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Sickinger" title="Robert Sickinger">
        Robert Sickinger
       </a>
       , and began to work occasionally at Sickinger's
       <a href="/wiki/Hull_House" title="Hull House">
        Hull House Theatre
       </a>
       . This represented the start of Mamet's lifelong involvement with the theater.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nadel2016_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nadel2016-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Career">
        Career
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Mamet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Theater">
        Theater
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Mamet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theater">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Mamet is a founding member of the
       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Theater_Company" title="Atlantic Theater Company">
        Atlantic Theater Company
       </a>
       ; he first gained acclaim for a trio of off-Broadway plays in 1976,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Duck_Variations" title="The Duck Variations">
         The Duck Variations
        </a>
        ,
       </i>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sexual_Perversity_in_Chicago" title="Sexual Perversity in Chicago">
         Sexual Perversity in Chicago
        </a>
        ,
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/American_Buffalo_(play)" title="American Buffalo (play)">
         American Buffalo
        </a>
        .
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filmmakers1_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-filmmakers1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was awarded the
       <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">
        Pulitzer Prize
       </a>
       in 1984 for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Glengarry_Glen_Ross" title="Glengarry Glen Ross">
         Glengarry Glen Ross
        </a>
        ,
       </i>
       which received its first Broadway revival in the summer of 2005. His play
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Race_(play)" title="Race (play)">
         Race
        </a>
       </i>
       , which opened on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadway_theater" title="Broadway theater">
        Broadway
       </a>
       on December 6, 2009 and featured
       <a href="/wiki/James_Spader" title="James Spader">
        James Spader
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/David_Alan_Grier" title="David Alan Grier">
        David Alan Grier
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kerry_Washington" title="Kerry Washington">
        Kerry Washington
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Thomas_(actor)" title="Richard Thomas (actor)">
        Richard Thomas
       </a>
       in the cast, received mixed reviews.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His play
       <i>
        The Anarchist
       </i>
       , starring
       <a href="/wiki/Patti_LuPone" title="Patti LuPone">
        Patti LuPone
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Debra_Winger" title="Debra Winger">
        Debra Winger
       </a>
       , in her Broadway debut, opened on Broadway on November 13, 2012 in previews and was scheduled to close on December 16, 2012.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His 2017 play
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Penitent_(play)" title="The Penitent (play)">
         The Penitent
        </a>
       </i>
       previewed off-Broadway on February 8, 2017.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2002, Mamet was inducted into the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Theatre_Hall_of_Fame" title="American Theatre Hall of Fame">
        American Theatre Hall of Fame
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Mamet later received the
       <a href="/wiki/PEN/Laura_Pels_International_Foundation_for_Theater_Award" title="PEN/Laura Pels International Foundation for Theater Award">
        PEN/Laura Pels International Foundation for Theater Award
       </a>
       for Grand Master of American Theater in 2010.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2017, Mamet released an online class for writers entitled
       <i>
        David Mamet teaches dramatic writing
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Mamet's first produced screenplay was the 1981 production of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Postman_Always_Rings_Twice_(1981_film)" title="The Postman Always Rings Twice (1981 film)">
         The Postman Always Rings Twice
        </a>
       </i>
       (directed by
       <a href="/wiki/Bob_Rafelson" title="Bob Rafelson">
        Bob Rafelson
       </a>
       ), based upon
       <a href="/wiki/James_M._Cain" title="James M. Cain">
        James M. Cain
       </a>
       's novel. He received an
       <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">
        Academy Award
       </a>
       nomination one year later for his first script,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Verdict" title="The Verdict">
         The Verdict
        </a>
       </i>
       , written in the late 1970s. He also wrote the screenplay for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Untouchables_(film)" title="The Untouchables (film)">
         The Untouchables
        </a>
        .
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1987, Mamet made his film directing debut with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/House_of_Games" title="House of Games">
         House of Games
        </a>
       </i>
       , starring his then-wife,
       <a href="/wiki/Lindsay_Crouse" title="Lindsay Crouse">
        Lindsay Crouse
       </a>
       , and a host of longtime stage associates. He uses friends as actors,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       especially in one early scene in the movie, which featured Vermont poker-playing friends. He is quoted as saying, "It was my first film as a director and I needed support, so I stacked the deck."
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Two of the four poker friends included in the film were fellow
       <a href="/wiki/Goddard_College" title="Goddard College">
        Goddard College
       </a>
       graduates Allen Soule and Bob Silverstein. Three of Mamet's own films,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/House_of_Games" title="House of Games">
         House of Games
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Spanish_Prisoner" title="The Spanish Prisoner">
         The Spanish Prisoner
        </a>
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heist_(film)" title="Heist (film)">
         Heist
        </a>
        ,
       </i>
       have involved the world of con artists.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mamet adapted
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Glengarry_Glen_Ross_(film)" title="Glengarry Glen Ross (film)">
         Glengarry Glen Ross
        </a>
       </i>
       for the cinema in 1992, writing an additional part (including the monologue "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coffee%27s_for_closers" title="Coffee's for closers">
        Coffee's for closers
       </a>
       ") for
       <a href="/wiki/Alec_Baldwin" title="Alec Baldwin">
        Alec Baldwin
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Mamet remains a writer and director, and has assembled an informal repertory company for his films, including Crouse,
       <a href="/wiki/William_H._Macy" title="William H. Macy">
        William H. Macy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Joe_Mantegna" title="Joe Mantegna">
        Joe Mantegna
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rebecca_Pidgeon" title="Rebecca Pidgeon">
        Rebecca Pidgeon
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Jay" title="Ricky Jay">
        Ricky Jay
       </a>
       , as well as some of the aforementioned poker associates. Mamet has funded his own films with payments he receives for credited and uncredited rewrites of typically big-budget films.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       For instance, Mamet did a rewrite of the script for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ronin_(film)" title="Ronin (film)">
         Ronin
        </a>
       </i>
       under the pseudonym “Richard Weisz” and turned in an early version of a script for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X_(1992_film)" title="Malcolm X (1992 film)">
         Malcolm X
        </a>
       </i>
       that director
       <a href="/wiki/Spike_Lee" title="Spike Lee">
        Spike Lee
       </a>
       rejected.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2000, Mamet directed a film version of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Beckett_on_Film#Catastrophe" title="Beckett on Film">
         Catastrophe
        </a>
        ,
       </i>
       a one-act play by
       <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Beckett" title="Samuel Beckett">
        Samuel Beckett
       </a>
       featuring
       <a href="/wiki/Harold_Pinter" title="Harold Pinter">
        Harold Pinter
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/John_Gielgud" title="John Gielgud">
        John Gielgud
       </a>
       (in his final screen performance). In 2008, he directed and wrote the
       <a href="/wiki/Mixed_martial_arts" title="Mixed martial arts">
        mixed martial arts
       </a>
       movie
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Redbelt" title="Redbelt">
         Redbelt
        </a>
        ,
       </i>
       about a martial arts instructor tricked into fighting in a professional bout. Mamet teamed up with his wife
       <a href="/wiki/Rebecca_Pidgeon" title="Rebecca Pidgeon">
        Rebecca Pidgeon
       </a>
       to adapt the novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Come_Back_to_Sorrento" title="Come Back to Sorrento">
         Come Back to Sorrento
        </a>
       </i>
       as a screenplay. The film was in development during 2010. He is also director of the TV film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Phil_Spector_(film)" title="Phil Spector (film)">
         Phil Spector
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <i>
        On Directing Film,
       </i>
       Mamet asserts that directors should focus on getting the point of a scene across, rather than simply following a protagonist, or adding visually beautiful or intriguing shots. Films should create order from disorder in search of the objective.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Books">
        Books
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Mamet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Books">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1990 Mamet published
       <i>
        The Hero Pony
       </i>
       , a 55-page collection of poetry. He has also published a series of short plays, monologues and four novels,
       <i>
        The Village
       </i>
       (1994),
       <i>
        The Old Religion
       </i>
       (1997),
       <i>
        Wilson: A Consideration of the Sources
       </i>
       (2000), and
       <i>
        Chicago
       </i>
       (2018). He has written several non-fiction texts, and children's stories, including "True and False: Heresy and Common Sense for the Actor"(1997). In 2004 he published a lauded version of the classical
       <a href="/wiki/Faust" title="Faust">
        Faust
       </a>
       story,
       <i>
        Faustus
       </i>
       , however, when the play was staged in
       <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">
        San Francisco
       </a>
       during the spring of 2004, it was not well received by critics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On May 1, 2010, Mamet released a graphic novel
       <i>
        The Trials of Roderick Spode (The Human Ant)
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       On June 2, 2011,
       <i>
        The Secret Knowledge: On the Dismantling of American Culture
       </i>
       , Mamet's book detailing his conversion from modern liberalism to "a reformed liberal" was released.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Mamet published
       <i>
        Three War Stories
       </i>
       , a collection of novellas, on November 11, 2013. In an interview with Newsmax TV, Mamet said he wanted to write about war, despite never having served. Moreover, the book allowed Mamet to free characters that had occupied his mind for years. On the subject of characters as a reason for writing, Mamet told the host, “You want to get these guys out of your head. You just want them to stop talking to you."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Television_and_radio">
        Television and radio
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

         ]
        </span>
       </span>

      <p>
       Mamet wrote the "Wasted Weekend" episode of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Hill_Street_Blues" title="Hill Street Blues">

        </a>
       </i>
       that aired in 1987. His then-wife,
       <a href="/wiki/Lindsay_Crouse" title="Lindsay Crouse">
        Lindsay Crouse
       </a>
       , appeared in numerous episodes (including that one) as Officer McBride. Mamet is also the creator, producer and frequent writer of the television series
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Unit" title="The Unit">
         The Unit
        </a>
       </i>
       , where he wrote a well-circulated
       <a class="external text" href="http://movieline.com/2010/03/23/david-mamets-memo-to-the-writers-of-the-unit/" rel="nofollow">
        memo
       </a>
       to the writing staff. He directed a third-season episode of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Shield" title="The Shield">
         The Shield
        </a>
       </i>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Shawn_Ryan" title="Shawn Ryan">
        Shawn Ryan
       </a>
       . In 2007, Mamet directed two television commercials for
       <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">
        Ford Motor Company
       </a>
       . The two 30-second ads featured the
       <a href="/wiki/Ford_Edge" title="Ford Edge">
        Ford Edge
       </a>
       and were filmed in Mamet's signature style of fast-paced dialogue and clear, simple imagery. Mamet's sister,
       <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Mamet" title="Lynn Mamet">
        Lynn
       </a>
       , is a producer and writer for television shows, such as
       <i>
        The Unit
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Law &amp; Order
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Mamet has contributed several dramas to
       <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio" title="BBC Radio">
        BBC Radio
       </a>
       through Jarvis &amp; Ayres Productions, including an adaptation of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Glengarry_Glen_Ross" title="Glengarry Glen Ross">
         Glengarry Glen Ross
        </a>
       </i>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_3" title="BBC Radio 3">
        BBC Radio 3
       </a>
       and new dramas for
       <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">
        BBC Radio 4
       </a>
       . The comedy
       <i>
        Keep Your Pantheon (or On the Whole I'd Rather Be in Mesopotamia)
       </i>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_media_and_political_views">
        Other media and political views
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Mamet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other media and political views">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Since May 2005 he has been a contributing blogger at
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" title="The Huffington Post">
         The Huffington Post
        </a>
       </i>
       , drawing satirical cartoons with themes including political strife in Israel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In a 2008 essay at
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">
         The Village Voice
        </a>
       </i>
       titled
       <i>
        Why I Am No Longer a 'Brain-Dead Liberal'
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       he revealed that he had gradually rejected
       <a href="/wiki/Political_correctness" title="Political correctness">
        political correctness
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Progressivism" title="Progressivism">
        progressivism
       </a>
       and embraced
       <a href="/wiki/Conservatism_in_the_United_States" title="Conservatism in the United States">
        conservatism
       </a>
       . Mamet has spoken in interviews of changes in his views,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       highlighting his agreement with
       <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">
        free market
       </a>
       theorists such as
       <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Hayek" title="Friedrich Hayek">
        Friedrich Hayek
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the historian
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Johnson_(writer)" title="Paul Johnson (writer)">
        Paul Johnson
       </a>
       , and economist
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Sowell" title="Thomas Sowell">
        Thomas Sowell
       </a>
       , whom Mamet called "one of our greatest minds".
      </p>
      <p>
       During promotion of a book, Mamet was criticized for claiming that the British people had "a taint of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-semitism" title="Anti-semitism">
        anti-semitism
       </a>
       ," claiming they "want to give [Israel] away."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the same interview, Mamet went on to say that "there are famous dramatists and novelists [in the UK] whose works are full of anti-Semitic filth," but that he could not specify to whom he was referring for fear of litigation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He is known for his pro-Israel positions; in his book
       <i>
        The Secret Knowledge
       </i>
       he claimed that "Israelis would like to live in peace within their borders; the Arabs would like to kill them all."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In November 2012 Mamet penned an article for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Jewish_Journal_of_Greater_Los_Angeles" title="The Jewish Journal of Greater Los Angeles">
         The Jewish Journal of Greater Los Angeles
        </a>
       </i>
       imploring fellow
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_Americans" title="Jewish Americans">
        Jewish Americans
       </a>
       to vote for
       <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">
        Republican
       </a>
       nominee
       <a href="/wiki/Mitt_Romney" title="Mitt Romney">
        Mitt Romney
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In an essay for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">
         Newsweek
        </a>
       </i>
       , published on 29 January 2013, Mamet argued against
       <a href="/wiki/Gun_control" title="Gun control">
        gun control
       </a>
       laws: "It was intended to guard us against this inevitable decay of government that the Constitution was written. Its purpose was and is not to enthrone a Government superior to an imperfect and confused electorate, but to protect us from such a government."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception_of_Mamet">
        Critical reception of Mamet
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Mamet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Critical reception of Mamet">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span id=".27Mamet_speak.27">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="'Mamet_speak'">
        'Mamet speak'
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Mamet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 'Mamet speak'">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Mamet's style of writing dialogue, marked by a cynical, street-smart edge, precisely crafted for effect, is so distinctive that it has come to be called
       <i>
        Mamet speak.
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Mamet has recognized an association of his edgy narrative style by noting his debt to
       <a href="/wiki/Harold_Pinter" title="Harold Pinter">
        Harold Pinter
       </a>
       , to whom he dedicated
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Glengarry_Glen_Ross" title="Glengarry Glen Ross">
         Glengarry Glen Ross
        </a>
       </i>
       . He often uses italics and quotation marks to highlight particular words and to draw attention to his characters' frequent manipulation and deceitful use of language. His characters frequently interrupt one another, their sentences trail off unfinished, and their dialogue overlaps. Moreover, certain expressions and figures of speech are deliberately misrepresented to show that the character is not paying close attention to every detail of his dialogue (e.g.,
       <i>
        or so forth
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        and so forth
       </i>
       ). Mamet himself has criticized his (and other writers') tendency to write "pretty" at the expense of sound, logical plots.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When asked how he developed his style for writing dialogue, Mamet said, "In my family, in the days prior to television, we liked to while away the evenings by making ourselves miserable, based solely on our ability to speak the language viciously. That's probably where my ability was honed."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       One instance of Mamet's dialogue style can be found in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Glengarry_Glen_Ross" title="Glengarry Glen Ross">
         Glengarry Glen Ross
        </a>
       </i>
       , in which two down-on-their-luck real estate salesmen are considering stealing from their employer's office.
       <a href="/wiki/Glengarry_Glen_Ross#Characters" title="Glengarry Glen Ross">
        George Aaronow
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Glengarry_Glen_Ross#Characters" title="Glengarry Glen Ross">
        Dave Moss
       </a>
       equivocate on the meaning of "talk" and "speak", turning language and meaning to deceptive purposes:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Moss
        </b>
        No. What do you mean? Have I talked to him about
        <i>
         this
        </i>
        [Pause]
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Aaronow
        </b>
        Yes. I mean are you actually
        <i>
         talking
        </i>
        about this, or are we just...
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Moss
        </b>
        No, we're just...
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Aaronow
        </b>
        We're just
        <i>
         "talking"
        </i>
        about it.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Moss
        </b>
        We're just
        <i>
         speaking
        </i>
        about it. [Pause] As an
        <i>
         idea.
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Aaronow
        </b>
        As an idea.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Moss
        </b>
        Yes.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Aaronow
        </b>
        We're not actually
        <i>
         talking
        </i>
        about it.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Moss
        </b>
        No.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Aaronow
        </b>
        Talking about it as a...
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Moss
        </b>
        <i>
         No.
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Aaronow
        </b>
        As a
        <i>
         robbery.
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Moss
        </b>
        As a "robbery?" No.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Mamet dedicated
       <i>
        Glengarry Glen Ross
       </i>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Harold_Pinter" title="Harold Pinter">
        Harold Pinter
       </a>
       , who was instrumental in its being first staged at the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Theatre" title="Royal National Theatre">
        Royal National Theatre
       </a>
       , (London) in 1983, and whom Mamet has acknowledged as an influence on its success, and on his other work.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NightWaves_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NightWaves-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mamet_and_gender_issues">
        Mamet and gender issues
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Mamet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mamet and gender issues">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Mamet's plays have frequently sparked debate and controversy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TG_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TG-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During a staging of
       <i>
        Oleanna
       </i>
       in 1992, in which a post-secondary student accuses her professor of sexual harassment, a critic reported that the play divided the audience by gender and recounted "couples emerged screaming at each other".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TG_32-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-TG-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Arthur Holmberg in his 2014 book
       <i>
        David Mamet and Male Friendship
       </i>
       , has reconsidered the gender issue in many of Mamet's plays throughout his career by asserting a prominent and recurrent reversed sexual orientation of portrayed male gender preferences.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">
        Personal life
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </a>

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Mamet and actress
       <a href="/wiki/Lindsay_Crouse" title="Lindsay Crouse">
        Lindsay Crouse
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Zosia_Mamet" title="Zosia Mamet">
        Zosia
       </a>
       . Willa is a professional photographer and Zosia is an actress. Mamet has been married to actress and singer-songwriter
       <a href="/wiki/Rebecca_Pidgeon" title="Rebecca Pidgeon">
        Rebecca Pidgeon
       </a>
       since 1991. They have two children,
       <a href="/wiki/Clara_Mamet" title="Clara Mamet">
        Clara
       </a>
       and Noah.
      </p>

   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Gaffer (filmmaking)
   </h1>

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        gaffer
       </b>
       in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_picture_industry" title="Motion picture industry">
        motion picture industry
       </a>
       and on a
       <a href="/wiki/Television_crew" title="Television crew">
        television crew
       </a>
       is the head
       <a href="/wiki/Electrician" title="Electrician">
        electrician
       </a>
       , responsible for the execution (and sometimes the design) of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lighting" title="Lighting">
        lighting
       </a>
       plan for a production. The term gaffer originally related to the moving of overhead equipment to control lighting levels using a
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gaff" title="wiktionary:gaff">
        gaff
       </a>
       . The gaffer's assistant is the
       <a href="/wiki/Best_boy" title="Best boy">
        best boy
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taub153_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Taub153-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sometimes the gaffer is credited as
       <a href="/wiki/Lighting_technician" title="Lighting technician">
        Chief Lighting Technician
       </a>
       (CLT).
      </p>
      <p>
       The term has been used for the chief electrician in films since the 1930s. The
       <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">
        Oxford English Dictionary
       </a>
       has a citation from 1936;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-OED-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a 1929 book on motion picture production also uses the term.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The gaffer is responsible for managing lighting, including associated resources such as labour, lighting instruments and electrical equipment under the direction of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Director_of_Photography" title="Director of Photography">
        Director of Photography
       </a>
       (the DP or DOP) or, in television, the Lighting Director (LD).
      </p>
      <p>
       The DP/LD is responsible for the overall lighting design, but delegates the implementation of the design to the gaffer and the
       <a href="/wiki/Key_grip" title="Key grip">
        key grip
       </a>
       . The key grip is the head
       <a href="/wiki/Grip_(job)" title="Grip (job)">
        grip
       </a>
       , in charge of the labour and non-electrical equipment used to support and modify the lighting. Grip equipment includes stands,
       <a href="/wiki/Flag_(lighting)" title="Flag (lighting)">
        flags
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Gobo_(lighting)" title="Gobo (lighting)">
        gobos
       </a>
       . The gaffer will usually have an assistant called a
       <a href="/wiki/Best_boy" title="Best boy">
        best boy
       </a>
       and, depending on the size of the job, crew members who are called "set lighting technicians" or "
       <a href="/wiki/Lighting_technician" title="Lighting technician">
        electricians
       </a>
       ", although not all of them are trained as
       <a href="/wiki/Electrician" title="Electrician">
        electricians
       </a>
       in the usual sense of the term.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although
       <a href="/wiki/Gaffer_tape" title="Gaffer tape">
        gaffer tape
       </a>
       is used within the film/TV Industry as a strong cloth-backed adhesive tape, many other types of tape are also used, such as paper tape,
       <a href="/wiki/Pressure-sensitive_tape" title="Pressure-sensitive tape">
        pressure-sensitive tape
       </a>
       (A.K.A. snot tape),
       <a href="/wiki/Electrical_tape" title="Electrical tape">
        electrical tape
       </a>
       , J-LAR, and cloth tape. Gaffer tape is typically utilized by set lighting technicians under the supervision of, and not directly by, a gaffer.
      </p>

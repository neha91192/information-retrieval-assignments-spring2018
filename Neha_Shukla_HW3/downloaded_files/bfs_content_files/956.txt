      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Casting_director">
        Casting director
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Television_crew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Casting director">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casting_Director" title="Casting Director">
        Casting Director
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        The casting director casts actors, and so is usually one of the first crew members on the project. In fact, during initial casting for a
        <a href="/wiki/Television_pilot" title="Television pilot">
         television pilot
        </a>
        , the
        <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">
         executive producer
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casting_director" title="Casting director">
         casting director
        </a>
        are often the only crew members.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Costume designer
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        The
        <b>
         costume designer
        </b>
        makes all the clothing and costumes worn by all the Actors on screen, as well as designing, planning, and organizing the construction of the garments down to the fabric, colours, and sizes. They greatly contribute to the appearance of the production, and set a particular mood, time, feeling, or genre. They alter the overall appearance of a project with their designs and constructions, including impacting on the style of the project, and how the audience interprets the show's characters.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Director">
        Director
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Television_crew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Director">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Television_director" title="Television director">
        Television director
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        A
        <b>
         television director
        </b>
        is usually responsible for directing the actors and other filmed aspects of a television production. The role differs from that of a
        <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">
         film director
        </a>
        because the major creative control usually belongs to the producer. In general, actors and other regular artists on a show are familiar enough with their roles that the director's input is confined to technical issues. The director is responsible for all creative aspects of a movie. The director typically helps hire the cast (and possibly crew). The Director helps decide on locations, and creates a shooting plan. During shooting, the director supervises the overall project, manages shots, and keeps the assignment on budget, and schedule. Though directors hold much power, they are second in command after the producer. The producer usually hires the director (unless the director is also the producer). Some directors produce their own
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">
         television programs
        </a>
        , and, with formal approval of the funding studio, enjoy a tighter grip on what makes the final cut than Directors usually have.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Associate Director
        </b>
        (
        <b>
         AD
        </b>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Assistant_director" title="Assistant director">
        Assistant director
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        An
        <b>
         associate director (AD)
        </b>
        in television production is usually responsible for floor directing in the studio and ensuring that the sets, props and technical equipment are safe, ready to use and positioned correctly before filming. Associate directors are also responsible for communications with the
        <a href="/wiki/Audience" title="Audience">
         audience
        </a>
        and any guests, for example ensuring they are seated in good time, and assisting the Director with production. In scripted
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_series" title="Television series">
         television series
        </a>
        , an associate director occasionally serves as an
        <a href="/wiki/Episode" title="Episode">
         episode
        </a>
        's director, in which case someone else substitutes for the AD. Until the mid-2000s in the United States, associate directors were usually credited as
        <i>
         technical coordinators
        </i>
        , for most
        <a href="/wiki/Sitcom" title="Sitcom">
         sitcoms
        </a>
        were shot on film. Drama programs don't usually use ADs.
       </dd>
      </dl>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Make-up_artist">
        Make-up artist
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Television_crew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Make-up artist">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Make-up_artist" title="Make-up artist">
        Make-up artist
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        A professional
        <b>
         make-up artist
        </b>
        is usually a
        <a href="/wiki/Cosmetology" title="Cosmetology">
         cosmetology
        </a>
        beautician, and applies makeup to anyone who appears on screen. They concentrate on the area above the chest, the face, the top of the head, the fingers, hands, arms, and elbows. Their role is to manipulate the actor's on-screen appearance to make them look younger, older, larger, etc.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Body_makeup_artist&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Body makeup artist (page does not exist)">
         Body makeup artists
        </a>
        concentrate on the body rather than the head. Make-up itself is substances to enhance the beauty of the human body, but can also change the appearance, disguise, or costume someone. Make-up artists,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hair_stylist" title="Hair stylist">
         hair stylists
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Costume_designer" title="Costume designer">
         costume designers
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Theatrical_technician#Wardrobe" title="Theatrical technician">
         dress technicians
        </a>
        combine their efforts to transform actors into characters,
        <a href="/wiki/Presenter" title="Presenter">
         presenters
        </a>
        , etc.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Production_designer">
        Production designer
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Television_crew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Production designer">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Production_designer" title="Production designer">
        Production designer
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        The
        <b>
         production designer
        </b>
        is responsible for the production's visual appearance.They design, plan, organize, and arrange set design, equipment availability, and control a production's on-screen appearance. The production designer is often called the
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Set_designer" title="Set designer">
          set designer
         </a>
        </i>
        , or
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scenic_designer" title="Scenic designer">
          scenic designer
         </a>
        </i>
        . They are trained professionals, often with
        <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Fine_Arts" title="Master of Fine Arts">
         Master of Fine Arts
        </a>
        (MFA) degrees in scenic design. The set designer collaborates with the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theater_director" title="Theater director">
         theater director
        </a>
        to create an environment for the production—and communicates details of this environment to the
        <a href="/wiki/Technical_director" title="Technical director">
         technical director
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Charge_scenic_artist" title="Charge scenic artist">
         charge scenic artist
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Property_master" title="Property master">
         property master
        </a>
        . Scenic designers create drawings and scale models of the scenery. The set designer also takes instructions from the
        <a href="/wiki/Art_director" title="Art director">
         art director
        </a>
        to create the appearance of the stage, and design its technical assembly. The art director, who may also be the
        <a href="/wiki/Production_designer" title="Production designer">
         production designer
        </a>
        , plans and oversees the formation of settings for a project. They must be well versed in art and design styles, including architecture and interior design. They also work with the
        <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">
         Cinematographer
        </a>
        to accomplish the precise appearance for the project.
       </dd>
      </dl>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Television_producer">
        Television producer
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Television_crew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Television producer">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Television_producer" title="Television producer">
        Television producer
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        In the entertainment industry, a
        <b>
         television producer
        </b>
        (compare to
        <a href="/wiki/Film_producer" title="Film producer">
         film producer
        </a>
        ) is generally in charge of, or helps coordinate, the financial, legal, administrative, technological, and artistic aspects of a production. In television, a
        <b>
         television producer
        </b>
        can be given one of the following titles:
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Associate producer
        </b>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        The
        <b>
         associate producer
        </b>
        performs limited producing functions under the authority of a producer; often in charge of the day-to-day running of a production. Usually the producer's head assistant, although the task can differ. They frequently form a connection between everyone involved in shooting (the
        <a href="/wiki/Production_team" title="Production team">
         production team
        </a>
        ) and the people involved after filming to finalize the production, and get it publicized (the
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">
          post-production
         </a>
         team
        </i>
        ). Occasionally, credit for this role goes to the product's financial backer, or the person who originally brought the assignment to the producer.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Assistant Producer
        </b>
        (
        <b>
         AP
        </b>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        In the UK),
        <b>
         assistant producer
        </b>
        is the closest role to that of a
        <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">
         film director
        </a>
        . An assistant producer often doubles as an experienced
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Researcher" title="Researcher">
         researcher
        </a>
        , and takes direct charge of the creative content and action within a programme. The title of
        <a href="/wiki/Television_director" title="Television director">
         television director
        </a>
        is usually reserved for
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dramatic_programming" title="Dramatic programming">
         dramatic programming
        </a>
        , productions and most similar to films, or those who control a multi-camera set up from the
        <i>
         gallery
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Co-producer
        </b>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Typically performs producing functions in tandem with one or more other
        <b>
         co-producers
        </b>
        (working as a team, rather than separately on different aspects of the production).
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Coordinating producer
        </b>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        The coordinating producer coordinates the work of two or more producers working separately on one or more productions.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Executive producer
        </b>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">
        Executive producer
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        The executive producer supervises one or more producers in all aspects of their work, and sometimes initiated the production. They are usually the ultimate authority on creative and business aspects of the production. If the title is designated correctly, the executive producer arranges the project's financial backing and maintains a sound
        <a href="/wiki/Production_budget" title="Production budget">
         production budget
        </a>
        . On scripted programs, the
        <a href="/wiki/Showrunner" title="Showrunner">
         showrunner
        </a>
        receives an executive producer credit.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Line producer
        </b>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Line_producer" title="Line producer">
        Line producer
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        A line producer supervises physical aspects of the production (not the creative aspects), including personnel, technology, budget, and scheduling. The line producer oversees the budget. This involves operating costs such as salaries, production costs, and everyday equipment rental costs. The Line Producer works with the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Production_manager" title="Production manager">
         Production manager
        </a>
        on costs and expenditure.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Segment producer
        </b>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Produces one or more components of a multipart production.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Supervising producer
        </b>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Supervises one or more producers in some or all aspects of their work; usually works under the authority of an executive producer.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Additionally, more senior members of a television show's writing staff are credited as producers, with the specific title dependent upon the seniority and rate of pay for the writer. For example, a writer credited as a "co-executive producer" will typically receive a higher salary and be considered more senior than a writer credited as a "producer", who will in turn be higher "ranking" than a writer credited as "co-producer."

      <h3>
       <span id="Grip_.28job.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grip_(job)">
        Grip (job)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Television_crew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Grip (job)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Grip_(job)" title="Grip (job)">
        Grip (job)
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        In the U.S. and Canada, grips are lighting and rigging technicians in the film and video industries. They constitute their own department on a film set and are directed by a key grip. Grips have two main functions. The first is to work closely with the camera department to provide camera support, especially if the camera is mounted to a dolly, crane, or in an unusual position, such as the top of a ladder. Some grips may specialize in operating camera dollies or camera cranes. The second main function of grips is to work closely with the electrical department to create lighting set-ups necessary for a shot under the direction of the director of photography.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Key grip
        </b>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Key_grip" title="Key grip">
        Key grip
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        The '
        <i>
         key grip
        </i>
        is the head grip. Grips affect shadow effects with lights, and occasionally maneuver camera cranes, dollies, and platforms under direction from the Cinematographer. The term grip is used in slightly different ways in American and British or Australian film making. In the British and Australian film industries, a grip mounts and supports cameras, which can include anything beyond a basic tripod. Lighting in British and Australian film-making is headed by the gaffer, who is also part of the camera department. Grips can also be the people that do the laborious work on sets. These type of grips push, pull, roll, and lift various pieces of equipment under direction from a television director, television producer, or set designer.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Dolly grip
        </b>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Dolly_grip" title="Dolly grip">
        Dolly grip
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Cinematography" title="Cinematography">
         cinematography
        </a>
        , the
        <b>
         dolly grip
        </b>
        places and moves the dolly track where required, and then pushes and pulls the dolly along that track during filming. A dolly grip works closely with the camera crew to perfect these complex movements through rehearsals. For moving shots, dolly grips may also push the wheeled platform that holds the microphone and
        <a href="/wiki/Boom_operator_(media)" title="Boom operator (media)">
         Boom Operator
        </a>
        . The dolly is a cart that the tripod and camera (and occasionally the camera crew) rest on. It transports the camera without bumps and visual interruptions throughout a shot. It is commonly used to follow beside an actor to give the audience the sense of walking with the actor, or as the actor.
       </dd>
      </dl>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Editor">
        Editor
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Television_crew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Editor">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Film_editing" title="Film editing">
        Film editing
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Video_editing" title="Video editing">
        Video editing
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Editor_in_linear_suite.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="252" data-file-width="550" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Editor_in_linear_suite.png/220px-Editor_in_linear_suite.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Editor_in_linear_suite.png/330px-Editor_in_linear_suite.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Editor_in_linear_suite.png/440px-Editor_in_linear_suite.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Editor_in_linear_suite.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Editor in linear suite
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        The editor works in tandem with the director to edit raw footage into a finished work. The director has ultimate accountability for editing choices, but often the editor contributes substantially to the creative decisions concerned in piecing together a finalized product. Often, the editor commences their role whilst filming is still in process, by compiling initial takes of footage. It is an extremely long process to edit a television show, demonstrating the importance, and significance editing has on a production. Gradually more editors work on
        <a href="/wiki/Non-linear_editing_system" title="Non-linear editing system">
         non-linear editing systems
        </a>
        (NLE), limiting physical touching of the actual film, decreasing film corruption due to touch.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        The Editor follows the
        <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">
         screenplay
        </a>
        as the guide for establishing the structure of the story, and assembles the various shots and takes for greater, clearer artistic effect. There are several editing stages. In the first stage, the editor is supervised by the director, who conveys their vision to the Editor. Therefore, this first
        <a href="/wiki/Rough_cut" title="Rough cut">
         rough cut
        </a>
        is created during
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Offline_editing" title="Offline editing">
          offline editing
         </a>
        </i>
        . After the first stage, the following cuts may be supervised by one or more
        <a href="/wiki/Television_producer" title="Television producer">
         television producers
        </a>
        , who represent the
        <a href="/wiki/Production_company" title="Production company">
         production company
        </a>
        and its investors. Consequently, the final cut is the one that most closely represents what the studio wants from the film, and not necessarily what the director wants during
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Online_editing" title="Online editing">
          online editing
         </a>
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>

         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Television_crew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Publicist">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Publicist" title="Publicist">
        Publicist
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        A
        <b>
         publicist
        </b>
        , or
        <b>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advertiser" title="Advertiser">
          advertiser
         </a>
        </b>
        has the task of raising public awareness of a production, and ultimately increase viewers and sales of it and its merchandise. The publicist's main task is to stimulate demand for a product through advertising and promotion. Advertisers use several recognizable techniques in order to better convince the public to buy a product. These may include:
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Repetition:
        </b>
        Some advertisers concentrate on making sure their product is widely recognized. To that end, they simply attempt to make the name remembered through repetition.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Bandwagon:
        </b>
        By implying that the product is widely viewed, advertisers hope to convince potential buyers to "get on the bandwagon."
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Testimonials:
        </b>
        Advertisers often attempt to promote the superior worth of their product through the testimony of ordinary users, experts, or both. For example, using film critics or media personalities. This approach often involves an appeal to authority such as a doctor of media science.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Pressure:
        </b>
        By attempting to make people choose quickly and without long consideration, some advertisers hope to make rapid sales, and a sense of urgency to watch or buy a product.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Association:
        </b>
        Advertisers often attempt to associate their product with desirable things, in order to make it seem equally desirable. The use of attractive models, picturesque landscapes, and other similar imagery is common. "Buzzwords" with desired associations are also used.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Imagery:
        </b>
        Using
        <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">
         advertising
        </a>
        slogans, logos, or a common image increases familiarity, trust, personality of a production, and the ability for the show to be remembered.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The publicist ensures the media are well aware of a project by distributing the show as a trial run or sneak preview. They issue press releases and arrange interviews with cast and crew members. They may arrange public visits to the set, or distribute media kits that contain pictures, posters, clips, shorts, trailers, and descriptions of the show.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sound_editor">
        Sound editor
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Television_crew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Sound editor">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_editor_(filmmaking)" title="Sound editor (filmmaking)">
        Sound editor (filmmaking)
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_engineering" title="Audio engineering">
        Audio engineering
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        In television, the
        <b>
         sound editor
        </b>
        deals with audio editing, adjusting and fixing of the
        <a href="/wiki/Soundtrack" title="Soundtrack">
         soundtrack
        </a>
        . They usually have a major decision-making and creative role when it comes to sound and audio. A sound editor also decides what
        <a href="/wiki/Sound_effect" title="Sound effect">
         sound effects
        </a>
        to use and what effects to achieve from the sound effects, edits and makes new sounds using filters and combining sounds, shaping sound with volume curves, and equalizing. A sound editor places the
        <b>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foley_artist" title="Foley artist">
          Foley artist
         </a>
         's
        </b>
        sounds into the sound track. Often, a sound editor uses a sound effects library, either self-compiled, bought or both.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Title_sequence_designer">
        Title sequence designer
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Television_crew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Title sequence designer">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_designer" title="Broadcast designer">
        Broadcast designer
       </a>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        A
        <a href="/wiki/Title_sequence" title="Title sequence">
         title sequence
        </a>
        , in a television program, appears at the beginning of the show and displays the show name and credits, usually including actors, producers and, directors. A montage of selected images and a theme song are often included to suggest the essential tone of the series. A title sequence is essential in preparing the audience for the following program, and gives them a sense of familiarity that makes them trust, and feel comfortable with the film. It is up to the
        <b>
         title sequence designer
        </b>
        to achieve this very goal, and make it catchy, entertaining.
       </dd>
      </dl>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Specialist_editors">
        Specialist editors
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="ADR_editor">
        ADR editor
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Television_crew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: ADR editor">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <a href="/wiki/Dubbing_(filmmaking)" title="Dubbing (filmmaking)">
         Automatic dialogue replacement
        </a>
        (ADR) is the process of replacing dialogue that was recorded incorrectly during filming, with the actors voices recorded and put into place during editing. The
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ADR_editor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ADR editor (page does not exist)">
         ADR editor
        </a>
        oversees the procedure and takes the corrupted dialogue, and replaces it with newly recorded lines to the actor's mouth on film to make it lip sync correctly.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span id="Bluescreen_director.2Fmatte_Artist">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Bluescreen_director/matte_Artist">
        Bluescreen director/matte Artist
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Television_crew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: Bluescreen director/matte Artist">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Bluescreen is the film technique of shooting foreground action against a blue background, which is then replaced by a separately shot
        <i>
         background plate
        </i>
        scene by either optical effects or digital composting. This process is directed and co-ordinated by the
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bluescreen_Director&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bluescreen Director (page does not exist)">
         Bluescreen Director
        </a>
        . The matte artist is a part of the special effects department who assists in making scenery and locations that do not exist. They assemble backgrounds using traditional techniques or computers that mix with the footage filmed to create a false set. Both are fairly alike, but
        <a href="/wiki/Chroma_key" title="Chroma key">
         bluescreen
        </a>
        technology is more modern and more widely used.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_effects_artist">
        Visual effects artist
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Television_crew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: Visual effects artist">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        visual effects artist (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VFX" title="VFX">
         VFX
        </a>
        ) are used in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_production" title="Television production">
         television productions
        </a>
        to create effects that cannot be achieved by normal means, such as depicting travel to other star systems. They are also used when creating the effect by normal means is prohibitively expensive, such as an enormous explosion. They are also used to enhance previously filmed elements, by adding, removing or enhancing objects within the scene. The
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Visual_effects_artist&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Visual effects artist (page does not exist)">
         visual effects artist
        </a>
        creates these effects, and developes them with the help of the
        <a href="/wiki/Visual_effects_supervisor" title="Visual effects supervisor">
         visual effects supervisor
        </a>
        . The task of the Visual effects artist differs frequently, and can range from combining extensive over-the-top
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_effects" title="Special effects">
         special effects
        </a>
        with computer
        <a href="/wiki/Visual_effects" title="Visual effects">
         visual effects
        </a>
        and CGI animation.
       </dd>
      </dl>

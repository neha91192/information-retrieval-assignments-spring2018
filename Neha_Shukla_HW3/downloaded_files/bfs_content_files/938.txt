      <p>
       A
       <b>
        production company
       </b>
       or a
       <b>
        production house
       </b>
       provides the physical basis for works in the realms of the
       <a href="/wiki/Performing_arts" title="Performing arts">
        performing arts
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_media_art" title="New media art">
        new media art
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        film
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">
        radio
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">
        video
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tasks_and_functions">
        Tasks and functions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Production_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tasks and functions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The production company may be directly responsible for fundraising for the production or may accomplish this through a
       <a href="/wiki/Parent_company" title="Parent company">
        parent company
       </a>
       , partner, or private investor. It handles budgeting, scheduling, scripting, the
       <a href="/wiki/Casting_(performing_arts)" title="Casting (performing arts)">
        supply with talent and resources
       </a>
       , the organization of staff, the production itself,
       <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">
        post-production
       </a>
       , distribution, and
       <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">
        marketing
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Production companies are often either owned or under contract with a
       <a href="/wiki/Media_conglomerate" title="Media conglomerate">
        media conglomerate
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Film_studio" title="Film studio">
        film studio
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Entertainment" title="Entertainment">
        entertainment
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company">
        company
       </a>
       , or Motion Picture Company, who act as the production company's partner or
       <a href="/wiki/Parent_company" title="Parent company">
        parent company
       </a>
       . This has become known as the "studio system". They can also be
       <a href="/wiki/Mainstream" title="Mainstream">
        mainstream
       </a>
       , independent (see
       <a href="/wiki/Lucasfilm" title="Lucasfilm">
        Lucasfilm
       </a>
       ), or completely independent (see
       <a href="/wiki/Lionsgate" title="Lionsgate">
        Lionsgate
       </a>
       ). In the case of television, a production company would serve under a
       <a href="/wiki/Television_network" title="Television network">
        television network
       </a>
       . Production companies can work together in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Co-production_(filmmaking)" title="Co-production (filmmaking)">
        co-productions
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Type">
        Type
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Production_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Type">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Entertainment companies operate as mini
       <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(company)" title="Conglomerate (company)">
        conglomerates
       </a>
       , operating many divisions or subsidiaries in many different industries.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Entertainment" title="Warner Bros. Entertainment">
        Warner Bros. Entertainment
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lionsgate_Entertainment" title="Lionsgate Entertainment">
        Lionsgate Entertainment
       </a>
       are two companies with this corporate structure. It allows for a single company to maintain control over seemingly unrelated companies that fall within the ranges of entertainment, which increases and centralises the revenue into one company (example: a film production company, TV production company, video game company, and comic book company are all owned by a single entertainment company). A motion picture company, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">
        Paramount Pictures
       </a>
       , specializing "only" in motion pictures is only connected with its other counterpart industries through its parent company. Instead of performing a corporate reorganization, many motion picture companies often have sister companies they collaborate with in other industries that are subsidiaries owned by their parent company and is often not involved in the making of products that are not motion picture related. A production company can either operate as an affiliate (under a contract) or as a subsidiary for an entertainment company, motion picture company, television network, or all, and are generally smaller than the company they are partnered with.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Book_to_film_unit">
        Book to film unit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Production_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Book to film unit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A book to film unit is a unit of a book publishing company for the purposes of getting books that they published
       <a href="/wiki/Film_adaptation" title="Film adaptation">
        adapted into film
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Production_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Films have been using books as a prime source for films for years. In 2012, six out of the nine best picture Oscar nominees were originally books. Previously, publishers did not develop their books into movie nor receive any of the profits. Neither Scholastic or Little Brown, get any box office revenue from the
       <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">
        Harry Potter
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Saga_(film_series)" title="The Twilight Saga (film series)">
        Twilight
       </a>
       movies just through book sales. As the publishers faced decreasing revenue due to increased competition from self-published e-books, or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">
        Amazon.com
       </a>
       moving into the publishing field, publishers have started to enter the film and TV production business to boost their net income
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thr_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-thr-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with Amazon attempting to compete there too. More screenwriters are turning to book publishers to get their screenplay published as a book, so as to have a boost in their attempt to have the screenplay turned into a movie, given that it is a known product after the book.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Production_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Publisher
       <a href="/wiki/Simon_%26_Schuster" title="Simon &amp; Schuster">
        Simon &amp; Schuster
       </a>
       has been owned by media companies lately by
       <a href="/wiki/CBS_Corporation" title="CBS Corporation">
        CBS Corporation
       </a>
       while the publisher is not involved with film and TV, S&amp;S shares with CBS for possible film or TV deals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Alloy_Entertainment" title="Alloy Entertainment">
        Alloy Entertainment
       </a>
       while not a unit of a publisher started using a book packaging to film model of film and TV development by developing the property in-house, hire authors for the books and films, so as to own the property. Random House was the first big six book publisher to establish a book to film unit,
       <a href="/wiki/Random_House_Studio" title="Random House Studio">
        Random House Films
       </a>
       , in 2005 with a
       <a href="/wiki/Focus_Features" title="Focus Features">
        Focus Features
       </a>
       deal under a development and co-finance plan.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thr_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-thr-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Dunne_Books#Macmillan_Entertainment" title="Thomas Dunne Books">
        Macmillan Films
       </a>
       was launched by
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Dunne_Books" title="Thomas Dunne Books">
        Thomas Dunne Books
       </a>
       in October 2010 under the packaging model similar to Alloy while also moving to get film rights from Dunne's published author.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dl0_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-dl0-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Also that year, Random House changed their strategy to film development and packaging only.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thr_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-thr-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Cond%C3%A9_Nast_Entertainment" title="Condé Nast Entertainment">
        Condé Nast Entertainment
       </a>
       was started by Magazine publisher
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conde_Nast" title="Conde Nast">
        Conde Nast
       </a>
       in October 2012.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thr_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-thr-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2013, Macmillan Films became Macmillan Entertainment with an expansion to look at other divisions' book for possible films.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">

       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_and_profit">
        Operation and profit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Production_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Operation and profit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A production company is usually run by a producer or director, but can also be run by a career executive. In entertainment, a production company relies highly on
       <a href="/wiki/Celebrity" title="Celebrity">
        talent
       </a>
       or a well known entertainment franchise to raise the value of an entertainment project and draw out larger audiences. This gives the entertainment industry a democratized power structure to ensure that both the companies and talent receive their fair share of pay and recognition for work done on a production. The entertainment industry is centered on funding (studio investments, private investments, or self investments either from earnings from previous productions or personal wealth), projects (scripts and entertainment franchises), and talent (
       <a href="/wiki/Actor" title="Actor">
        actors
       </a>
       , directors,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Screenwriters" title="Screenwriters">
        screenwriters
       </a>
       , and crew). Production companies are judged and ranked based on the amount of funding it has, as well the productions it has completed or been involved with in the past. If a production company has major funding either through earnings, studio investors, or private investors, and has done or been involved with big budget productions in the past, it is considered to be a major production company. These companies often work with well-known and expensive talent. If a production company does not have much funding and has not done or been involved with any big budget productions, it is considered to be a small production company. These companies often work with up and coming talent. Small production companies will either grow to become a major production company, a
       <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">
        subsidiary
       </a>
       completely owned by another company, or remain small. The success of an entertainment production company is centered on the projects it produces, the talent it can acquire, and the performance of the talent.
       <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">
        Marketing
       </a>
       is also a major factor. All films, as a tradition, are often marketed around the image and the performance of the actors; with an option of marketing the behind the scenes crew such as the directors and screenwriters. Unlike many other businesses, a production company does not rely on an ongoing
       <a href="/wiki/Revenue_stream" title="Revenue stream">
        revenue stream
       </a>
       , they operate on ongoing investments; this often requires a parent company or a private corporate investment entity (see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legendary_Pictures" title="Legendary Pictures">
        Legendary Pictures
       </a>
       ). Their only source of profit come from the productions they produce. Because entertainment and media are currently in "high demand", a production company can profit if its
       <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">
        management
       </a>
       is capable of using its resources to supply good quality products and services to the public. Many entertainment production companies
       <a href="/wiki/Brand" title="Brand">
        brand
       </a>
       their entertainment projects. An entertainment project can either become a "one time hit" or an ongoing "entertainment
       <a href="/wiki/Media_franchise" title="Media franchise">
        franchise
       </a>
       " that can be continued, remade,
       <a href="/wiki/Reboot_(fiction)" title="Reboot (fiction)">
        rebooted
       </a>
       , or expanded into other sister industries; such as the video game industry (see
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">
         Star Wars
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">
         Star Trek
        </a>
       </i>
       ). Entertainment projects can be either an original or an adaptation from another industry.
      </p>
      <p>
       In rare occasional cases, a few troubled major studios would also shed their distribution and/or marketing staffs, mainly due to reduced resources, and resort to co-investing and/or co-distributing film projects with larger studios, operating as virtual, production-only movie studios. Notable examples include legendary studio
       <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">
        Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer
       </a>
       , which, after many years of box office flops (mostly with low budgets), bad management and distribution, and bankruptcy, was restructured at the end of 2010 under new management and currently struck deals with some of the Big Six studios (most notably the
       <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Motion_Picture_Group" title="Sony Pictures Motion Picture Group">
        Sony Pictures Motion Picture Group
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">
        Warner Bros.
       </a>
       );
       <a href="/wiki/Miramax" title="Miramax">
        Miramax
       </a>
       , which was downsized by former owner
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney" title="Disney">
        Disney
       </a>
       into a smaller division after the Weinstein brothers' 2005 divestment and, after 17 years under Disney ownership, was sold to a group of investors at the end of 2010 who eventually struck deals to co-finance the studio's projects with other independent companies; and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DreamWorks_Pictures" title="DreamWorks Pictures">
        DreamWorks Pictures
       </a>
       , the independently-run live action studio, which currently releases many projects through
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Pictures" title="Universal Pictures">
        Universal Pictures
       </a>
       and formerly released their projects through Disney after spinning off from
       <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">
        Paramount Pictures
       </a>
       (which purchased the studio in 2006 and stripped it of its independent distributor status) in 2008.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Staffing.2C_funds.2C_and_equipment">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Staffing,_funds,_and_equipment">
        Staffing, funds, and equipment
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Production_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Staffing, funds, and equipment">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Because a production company is only operational when a production is being produced and most of the talent and crew are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freelance" title="Freelance">
        freelancers
       </a>
       , many production companies are only required to hire
       <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">
        management
       </a>
       staff that helps to oversee the company's daily activities. In some cases, a production company can be run by only a handful of people. The company's funds are mainly committed towards employing talent, crew, and acquiring new updated production equipment on a regular basis. Many productions often require at least one to two cameras and lighting equipment for on location shooting. Production equipment is either leased or purchased from another production company or directly from the manufacturer. In the entertainment industry, in order to secure experienced professional talent and crew, production companies often become a signatory company to that talent or crew members "guild". By becoming a signatory company, it agrees to abide by the guild regulations. All big budget guild productions are exclusive to guild members and non guild members are not allowed to participate in these productions unless authorized by the guild. Productions with smaller budgets are allowed to use both guild talent and talent from the public. The majority of the talent and crew working in the entertainment industry are members of their professions guild. Most productions in the entertainment industry are guild productions.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
        Production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Production_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A production company is responsible for the development and filming of a specific production or media broadcast. In entertainment, the production process begins with the development of a specific project. Once a final script has been produced by the screenwriters, the production enters into the
       <a href="/wiki/Pre-production" title="Pre-production">
        pre-production
       </a>
       phase, most productions never reach this phase for financing or talent reasons. In pre-production, the actors are signed on and prepared for their roles, crew is signed on, shooting locations are found, sets are built or acquired, and the proper shooting permits are acquired for on location shooting. Actors and crew are hand picked by the producer, director, and casting director, who often use collaborators or referenced personnel to prevent untrusted or unwelcomed people from gaining access to a specific production and compromising the entire production through leaks. Once a production enters into principal photography, it begins filming. Productions are almost never cancelled once they reach this phase. Codenames are often used on bigger productions during filming to conceal the production's shooting locations for both privacy and safety reasons. In many cases, the director, producers, and the leading actors are often the only people with access to a full or majority of a single script. Supporting actors, background actors, and crew often never receive a full copy of a specific script to prevent leaks. Productions are often shot in secured studios, with limited to no public access, but they are also shot on location on secured sets or locations. Due to the exposure, when shooting in public locations, major productions often employ security to ensure the protection of the talent and crew working on a specific production. After filming is completed, the production enters into post production, which is handled by a post production company and overseen by the production company. The editing, musical score, visual effects, re-recording of the dialog, and sound effects are "mixed" to create the final film, which is then screened at the final screening. Marketing is also launched during this phase, such as the release of trailers and posters. Once a final film has been approved, the film is taken over by the distributors, who then release the film.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_details">
        Other details
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Production_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other details">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       For legal reasons, it is common within the entertainment industry for production companies not to accept unsolicited materials from any other company, talent, or the general public. It is also common for
       <a href="/wiki/Filmmaking" title="Filmmaking">
        filmmakers
       </a>
       or producers to become
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entrepreneur" title="Entrepreneur">
        entrepreneurs
       </a>
       and open their own production companies so that they can have more control over their careers and pay, while acting as an "in-house" creative and business driving force for their company but continuing to freelance as an artist for other companies, if desired.
      </p>

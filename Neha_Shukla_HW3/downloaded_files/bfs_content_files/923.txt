    Screenwriting
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Screenwriting
       </b>
       , also called
       <b>
        scriptwriting
       </b>
       , is the art and craft of writing
       <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">
        scripts
       </a>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">
        mass media
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Feature_film" title="Feature film">
        feature films
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_production" title="Television production">
        television productions
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">
        video games
       </a>
       . It is frequently a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freelance" title="Freelance">
        freelance
       </a>
       profession.
      </p>
      <p>
       Screenwriters are responsible for researching the story, developing the narrative, writing the screenplay and delivering it, in the required format, to development executives. Screenwriters therefore have great influence over the creative direction and emotional impact of the screenplay and, arguably, of the finished film. Screenwriters either pitch original ideas to producers, in the hope that they will be optioned or sold; or are commissioned by a producer to create a screenplay from a concept, true story, existing screen work or literary work, such as a novel, poem, play, comic book or short story.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">
        Types
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The act of screenwriting takes many forms across the entertainment industry. Often, multiple writers work on the same script at different stages of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Script_development" title="Script development">
        development
       </a>
       with different tasks. Over the course of a successful career, a screenwriter might be hired to write in a wide variety of roles.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some of the most common forms of screenwriting jobs include:
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spec_script_writing">
        Spec script writing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spec script writing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Spec_script" title="Spec script">
        Spec scripts
       </a>
       are feature film or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">
        television show
       </a>
       scripts written on speculation of sale, without the commission of a
       <a href="/wiki/Film_studio" title="Film studio">
        film studio
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Production_company" title="Production company">
        production company
       </a>
       or TV network. The content is usually invented solely by the screenwriter, though spec screenplays can also be based on established works or real people and events. The spec script is a Hollywood sales tool. The vast majority of scripts written each year are spec scripts, but only a small percentage make it to the screen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A spec script is usually a wholly original work, but can also be an
       <a href="/wiki/Film_adaptation" title="Film adaptation">
        adaptation
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In television writing, a spec script is a sample teleplay written to demonstrate the writer's knowledge of a show and ability to imitate its style and conventions. It is submitted to the show's producers in hopes of being hired to write future episodes of the show. Budding screenwriters attempting to break into the business generally begin by writing one or more spec scripts.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although writing spec scripts is part of any writer's career, the
       <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America" title="Writers Guild of America">
        Writers Guild of America
       </a>
       forbids members to write "on speculation". The distinction is that a "spec script" is written as a sample by the writer on his or her own; what is forbidden is writing a script for a specific producer without a contract. In addition to writing a script on speculation, it is generally not advised to write camera angles or other directional terminology, as these are likely to be ignored. A director may write up a shooting script himself or herself, a script that guides the team in what to do in order to carry out the director's vision of how the script should look. The director may ask the original writer to co-write it with him or her, or to rewrite a script that satisfies both the director and producer of the film/TV show.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spec writing is also unique in that the writer must pitch the idea to producers. In order to sell the script, it must have a killer title, good writing, and a great logline. A logline is one sentence that lays out what the movie is about. A well written logline will convey the tone of the film, introduce the main character, and touch on the primary conflict. Usually the logline and title work in tandem to draw people in, and it is highly suggested to incorporate irony into them when possible. These things, along with nice, clean writing will hugely impact whether or not a producer picks up the spec script.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Commissioned_screenplay">
        Commissioned screenplay
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commissioned screenplay">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A commissioned screenplay is written by a hired writer. The concept is usually developed long before the screenwriter is brought on, and often has multiple writers work on it before the script is given a
       <a href="/wiki/Green-light" title="Green-light">
        green light
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Feature_assignment_writing">
        Feature assignment writing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Feature assignment writing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Scripts written on assignment are screenplays created under contract with a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movie_studio" title="Movie studio">
        studio
       </a>
       , production company, or producer. These are the most common assignments sought after in screenwriting. A screenwriter can get an assignment either exclusively or from "open" assignments. A screenwriter can also be approached and offered an assignment. Assignment scripts are generally adaptations of an existing idea or property owned by the hiring company,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but can also be original works based on a concept created by the writer or producer.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rewriting_and_script_doctoring">
        Rewriting and script doctoring
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rewriting and script doctoring">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Most produced films are rewritten to some extent during the development process. Frequently, they are not rewritten by the original writer of the script.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many established screenwriters, as well as new writers whose work shows promise but lacks marketability, make their living rewriting scripts.
      </p>
      <p>
       When a script's central premise or characters are good but the script is otherwise unusable, a different writer or team of writers is contracted to do an entirely new draft, often referred to as a "page one rewrite".
       <span id="polish">
       </span>
       When only small problems remain, such as bad dialogue or poor humor, a writer is hired to do a "polish" or "punch-up".
      </p>
      <p>
       Depending on the size of the new writer's contributions,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Screen_credit" title="Screen credit">
        screen credit
       </a>
       may or may not be given. For instance, in the American film industry, credit to rewriters is given only if 50% or more of the script is substantially changed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These standards can make it difficult to establish the identity and number of screenwriters who contributed to a film's creation.
      </p>
      <p>
       When established writers are called in to rewrite portions of a script late in the development process, they are commonly referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Script_doctor" title="Script doctor">
        script doctors
       </a>
       . Prominent script doctors include Christopher Keane,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steve_Zaillian" title="Steve Zaillian">
        Steve Zaillian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/William_Goldman" title="William Goldman">
        William Goldman
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Towne" title="Robert Towne">
        Robert Towne
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mort_Nathan" title="Mort Nathan">
        Mort Nathan
       </a>
       , Quentin Tarantino and Peter Russell.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many up-and-coming screenwriters work as ghost writers.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Television_writing">
        Television writing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Television writing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A freelance television writer typically uses spec scripts or previous credits and reputation to obtain a contract to write one or more episodes for an existing television show. After an episode is submitted, rewriting or polishing may be required.
      </p>
      <p>
       A staff writer for a TV show generally works in-house, writing and rewriting episodes. Staff writers—often given other titles, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Story_editor" title="Story editor">
        story editor
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Television_producer" title="Television producer">
        producer
       </a>
       —work both as a group and individually on episode scripts to maintain the show's tone, style, characters, and plots.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Television show creators write the
       <a href="/wiki/Television_pilot" title="Television pilot">
        television pilot
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bible_(writing)" title="Bible (writing)">
        bible
       </a>
       of new television series. They are responsible for creating and managing all aspects of a show's characters, style, and plots. Frequently, a creator remains responsible for the show's day-to-day creative decisions throughout the series run as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Show_runner" title="Show runner">
        show runner
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Head_writer" title="Head writer">
        head writer
       </a>
       or story editor.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_for_daily_series">
        Writing for daily series
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Writing for daily series">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The process of writing for
       <a href="/wiki/Soap_opera" title="Soap opera">
        soap operas
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Telenovela" title="Telenovela">
        telenovelas
       </a>
       is different from that used by
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_time" title="Prime time">
        prime time
       </a>
       shows, due in part to the need to produce new episodes five days a week for several months. In one example cited by
       <a href="/wiki/Jane_Espenson" title="Jane Espenson">
        Jane Espenson
       </a>
       , screenwriting is a "sort of three-tiered system":
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jane_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-jane-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        a few top writers craft the overall
        <a href="/wiki/Story_arc" title="Story arc">
         story arcs
        </a>
        . Mid-level writers work with them to turn those arcs into things that look a lot like traditional episode outlines, and an array of writers below that (who do not even have to be local to Los Angeles), take those outlines and quickly generate the dialogue while adhering slavishly to the outlines.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Espenson notes that a recent trend has been to eliminate the role of the mid-level writer, relying on the senior writers to do rough outlines and giving the other writers a bit more freedom. Regardless, when the finished scripts are sent to the top writers, the latter do a final round of rewrites. Espenson also notes that a show that airs daily, with characters who have decades of history behind their voices, necessitates a writing staff without the distinctive voice that can sometimes be present in prime-time series.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jane_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-jane-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_for_game_shows">
        Writing for game shows
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Writing for game shows">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Game shows feature live contestants, but still use a team of writers as part of a specific format.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WGA_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-WGA-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This may involve the slate of questions and even specific phrasing or dialogue on the part of the host. Writers may not script the dialogue used by the contestants, but they work with the producers to create the actions, scenarios, and sequence of events that support the game show's concept.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Video_game_writing">
        Video game writing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Video game writing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       With the continued development and increased complexity of video games, many opportunities are available to employ screenwriters in the field of
       <a href="/wiki/Video_game_design" title="Video game design">
        video game design
       </a>
       . Video game writers work closely with the other game designers to create characters, scenarios, and dialogue.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Fundamentally, the screenplay is a unique literary form. It is like a musical score, in that it is intended to be interpreted on the basis of other artists' performance, rather than serving as a finished product for the enjoyment of its audience. For this reason, a screenplay is written using technical jargon and tight, spare prose when describing stage directions. Unlike a
       <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">
        novel
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">
        short story
       </a>
       , a screenplay focuses on describing the literal, visual aspects of the story, rather than on the internal thoughts of its characters. In screenwriting, the aim is to evoke those thoughts and emotions through subtext, action, and
       <a href="/wiki/Symbolism_(arts)" title="Symbolism (arts)">
        symbolism
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Several main screenwriting theories help writers approach the screenplay by systematizing the structure, goals and techniques of writing a script. The most common kinds of theories are structural. Screenwriter William Goldman is widely quoted as saying "Screenplays are structure".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Three-act_structure">
        Three-act structure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Three-act structure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Three-act_structure" title="Three-act structure">
        Three-act structure
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The three acts are setup (of the location and characters), confrontation (with an obstacle), and resolution (culminating in a climax and a dénouement). Usually, in a two-hour film, the first and third acts both typically last around 30 minutes, with the middle act lasting roughly an hour, but today many films start from the confrontation point and then goes to the setup act or they might even start at the last act and then go back to the start.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Writing_Drama" title="Writing Drama">
         Writing Drama
        </a>
       </i>
       , French writer and director
       <a href="/wiki/Yves_Lavandier" title="Yves Lavandier">
        Yves Lavandier
       </a>
       shows a slightly different approach.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As do most theorists, he maintains that every human action, whether fictitious or real, contains three logical parts: before the action, during the action, and after the action. But since the climax is part of the action, Yves Lavandier maintains that the second act must include the climax, which makes for a much shorter third act than is found in most screenwriting theories.
      </p>
      <p>
       Besides the three-act structure, it is also common to use a four- or five-act structure in a screenplay, though certain screenplays may include as many as twenty separate acts.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="The_Hero.27s_Journey">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Hero's_Journey">
        The Hero's Journey
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The Hero's Journey">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The hero's journey, also referred to as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monomyth" title="Monomyth">
        monomyth
       </a>
       , is an idea formulated by noted mythologist
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Campbell" title="Joseph Campbell">
        Joseph Campbell
       </a>
       . The central concept of the monomyth is that a pattern can be seen in stories and myths across history. Campbell defined and explained that pattern in his book
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Hero_with_a_Thousand_Faces" title="The Hero with a Thousand Faces">
         The Hero with a Thousand Faces
        </a>
       </i>
       (1949).
      </p>
      <p>
       Campbell's insight was that important myths from around the world, which have survived for thousands of years, all share a fundamental structure. This fundamental structure contains a number of stages, which include
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        <b>
         a call to adventure
        </b>
        , which the hero has to accept or decline,
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         a road of trials
        </b>
        , on which the hero succeeds or fails,
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         achieving the goal (or "boon")
        </b>
        , which often results in important self-knowledge,
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         a return to the ordinary world
        </b>
        , which again the hero can succeed or fail, and
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         application of the boon,
        </b>
        in which what the hero has gained can be used to improve the world.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       Later, screenwriter
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Vogler" title="Christopher Vogler">
        Christopher Vogler
       </a>
       refined and expanded the hero's journey for the screenplay form in his book,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Writer%27s_Journey:_Mythic_Structure_for_Writers" title="The Writer's Journey: Mythic Structure for Writers">
         The Writer's Journey: Mythic Structure for Writers
        </a>
       </i>
       (1993).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Syd_Field.27s_Paradigm">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Syd_Field's_Paradigm">
        Syd Field's Paradigm
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Syd Field's Paradigm">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In his book
       <i>
        Screenplay
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Syd_Field" title="Syd Field">
        Syd Field
       </a>
       posited a new theory, which he called the Paradigm. Field noticed that in a 120-page screenplay, Act Two was notoriously boring, and was also twice the length of Acts One and Three. He also noticed that an important dramatic event usually occurred at the middle of the picture, which implied to him that the middle act was actually two acts in one. So the Three Act Structure is notated 1, 2a, 2b, 3, resulting in
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
        Aristotle
       </a>
       's Three Acts divided into four pieces.
      </p>
      <p>
       Field also introduced the idea of
       <a href="/wiki/Plot_point" title="Plot point">
        Plot Points
       </a>
       into screenwriting theory. Plot Points are important structural functions that happen in approximately the same place in most successful movies, like the verses and choruses in a popular song. In subsequent books, Field has added to his original list, and students of his like Viki King and Linda Seger have added to the list of Plot Points. Here is a current list of the major Plot Points that are congruent with Field's Paradigm:
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Opening Image
       </b>
       : The first image in the screenplay should summarize the entire film, especially its tone. Often, writers go back and redo this as the last thing before submitting the script.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Exposition
       </b>
       : Provides some background information to the audience about the plot, characters' histories, setting, and theme.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inciting_Incident" title="Inciting Incident">
         Inciting Incident
        </a>
       </b>
       : Also called the catalyst, this is the point in the story when the Protagonist encounters the problem that will change their life. This is when the detective is assigned the case, where Boy meets Girl, and where the Comic Hero gets fired from his cushy job, forcing him into comic circumstances.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Plot Point 1
       </b>
       : The last scene in Act One, Plot Point 1 is a surprising development that radically changes the Protagonist's life, and forces him to confront the Opponent. In
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars_(film)" title="Star Wars (film)">
         Star Wars
        </a>
       </i>
       , this is when
       <a href="/wiki/Luke_Skywalker" title="Luke Skywalker">
        Luke
       </a>
       's family is killed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Empire_(Star_Wars)" title="Galactic Empire (Star Wars)">
        Empire
       </a>
       . He has no home to go back to, so he joins the
       <a href="/wiki/Rebel_Alliance" title="Rebel Alliance">
        Rebels
       </a>
       in opposing
       <a href="/wiki/Darth_Vader" title="Darth Vader">
        Darth Vader
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Pinch 1
       </b>
       : A reminder scene at about 3/8 the way through the script (halfway through Act 2a) that brings up the central conflict of the drama, reminding us of the overall conflict. For example, in
       <i>
        Star Wars
       </i>
       , Pinch 1 is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_stormtrooper" title="Imperial stormtrooper">
        Stormtroopers
       </a>
       attacking the
       <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Falcon" title="Millennium Falcon">
        Millennium Falcon
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Mos_Eisley" title="Mos Eisley">
        Mos Eisley
       </a>
       , reminding us the Empire is after the stolen plans to the
       <a href="/wiki/Death_Star" title="Death Star">
        Death Star
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/R2-D2" title="R2-D2">
        R2-D2
       </a>
       is carrying and Luke and
       <a href="/wiki/Obi-Wan_Kenobi" title="Obi-Wan Kenobi">
        Ben Kenobi
       </a>
       are trying to get to the Rebel Alliance (the main conflict).
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Midpoint
       </b>
       : An important scene in the middle of the script, often a reversal of fortune or revelation that changes the direction of the story. Field suggests that driving the story towards the Midpoint keeps the second act from sagging.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Pinch 2
       </b>
       : Another reminder scene about 5/8 through the script (halfway through Act 2b) that is somehow linked to Pinch 1 in reminding the audience about the central conflict. In
       <i>
        Star Wars
       </i>
       , Pinch 2 is the Stormtroopers attacking them as they rescue the
       <a href="/wiki/Princess_Leia" title="Princess Leia">
        Princess
       </a>
       in the Death Star. Both scenes remind us of the Empire's opposition, and using the Stormtrooper attack
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motif_(literature)" title="Motif (literature)">
        motif
       </a>
       unifies both Pinches.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Plot Point 2
       </b>
       : A dramatic reversal that ends Act 2 and begins Act 3, which is about confrontation and resolution. Sometimes Plot Point 2 is the moment when the Hero has had enough and is finally going to face the Opponent. Sometimes, like in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Toy_Story" title="Toy Story">
         Toy Story
        </a>
       </i>
       , it's the low-point for the Hero, and he must bounce back to overcome the odds in Act 3.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Showdown
       </b>
       : About midway through Act 3, the Protagonist will confront the Main Problem of the story and either overcome it, or come to a tragic end.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Resolution
       </b>
       : The issues of the story are resolved.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Tag
       </b>
       : An epilogue, tying up the loose ends of the story, giving the audience closure. This is also known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denouement" title="Denouement">
        denouement
       </a>
       . In general, films in recent decades have had longer denouements than films made in the 1970s or earlier.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_sequence_approach">
        The sequence approach
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The sequence approach">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The sequence approach to screenwriting, sometimes known as "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sequence_(film)" title="Sequence (film)">
        eight-sequence structure
       </a>
       ," is a system developed by
       <a href="/wiki/Frank_Daniel" title="Frank Daniel">
        Frank Daniel
       </a>
       , while he was the head of the Graduate Screenwriting Program at
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">
        USC
       </a>
       . It is based in part on the fact that, in the early days of cinema, technical matters forced screenwriters to divide their stories into sequences, each the length of a reel (about ten minutes).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The sequence approach mimics that early style. The story is broken up into eight 10-15 minute sequences. The sequences serve as "mini-movies", each with their own compressed three-act structure. The first two sequences combine to form the film's first act. The next four create the film's second act. The final two sequences complete the resolution and dénouement of the story. Each sequence's resolution creates the situation which sets up the next sequence.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Imagery">
        Imagery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Imagery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Imagery can be used in many metaphoric ways. In
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Talented_Mr._Ripley_(film)" title="The Talented Mr. Ripley (film)">
         The Talented Mr. Ripley
        </a>
       </i>
       , the title character talked of wanting to close the door on himself sometime, and then, in the end, he did.
       <a href="/wiki/Pathetic_fallacy" title="Pathetic fallacy">
        Pathetic fallacy
       </a>
       is also frequently used; rain to express a character feeling depressed, sunny days promote a feeling of happiness and calm. Imagery can be used to sway the emotions of the audience and to clue them in to what is happening.
      </p>
      <p>
       Imagery is well defined in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/City_of_God_(2002_film)" title="City of God (2002 film)">
         City of God
        </a>
       </i>
       . The opening image sequence sets the tone for the entire film. The film opens with the shimmer of a knife's blade on a sharpening stone. A drink is being prepared, The knife's blade shows again, juxtaposed is a shot of a chicken letting loose of its harness on its feet. All symbolising 'The One that got away'. The film is about life in the favelas in Rio - sprinkled with violence and games and ambition.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The first true screenplay is thought to be from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Melies" title="George Melies">
        George Melies
       </a>
       ' 1902 film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/A_Trip_to_the_Moon" title="A Trip to the Moon">
         A Trip to the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       . The movie is silent, but the screenplay still contains specific descriptions and action lines that resemble a modern-day script. As time went on and films became longer and more complex, the need for a screenplay became more prominent in the industry. The introduction of movie theaters also impacted the development of screenplays, as audiences became more widespread and sophisticated, so the stories had to be as well. Once the first non-silent movie was released in 1927, screenwriting became a hugely important position within Hollywood. The "studio system" of the 1930s only heightened this importance, as studio heads wanted productivity. Thus, having the "blueprint" (continuity screenplay) of the film beforehand became extremely optimal. Around 1970, the "spec script" was first created, and changed the industry for writers forever. Now, screenwriting for television (teleplays) is considered as difficult and competitive as writing is for feature films.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       , completed works may be
       <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">
        copyrighted
       </a>
       , but ideas and plots may not be. Any document written after 1978 in the U.S. is automatically copyrighted even without legal
       <a href="/wiki/Register_of_Copyrights" title="Register of Copyrights">
        registration
       </a>
       or notice. However, the
       <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">
        Library of Congress
       </a>
       will formally register a screenplay. U.S. Courts will not accept a lawsuit alleging that a defendant is infringing on the plaintiff's copyright in a work until the plaintiff registers the plaintiff's claim to those copyrights with the Copyright Office.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This means that a plaintiff's attempts to remedy an infringement will be delayed during the registration process.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Additionally, in many infringement cases, the plaintiff will not be able recoup
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attorney_fees" title="Attorney fees">
        attorney fees
       </a>
       or collect
       <a href="/wiki/Statutory_damages_for_copyright_infringement" title="Statutory damages for copyright infringement">
        statutory damages for copyright infringement
       </a>
       , unless the plaintiff registered before the infringement began.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For the purpose of establishing evidence that a screenwriter is the author of a particular screenplay (but not related to the legal copyrighting status of a work), the Writers Guild of America registers screenplays. However, since this service is one of record keeping and is not regulated by law, a variety of commercial and non-profit organizations exist for registering screenplays. Protection for teleplays, formats, as well as screenplays may be registered for instant proof-of-authorship by third-party assurance vendors, such as the
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.creatorsvault.com/" rel="nofollow">
        Creators Vault
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There is a line of
       <a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">
        precedent
       </a>
       in several states (including California and New York) that allows for "idea submission" claims, based on the notion that submission of a screenplay (or even a mere pitch for one) to a studio under very particular sets of factual circumstances could potentially give rise to an implied contract to pay for the ideas embedded in that screenplay, even if an alleged derivative work does not actually infringe the screenplay author's copyright.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biederman_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Biederman-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The unfortunate
       <a href="/wiki/Side_effect" title="Side effect">
        side effect
       </a>
       of such precedents (which were supposed to protect screenwriters) is that it is now that much harder to break into screenwriting. Naturally, motion picture and television production firms responded by categorically declining to read
       <i>
        all
       </i>
       unsolicited screenplays from unknown writers;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosman_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rosman-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       accepting screenplays only through official channels like talent agents, managers, and attorneys; and forcing screenwriters to sign broad
       <a href="/wiki/Legal_release" title="Legal release">
        legal releases
       </a>
       before their screenplays will be actually accepted, read, or considered.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biederman_23-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Biederman-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In turn, agents, managers, and attorneys have become extremely powerful gatekeepers on behalf of the major film studios and media networks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosman_24-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rosman-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One symptom of how hard it is to break into screenwriting as a result of such case law is that in 2008,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">
        Universal Studios
       </a>
       resisted construction of a bike path along the
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_River" title="Los Angeles River">
        Los Angeles River
       </a>
       next to its studio lot because it would worsen their existing problem with desperate amateur screenwriters throwing copies of their work over the studio wall.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

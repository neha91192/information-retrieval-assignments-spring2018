   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Script doctor
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        script doctor
       </b>
       , also called a
       <b>
        script consultant
       </b>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is a writer or playwright hired by a
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        film
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television
       </a>
       or theatre production to rewrite an existing script or polish specific aspects of it, including structure, characterization, dialogue, pacing, themes and other elements.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jones-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Script doctors generally do their work
       <a href="/wiki/Ghostwriter" title="Ghostwriter">
        uncredited
       </a>
       , for a variety of commercial and artistic reasons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jones-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurd_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hurd-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are usually brought in for scripts that have been almost "green-lit,"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       during the development and pre-production phases of a film, to address specific issues with the script, as identified by the financiers, production team, and cast.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abramowitz_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Abramowitz-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Under the
       <a href="/wiki/WGA_screenwriting_credit_system" title="WGA screenwriting credit system">
        Writers Guild of America screenwriting credit system
       </a>
       , a screenwriter must contribute more than 50 percent of an original screenplay or 33 percent of an adaptation to receive credit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abramowitz_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Abramowitz-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Uncredited screenwriters are not eligible to win the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">
        Academy Award
       </a>
       or the
       <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America_Award" title="Writers Guild of America Award">
        Writers Guild of America Award
       </a>
       .
      </p>

        <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Bruckheimer" title="Jerry Bruckheimer">
         Jerry Bruckheimer
        </a>
        for a while, because I was just going through a period where I was having a very difficult time coming up with my own ideas and I was climbing the walls. So I did what is called 'the production polish', where you are brought into the last two weeks on something that you are not emotionally invested in, where it is not your job to break the story, to come up with the moving parts and plot points. Basically, they just wanted some snappy dialogue for Sean Connery and Nicolas Cage. The first time I did it, actually, was for
        <i>
         Schindler's List
        </i>
        where no-one is looking for snappy dialogue, but the writer of that movie had gone on to direct a picture and there was a little more work that [director Steven Spielberg] wanted done before it went to Poland to begin shooting.

    Film budgeting
   </h1>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Film budgeting
       </b>
       refers to the process by which a line producer, unit production manager, or production accountant prepares a
       <a href="/wiki/Budget" title="Budget">
        budget
       </a>
       for a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_production" title="Film production">
        film production
       </a>
       . This document, which could be over 134 pages long, is used to secure financing for and lead to pre-production and production of the film. Multiple drafts of the budget may be required to whittle down costs. A budget is typically divided into four sections:
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_the_line_(filmmaking)" title="Above the line (filmmaking)">
         above the line
        </a>
       </i>
       (creative talent),
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Below_the_line_(filmmaking)" title="Below the line (filmmaking)">
         below the line
        </a>
       </i>
       (direct production costs),
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">
         post-production
        </a>
       </i>
       (editing, visual effects, etc.), and
       <i>
        other
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">
        insurance
       </a>
       , completion bond, etc.) The budget excludes
       <a href="/wiki/Film_promotion" title="Film promotion">
        film promotion
       </a>
       and marketing, which is the responsibility of the
       <a href="/wiki/Film_distributor" title="Film distributor">
        film distributor
       </a>
       . Film financing can be acquired from a private investor,
       <a href="/wiki/Sponsor_(commercial)" title="Sponsor (commercial)">
        sponsor
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Product_placement" title="Product placement">
        product placement
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Film_studio" title="Film studio">
        film studio
       </a>
       , entertainment company, and/or out-of-pocket funds.
      </p>
      <p>
       When it comes to reporting the budget of a film, the amount of the budget represents the gross budget, which is the grand total of actual spending to produce the project and not to be confused with net budget, which represents the final out of pocket for the producer after government incentives or rebates ("If you pay $50 for something but have a mail-in coupon for a $10 rebate, your gross spending still amounts to $50.")
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One of the consequences of the Sony hack was the release of budget information of many films or TV shows, including the 2015 Adam Sandler film Pixels. The actual cost to produce Pixels, or the "grand total", was $129.6 million and the net budget for Sony came to $111 million after they received a government rebate in Canada that covered a portion of their gross spend (cost) in the amount of just over $18 million.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Even though Sony's out of pocket for the film was reduced because of the incentive, it does not negate the fact that the actual cost (amount spent during production to make the film) was still $129 million.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Elements">
        Elements
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_budgeting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Elements">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Story rights
        </b>
        : The right to produce a film based on a
        <a href="/wiki/Play_(theatre)" title="Play (theatre)">
         play
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">
         novel
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">
         musical
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">
         video game
        </a>
        , or as a
        <a href="/wiki/Remake" title="Remake">
         remake
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">
         sequel
        </a>
        can cost anything from a couple of thousand (e.g.,
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Leaving_Las_Vegas" title="Leaving Las Vegas">
          Leaving Las Vegas
         </a>
        </i>
        ) to over $10 million (e.g., the video game
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halo_(video_game_series)" title="Halo (video game series)">
          Halo
         </a>
        </i>
        ). An original screenplay by a
        <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America" title="Writers Guild of America">
         Writers Guild of America
        </a>
        member can cost from its minimum, $69,499
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and upwards of $5 million (e.g.,
        <a href="/wiki/M._Night_Shyamalan" title="M. Night Shyamalan">
         M. Night Shyamalan
        </a>
        's
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Unbreakable_(film)" title="Unbreakable (film)">
          Unbreakable
         </a>
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">
          Screenplay
         </a>
        </b>
        : An A-list
        <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">
         screenwriter
        </a>
        may be paid between $100,000 to $2 million to write a script, including $400,000 a week for each rewrite of a film in trouble;
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <a href="/wiki/Script_doctor" title="Script doctor">
         script doctors
        </a>
        may be called upon to revise the final draft at $100,000 to $200,000 a week. Recently,
        <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">
         Columbia Pictures
        </a>
        has been offering the best screenwriters 2 percent of the gross profits (after the production and marketing budget has been deducted).
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Producers
        </b>
        :
        <a href="/wiki/Film_producer" title="Film producer">
         Film producers
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">
         executive producers
        </a>
        are often well-paid, with a top producer earning a seven-figure salary upfront as well as bonuses and a share of the profits. For
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Spider-Man_(2002_film)" title="Spider-Man (2002 film)">
          Spider-Man
         </a>
        </i>
        (2002), producer
        <a href="/wiki/Laura_Ziskin" title="Laura Ziskin">
         Laura Ziskin
        </a>
        is estimated to have been paid over $30 million.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Director
        </b>
        : The
        <a href="/wiki/Directors_Guild_of_America" title="Directors Guild of America">
         DGA
        </a>
        minimum is about $19,143 per week, for a minimum of ten weeks' work.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        An A-list
        <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">
         film director
        </a>
        can command $5 to $10 million per film.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Cast
        </b>
        : While the bulk of the cast usually gets paid by the Actors Guild standard rate of about 2,300 US$ per week,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        famous and bankable film stars can demand fees up to $30 million per film, plus perks (trailer, entourage, etc.) and possible gross participation.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Sometimes an actor will accept a minimal fee in exchange for a more lucrative share of the profits. Union extras are paid around $130 per day (plus extra for overtime or if they provide their own wardrobe), but on a low-budget film non-union extras are paid less, sometimes nothing at all.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Production costs
        </b>
        : The cost of producing the film includes crew wages, production design, live set and studio costs, costumes, catering, accommodation, transportation, travel, hotel stay, etc. The
        <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">
         director of photography
        </a>
        is usually the highest paid member of the crew.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Visual effects
        </b>
        : The cost of
        <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">
         computer-generated imagery
        </a>
        effects and other visual effect work in
        <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">
         post-production
        </a>
        depends largely on the amount of work, the desired quality, and the effects company involved (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_Light_and_Magic" title="Industrial Light and Magic">
         Industrial Light and Magic
        </a>
        is the most prestigious and expensive); extensive effect work, as in
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">
          Avatar
         </a>
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(film_series)" title="The Lord of the Rings (film series)">
          The Lord of the Rings
         </a>
        </i>
        , can easily cost up to $100,000,000.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Music
        </b>
        : Typically, the music budget of a major motion picture is about 2 percent of the final total. Some, such as
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Spider-Man_(2002_film)" title="Spider-Man (2002 film)">
          Spider-Man
         </a>
        </i>
        's music budget, have been as high as $4.5 million.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        The top film composers can ask for a seven-figure salary to compose about an hour of original
        <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">
         film score
        </a>
        . For well-known artists, like
        <a href="/wiki/Christina_Aguilera" title="Christina Aguilera">
         Christina Aguilera
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Shark_Tale" title="Shark Tale">
          Shark Tale
         </a>
        </i>
        ) or
        <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">
         Kanye West
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Mission:_Impossible_III" title="Mission: Impossible III">
          Mission: Impossible III
         </a>
        </i>
        ), the cost may be around $1 million for an original song;
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        for existing works it may be around $300,000.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        Sometimes a less-known artist will license the rights to their song for a small fee in exchange for the publicity.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tactics_for_cutting_costs">
        Tactics for cutting costs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_budgeting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tactics for cutting costs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Eliminate night scenes.
        </b>
        Shooting at night requires powerful/expensive lighting and the payment of nighttime rates to the crew.
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Broken_Arrow_(1996_film)" title="Broken Arrow (1996 film)">
          Broken Arrow
         </a>
        </i>
        (1996) cut costs by millions of dollars by eliminating the night scenes from the script. Many directors choose to use the "
        <a href="/wiki/Day_for_night" title="Day for night">
         day for night
        </a>
        " technique.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Avoid location filming in famous or commercial areas.
        </b>
        Shooting a scene on, for example, the
        <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge" title="Golden Gate Bridge">
         Golden Gate Bridge
        </a>
        requires stopping traffic with a resultant drop in revenue to the city of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_California" title="San Francisco, California">
         San Francisco
        </a>
        . Filming such a scene for
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Interview_with_the_Vampire" title="Interview with the Vampire">
          Interview with the Vampire
         </a>
        </i>
        cost Warner Bros. $500,000. Shifting the location to the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco-Oakland_Bay_Bridge" title="San Francisco-Oakland Bay Bridge">
         San Francisco-Oakland Bay Bridge
        </a>
        for
        <a href="/wiki/Close-up" title="Close-up">
         close-ups
        </a>
        could save hundreds of thousands of dollars in location fees. Moreover, some locations (such as commercial enterprises like hotels and nightclubs) are more willing to allow filming than others. Some producers of low-budget features avoid paying location fees and seek to capture shots by subterfuge.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Film action scenes early on Sunday morning.
        </b>
        Stopping traffic for a
        <a href="/wiki/Car_chase" title="Car chase">
         car chase
        </a>
        scene is easier in the early hours of Sunday morning, when traffic is at its lightest.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Use unknown, non-famous actors instead of well-established movie stars.
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Ask above-the-line talent to defer their salaries.
        </b>
        In exchange for dropping their large upfront salaries, actors, directors, and producers can receive a large share of the film's gross profits. This has the disadvantage of cutting the financier's eventual takings. It has the further disadvantage of ambiguity. In the case of net profit participation instead of gross profit participation, disagreements due to
        <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_accounting" title="Hollywood accounting">
         Hollywood accounting
        </a>
        methods can lead to
        <a href="/wiki/Audit" title="Audit">
         audits
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Litigation" title="Litigation">
         litigation
        </a>
        , as happened between
        <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">
         Peter Jackson
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/New_Line_Cinema" title="New Line Cinema">
         New Line Cinema
        </a>
        , after New Line claimed
        <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(film_series)" title="The Lord of the Rings (film series)">
         <i>
          The Lord of the Rings
         </i>
         film trilogy
        </a>
        , which grossed over 2 billion
        <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">
         USD
        </a>
        , failed to make any profit and thus denied payments to actors, the Tolkien estate, and Jackson.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Use a non-union crew.
        </b>
        This is not an option for studios that have signed contracts with the unions—the
        <a href="/wiki/Directors_Guild_of_America" title="Directors Guild of America">
         Directors Guild of America
        </a>
        (DGA),
        <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America" title="Writers Guild of America">
         Writers Guild of America
        </a>
        (WGA), and
        <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild" title="Screen Actors Guild">
         Screen Actors Guild
        </a>
        (SAG). Director
        <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rodriguez" title="Robert Rodriguez">
         Robert Rodriguez
        </a>
        has been known to use the skills of experienced non-union crews for his films.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Film in another region.
        </b>
        For example, many Hollywood movies set in U.S. cities are
        <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_North" title="Hollywood North">
         shot in Canada
        </a>
        to take advantage of lower labour costs, subject to fluctuating
        <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">
         exchange rates
        </a>
        . As well, they take advantage of federal and provincial
        <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">
         subsidies
        </a>
        designed to grow and sustain the film and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_production" title="Television production">
         television production
        </a>
        industries in the area. Many
        <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">
         U.S. states
        </a>
        have responded with tax incentives of their own (see
        <a href="/wiki/Movie_production_incentives_in_the_United_States" title="Movie production incentives in the United States">
         Movie production incentives in the United States
        </a>
        ). Australia, the
        <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">
         Czech Republic
        </a>
        , Germany,
        <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">
         Malta
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
         New Zealand
        </a>
        are other countries in which Hollywood movies are often filmed.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Going_over_budget">
        Going over budget
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Film_budgeting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Going over budget">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the US film production system, producers are typically not allowed to exceed the initial budget. Exceptions have of course been made, one of the most notable examples being
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Titanic_(1997_film)" title="Titanic (1997 film)">
         Titanic
        </a>
       </i>
       (1997). Director
       <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">
        James Cameron
       </a>
       ran aground with the budget and offered his fee back to the studio. In other countries, producers who exceed their budget tend to eat the cost by receiving less of their producer's fees. While the US system is profitable and can afford to go over budget, some other countries' film industries tend to be financed through government subsidies.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

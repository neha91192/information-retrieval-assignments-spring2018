      <p>
       <b>
        John Thomas Sayles
       </b>
       (born September 28, 1950) is an American
       <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">
        independent film
       </a>
       director, screenwriter, editor, actor and novelist. He has twice been nominated for the
       <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Screenplay" title="Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay">
        Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay
       </a>
       , for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Passion_Fish" title="Passion Fish">
         Passion Fish
        </a>
       </i>
       (1992) and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lone_Star_(1996_film)" title="Lone Star (1996 film)">
         Lone Star
        </a>
       </i>
       (1996). His film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Men_with_Guns" title="Men with Guns">
         Men with Guns
        </a>
       </i>
       (1997) was nominated for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe" title="Golden Globe">
        Golden Globe
       </a>
       for Best Foreign Language Film. His directorial debut,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Secaucus_7" title="Return of the Secaucus 7">
         Return of the Secaucus 7
        </a>
       </i>
       (1980), has been added to the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Registry" title="National Film Registry">
        National Film Registry
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
        Early life
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Sayles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Sayles was born in
       <a href="/wiki/Schenectady,_New_York" title="Schenectady, New York">
        Schenectady, New York
       </a>
       , the son of Mary (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Married_and_maiden_names" title="Married and maiden names">
        <i>
         née
        </i>
       </a>
       Rausch), a teacher, and Donald John Sayles, a school administrator.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Both of Sayles's parents were of half-Irish descent and were Catholic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He attended
       <a href="/wiki/Williams_College" title="Williams College">
        Williams College
       </a>
       with frequent collaborators
       <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Clapp" title="Gordon Clapp">
        Gordon Clapp
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/David_Strathairn" title="David Strathairn">
        David Strathairn
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Career">
        Career
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Sayles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Like
       <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">
        Martin Scorsese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">
        James Cameron
       </a>
       , Sayles began his career working with
       <a href="/wiki/Roger_Corman" title="Roger Corman">
        Roger Corman
       </a>
       . In 1979, Sayles used $30,000 he earned writing scripts for Corman to fund his first film,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Secaucus_7" title="Return of the Secaucus 7">
         Return of the Secaucus 7
        </a>
       </i>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . To make the film on a limited budget, he set the film in a large house so that he did not have to travel to or get permits for different locations, set the story over a three-day weekend to limit costume changes, and wrote about people his age so he could cast his friends in it. The film received near-unanimous critical acclaim at the time and has held its reputation. In November 1997, the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Preservation_Board" title="National Film Preservation Board">
        National Film Preservation Board
       </a>
       announced that
       <i>
        Return of the Secaucus 7
       </i>
       would be one of the 25 films selected that year for preservation in the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Registry" title="National Film Registry">
        National Film Registry
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">
        Library of Congress
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1983, after the films
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Baby_It%27s_You_(film)" title="Baby It's You (film)">
         Baby It's You
        </a>
       </i>
       (starring
       <a href="/wiki/Rosanna_Arquette" title="Rosanna Arquette">
        Rosanna Arquette
       </a>
       ) and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lianna" title="Lianna">
         Lianna
        </a>
       </i>
       (a story in which a married woman becomes discontented with her marriage and falls in love with another woman), Sayles received a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MacArthur_Fellowship" title="MacArthur Fellowship">
        MacArthur Fellowship
       </a>
       . He put the money into the fantasy
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Brother_from_Another_Planet" title="The Brother from Another Planet">
         The Brother from Another Planet
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">

       <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">
        Harlem
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Men_in_black" title="Men in black">

       </a>

      <p>
       In 1989, Sayles created and wrote the pilot episode for the short-lived television show
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Shannon%27s_Deal" title="Shannon's Deal">
         Shannon's Deal
        </a>
       </i>
       about a down-and-out
       <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">
        Philadelphia
       </a>
       lawyer played by
       <a href="/wiki/Jamey_Sheridan" title="Jamey Sheridan">
        Jamey Sheridan
       </a>
       . Sayles received a 1990
       <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Award" title="Edgar Award">
        Edgar Award
       </a>
       for his
       <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">
        teleplay
       </a>
       for the pilot. The show ran for 16 episodes before being cancelled in 1991.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sayles has funded most of his films by writing genre scripts, such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Piranha_(1978_film)" title="Piranha (1978 film)">
         Piranha
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>

       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Challenge_(1982_film)" title="The Challenge (1982 film)">
         The Challenge
        </a>
       </i>
       .

      <p>
       In February 2009, Sayles was reported to be writing an
       <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">
        HBO
       </a>
       series based on the early life of
       <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Kiedis" title="Anthony Kiedis">
        Anthony Kiedis
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Chili_Peppers" title="Red Hot Chili Peppers">
        Red Hot Chili Peppers
       </a>
       . The drama, tentatively titled
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Scar_Tissue" title="Scar Tissue">
         Scar Tissue
        </a>
       </i>
       , centers on Kiedis's early years living in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Hollywood" title="West Hollywood">
        West Hollywood
       </a>
       with his father. At that time, Kiedis's father, known as Spider, sold drugs (according to legend, his clients included
       <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">
        The Who
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">
        Led Zeppelin
       </a>
       ) and mingled with rock stars on the Sunset Strip, all while aspiring to get into show business.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In February 2010, Sayles began shooting his 17th feature film, the historical war drama
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Amigo_(film)" title="Amigo (film)">
         Amigo
        </a>
       </i>
       , in the Philippines. The film is a fictional account of events during the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippine%E2%80%93American_War" title="Philippine–American War">
        Philippine–American War
       </a>
       , with a cast that includes Joel Torre, Chris Cooper, and
       <a href="/wiki/Garret_Dillahunt" title="Garret Dillahunt">
        Garret Dillahunt
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       His novel
       <i>
        A Moment in the Sun
       </i>
       , set during the same period as
       <i>
        Amigo
       </i>
       , in the Philippines, Cuba, and the US, was released in 2011 by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McSweeney%E2%80%99s" title="McSweeney’s">
        McSweeney’s
       </a>
       . It includes an account of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilmington_Insurrection_of_1898" title="Wilmington Insurrection of 1898">
        Wilmington Insurrection of 1898
       </a>
       in North Carolina, the only
       <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">
        coup d'état
       </a>
       in United States history in which a duly elected government was overthrown.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_recognition">
        Other recognition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Sayles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other recognition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Sayles' first published story, "I-80 Nebraska", won an
       <a href="/wiki/O._Henry_Award" title="O. Henry Award">
        O. Henry Award
       </a>
       ; his novel,
       <i>
        Union Dues
       </i>
       , was nominated for a
       <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award" title="National Book Award">
        National Book Award
       </a>
       as well as the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Critics_Circle_Award" title="National Book Critics Circle Award">
        National Book Critics Circle Award
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1983,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sayles received the
       <a href="/wiki/John_D._and_Catherine_T._MacArthur_Foundation" title="John D. and Catherine T. MacArthur Foundation">
        John D. MacArthur Award
       </a>
       , given to 20 Americans in diverse fields each year for their innovative work. He has also been the recipient of the
       <a href="/wiki/Eugene_V._Debs" title="Eugene V. Debs">
        Eugene V. Debs
       </a>
       Award, the
       <a href="/wiki/John_Steinbeck_Award" title="John Steinbeck Award">
        John Steinbeck Award
       </a>

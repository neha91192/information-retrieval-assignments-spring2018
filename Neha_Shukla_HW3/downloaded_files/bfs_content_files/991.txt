      <p>
       A
       <b>
        camera operator
       </b>
       , sometimes informally called a
       <b>
        cameraman
       </b>
       , is a professional
       <a href="/wiki/Operator_(profession)" title="Operator (profession)">
        operator
       </a>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Movie_camera" title="Movie camera">
        film
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Video_camera" title="Video camera">
        video camera
       </a>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Filmmaking" title="Filmmaking">
        filmmaking
       </a>
       , the person designing the lighting is the
       <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">
        cinematographer
       </a>
       or director of photography, who is also informally called a "cameraman" though it is a different job. A camera operator in a
       <a href="/wiki/Video_production" title="Video production">
        video production
       </a>
       may be known as a
       <b>
        television camera operator
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        video camera operator
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Videographer" title="Videographer">
         videographer
        </a>
       </b>
       , depending on the context and technology involved, usually operating a
       <a href="/wiki/Professional_video_camera" title="Professional video camera">
        professional video camera
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The camera operator is responsible for physically operating the camera and maintaining
       <a href="/wiki/Composition_(visual_arts)" title="Composition (visual arts)">
        composition
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Camera_angle" title="Camera angle">
        camera angles
       </a>
       throughout a given scene or shot. In narrative filmmaking, the camera operator will collaborate with the director, director of photography, actors and crew to make technical and creative decisions. In this setting, a camera operator is part of a [film crew]] consisting of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Director_of_photography" title="Director of photography">
        director of photography
       </a>
       and one or more camera assistants. In documentary filmmaking and news, the camera is often called on to film unfolding, unscripted events. In 2006, there were approximately 27,000 television, video, and motion picture camera operators employed in the United States.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Important camera operator skills include choreographing and framing
       <a href="/wiki/Shot_(filmmaking)" title="Shot (filmmaking)">
        shots
       </a>
       , knowledge of and the ability to select appropriate
       <a href="/wiki/Camera_lens" title="Camera lens">
        camera lenses
       </a>
       , and other equipment (
       <a href="/wiki/Camera_dolly" title="Camera dolly">
        dollies
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camera_crane" title="Camera crane">
        camera cranes
       </a>
       , etc.) to portray dramatic scenes. The principles of dramatic story telling and
       <a href="/wiki/Film_editing" title="Film editing">
        film editing
       </a>
       fundamentals are important skills as well. The camera operator is required to communicate clearly and concisely on sets where time and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_budget" title="Film budget">
        film budget
       </a>
       constraints are ever present.
      </p>

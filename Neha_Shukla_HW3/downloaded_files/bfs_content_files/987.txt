      <p>
       A
       <b>
        matte painting
       </b>
       is a painted representation of a landscape, set, or distant location that allows filmmakers to create the illusion of an environment that is not present at the filming location. Historically, matte painters and film technicians have used various techniques to combine a matte-painted image with live-action footage. At its best, depending on the skill levels of the artists and technicians, the effect is "seamless" and creates environments that would otherwise be impossible or expensive to film. In the scenes the painting part is static and movements are integrated on it.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Matte_painting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Traditionally, matte paintings were made by artists using paints or pastels on large sheets of glass for integrating with the live-action footage.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first known matte painting shot was made in 1907 by
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_Dawn" title="Norman Dawn">
        Norman Dawn
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Cinematographers" title="American Society of Cinematographers">
        (ASC)
       </a>
       , who improvised the crumbling California Missions by painting them on glass for the movie
       <i>
        Missions of California
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Notable traditional matte-painting shots include Dorothy’s approach to the Emerald City in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">
         The Wizard of Oz
        </a>
       </i>
       , Charles Foster Kane’s Xanadu in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Citizen_Kane" title="Citizen Kane">
         Citizen Kane
        </a>
       </i>
       , and the seemingly bottomless tractor-beam set of
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_Wars_Episode_IV:_A_New_Hope" title="Star Wars Episode IV: A New Hope">
         Star Wars Episode IV: A New Hope
        </a>
       </i>
       . The first
       <i>
        Star Wars
       </i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Documentary" title="Documentary">
        documentary
       </a>
       ever made (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Making_of_Star_Wars" title="The Making of Star Wars">
         The Making of Star Wars
        </a>
       </i>
       , directed by
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Guenette" title="Robert Guenette">
        Robert Guenette
       </a>
       in 1977 for television) mentioned the technique used for the tractor beam scene as being a
       <b>
        glass painting
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       By the mid-1980s, advancements in computer graphics programs allowed matte painters to work in the digital realm. The first digital matte shot was created by painter
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Leith_Evans" title="Christopher Leith Evans">
        Chris Evans
       </a>
       in 1985 for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Young_Sherlock_Holmes" title="Young Sherlock Holmes">
         Young Sherlock Holmes
        </a>
       </i>
       for a scene featuring a computer-graphics (CG) animation of a knight leaping from a stained-glass window. Evans first painted the window in acrylics, then scanned the painting into LucasFilm’s Pixar system for further digital manipulation. The
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_animation" title="Computer animation">
        computer animation
       </a>
       (another first) blended perfectly with the digital matte, which could not have been accomplished using a traditional matte painting.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="New_technologies">
        New technologies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Matte_painting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: New technologies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Throughout the 1990s, traditional matte paintings were still in use, but more often in conjunction with digital compositing.
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Die_Hard_2" title="Die Hard 2">
         Die Hard 2
        </a>
       </i>
       (1990) was the first film to use digitally composited live-action footage with a traditional glass matte painting that had been photographed and scanned into a computer. It was for the last scene, which took place on an airport runway.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By the end of the decade, the time of hand-painted matte paintings was drawing to a close, although as late as 1997 some traditional paintings were still being made, notably
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Leith_Evans" title="Christopher Leith Evans">
        Chris Evans
       </a>
       ’ painting of the Carpathia rescue ship in
       <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">
        James Cameron
       </a>
       ’s
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Titanic_(1997_film)" title="Titanic (1997 film)">
         Titanic
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Paint has now been superseded by digital images created using photo references, 3-D models, and drawing tablets. Matte painters combine their digitally matte painted textures within computer-generated 3-D environments, allowing for 3-D camera movement.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lighting algorithms used to simulate lighting sources expanded in scope in 1995, when
       <a href="/wiki/Radiosity_(computer_graphics)" title="Radiosity (computer graphics)">
        radiosity rendering
       </a>
       was applied to film for the first time in Martin Scorsese’s
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Casino_(film)" title="Casino (film)">
         Casino
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Matte_World_Digital" title="Matte World Digital">
        Matte World Digital
       </a>
       collaborated with LightScape to simulate the indirect bounce-light effect
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of millions of neon lights of the 70s-era Las Vegas strip.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lower computer processing times continue to alter and expand matte painting technologies and techniques.
      </p>

   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Character actor
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>

     <a href="#p-search">
      search

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1620" data-file-width="1516" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Ernie_Weckbaugh_%26_Claude_Rains.jpg/220px-Ernie_Weckbaugh_%26_Claude_Rains.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Ernie_Weckbaugh_%26_Claude_Rains.jpg/330px-Ernie_Weckbaugh_%26_Claude_Rains.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Ernie_Weckbaugh_%26_Claude_Rains.jpg/440px-Ernie_Weckbaugh_%26_Claude_Rains.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ernie_Weckbaugh_%26_Claude_Rains.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Actor
         <a href="/wiki/Claude_Rains" title="Claude Rains">
          Claude Rains
         </a>
         (right) was sometimes a leading actor, sometimes a character actor, often cast as a sophisticated, sometimes "morally ambiguous" man.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsDigitalPolyphony_1-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-twsDigitalPolyphony-1">
           [1]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        character actor
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        character actress
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Supporting_actor" title="Supporting actor">
        supporting actor
       </a>
       who plays unusual, interesting, or eccentric
       <a href="/wiki/Character_(arts)" title="Character (arts)">
        characters
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsForeman_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsForeman-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsNYActingSchool_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsNYActingSchool-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The term, often contrasted with that of
       <a href="/wiki/Leading_actor" title="Leading actor">
        leading actor
       </a>
       , is somewhat abstract and open to interpretation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In a literal sense, all actors can be considered character actors since they all play "characters",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsTVTonight_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsTVTonight-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but in the usual sense it is an actor who plays a distinctive and important
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supporting_role" title="Supporting role">
        supporting role
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsDigitalPolyphony_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsDigitalPolyphony-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsYahoo_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsYahoo-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The term is sometimes used to describe an actor who plays characters who are very different from the actor's off-screen real-life personality, while in another sense it describes an actor who specializes in minor roles. In either case, character actor roles are more substantial than
       <a href="/wiki/Bit_part" title="Bit part">
        bit parts
       </a>
       or non-speaking
       <a href="/wiki/Extra_(acting)" title="Extra (acting)">
        extras
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The term is used primarily to describe
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        film
       </a>
       actors, and is less used to describe
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theater" title="Theater">
        theater
       </a>
       actors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsTobolowsky_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsTobolowsky-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An early use of the term was in the 1883 edition of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Stage" title="The Stage">
         The Stage
        </a>
       </i>
       , which defined a character actor as "one who portrays individualities and eccentricities".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Actors with a long career history of playing character roles may be difficult for audiences to recognize as being the same actor.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsNYActingSchool_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsNYActingSchool-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Wizard_of_Oz_Margaret_Hamilton_Judy_Garland_1939.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="467" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/The_Wizard_of_Oz_Margaret_Hamilton_Judy_Garland_1939.jpg/220px-The_Wizard_of_Oz_Margaret_Hamilton_Judy_Garland_1939.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/The_Wizard_of_Oz_Margaret_Hamilton_Judy_Garland_1939.jpg/330px-The_Wizard_of_Oz_Margaret_Hamilton_Judy_Garland_1939.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/The_Wizard_of_Oz_Margaret_Hamilton_Judy_Garland_1939.jpg/440px-The_Wizard_of_Oz_Margaret_Hamilton_Judy_Garland_1939.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Wizard_of_Oz_Margaret_Hamilton_Judy_Garland_1939.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Character actress
         <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Hamilton_(actress)" title="Margaret Hamilton (actress)">
          Margaret Hamilton
         </a>
         (left) in real life was a "sweet, gentle woman" who even taught kindergarten prior to working on Broadway and Hollywood, which was different from her on-screen persona of the
         <a href="/wiki/Wicked_Witch_of_the_West" title="Wicked Witch of the West">
          Wicked Witch
         </a>
         in the 1939 film
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">
           The Wizard of Oz
          </a>
         </i>
         .
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MH_13-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-MH-13">
           [13]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Unlike
       <a href="/wiki/Leading_actor" title="Leading actor">
        leading actors
       </a>
       , they are generally seen as less glamorous.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsNYActingSchool_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsNYActingSchool-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsTVTonight_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsTVTonight-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsForeman_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsForeman-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While a leading actor often has
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_attraction" title="Physical attraction">
        physical beauty
       </a>
       needed to play the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_interest" title="Romantic interest">
        love interest
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsUSAToday2_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsUSAToday2-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a character actor may be short or tall, heavy or thin, balding, older, or simply unconventional-looking and distinctive in some physical way. For example, the face of Chicago character actor William Schutz was disfigured in a car accident when he was five years old, but his appearance despite reconstructive surgery helped him to be memorable and distinctive to theater audiences.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Generally, the names of character actors are not featured prominently in movie and television advertising on the
       <a href="/wiki/Marquee_(sign)" title="Marquee (sign)">
        marquee
       </a>
       , since a character actor's name is not expected to attract film audiences.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The roles that character actors play in film or television are often identified by only one name, such as "Officer Fred", while roles of leading actors often have a full name, such as "Captain Jack Sparrow".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsTobolowsky_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsTobolowsky-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some character actors have distinctive voices or accents, or they develop memorable mannerisms. A character actor with a long career may not have a well-known name, yet may be instantly recognizable.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During the course of an acting career, an actor can sometimes shift between leading roles and secondary roles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsUSAToday2_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsUSAToday2-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some leading actors, as they get older, find that access to leading roles is limited by their increasing
       <a href="/wiki/Ageism" title="Ageism">
        age
       </a>
       . In the past, actors of color, who were often barred from roles for which they were otherwise suited, found work performing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_stereotypes_in_popular_culture" title="Ethnic stereotypes in popular culture">
        ethnic stereotypes
       </a>
       . Sometimes character actors have developed careers based on specific talents needed in genre films, such as dancing, horsemanship, acrobatics, swimming ability, or boxing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many up-and-coming actors find themselves
       <a href="/wiki/Typecasting_(acting)" title="Typecasting (acting)">
        typecast
       </a>
       in character roles due to an early success with a particular part or in a certain genre, such that the actor becomes so strongly identified with a particular type of role that casting directors steer the actor to similar roles. Some character actors play essentially the same character over and over, as with
       <a href="/wiki/Andy_Devine" title="Andy Devine">
        Andy Devine
       </a>
       's humorous but resourceful sidekick, while other actors, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Olivier" title="Laurence Olivier">
        Sir Laurence Olivier
       </a>
       , have the capacity of submerging themselves in any role they play. That being said, some character actors can be known as "chameleons", actors who can play roles that vary wildly. One such example of this is
       <a href="/wiki/Gary_Oldman" title="Gary Oldman">
        Gary Oldman
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some character actors develop a
       <a href="/wiki/Cult_following" title="Cult following">
        cult following
       </a>
       with a particular audience, such as with the fans of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">
         Star Trek
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Rocky_Horror_Picture_Show" title="The Rocky Horror Picture Show">
         The Rocky Horror Picture Show
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Character actors tend to play the same type of role throughout their careers,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsDigitalPolyphony_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsDigitalPolyphony-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       including
       <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Keitel" title="Harvey Keitel">
        Harvey Keitel
       </a>
       as a "tough and determined guy",
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dame_Maggie_Smith" title="Dame Maggie Smith">
        Dame Maggie Smith
       </a>
       as an "upstanding lady matriarch",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Lloyd" title="Christopher Lloyd">
        Christopher Lloyd
       </a>
       as an eccentric,
       <a href="/wiki/Claude_Rains" title="Claude Rains">
        Claude Rains
       </a>
       as a "sophisticated, sometimes ambiguously moral man",
       <a href="/wiki/Abe_Vigoda" title="Abe Vigoda">
        Abe Vigoda
       </a>
       as a "leathery, sunken-eyed" and tired hoodlum on the verge of retirement,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Walken" title="Christopher Walken">
        Christopher Walken
       </a>
       as a "speech maker",
       <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Schiavelli" title="Vincent Schiavelli">
        Vincent Schiavelli
       </a>
       as "the confused guy",
       <a href="/wiki/Fairuza_Balk" title="Fairuza Balk">
        Fairuza Balk
       </a>
       as a "moody
       <a href="/wiki/Goth_subculture" title="Goth subculture">
        goth
       </a>
       girl",
       <a href="/wiki/Steve_Buscemi" title="Steve Buscemi">
        Steve Buscemi
       </a>
       as "a quirky, smart guy with a mind just outside of reality" and
       <a href="/wiki/Forest_Whitaker" title="Forest Whitaker">
        Forest Whitaker
       </a>
       as a "calm, composed character with an edge and potential to explode".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsDigitalPolyphony_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsDigitalPolyphony-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Ed_Lauter" title="Ed Lauter">
        Ed Lauter
       </a>
       usually portrayed a menacing figure because of his "long, angular face" which was easily recognized in public, although audiences rarely knew his name.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsUSAToday_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsUSAToday-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Character actors can play a variety of types, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Femme_fatale" title="Femme fatale">
        femme fatale
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gunslinger" title="Gunslinger">
        gunslinger
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sidekick" title="Sidekick">
        sidekick
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Town_drunk" title="Town drunk">
        town drunk
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Villain" title="Villain">
        villain
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hooker_with_a_heart_of_gold" title="Hooker with a heart of gold">
        whore with a heart of gold
       </a>
       , and many others. A character actor's roles are often perceived as being substantially different from their perceived real-life persona, meaning that they do not portray an extension of themselves, but rather a character substantially different from their off-screen persona.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsForeman_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsForeman-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Character actors subsume themselves into the characters they portray, such that their off-screen acting persona is practically unrecognizable.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsForeman_6-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsForeman-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to one view, great character actors are rarely out of work, and often have long careers that span decades.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twsForeman_6-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-twsForeman-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are also often highly regarded by fellow actors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

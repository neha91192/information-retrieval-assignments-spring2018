   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Great American Novel
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       This article is about the Great American Novel as a concept. For other uses, see
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Great_American_Novel_(disambiguation)" title="Great American Novel (disambiguation)">
        Great American Novel (disambiguation)
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       The idea of the
       <b>
        Great American Novel
       </b>
       is the concept of a
       <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">
        novel
       </a>
       of high literary merit which shows the
       <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_the_United_States" title="Culture of the United States">
        culture of the United States
       </a>
       at a specific time in the country's history. The novel is presumably written by an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_author" title="American author">
        American author
       </a>
       who is knowledgeable about the state, culture, and perspective of the common
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_citizen" title="United States citizen">
        American citizen
       </a>
       . The author uses the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_work" title="Literary work">
        literary work
       </a>
       to identify and exhibit
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        the language
       </a>
       used by the American people of the time and to capture the unique American experience, especially as it is perceived for the time. In
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_United_States" title="History of the United States">
        historical
       </a>
       terms, it is sometimes equated as being the American response to the
       <a href="/wiki/National_epic" title="National epic">
        national epic
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_American_Novel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       While
       <a href="/wiki/Fiction" title="Fiction">
        fiction
       </a>
       was written in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonial_America" title="Colonial America">
        colonial America
       </a>
       as early as the 17th century, it was not until a distinct American identity developed in the 18th century that works classified as
       <a href="/wiki/American_literature" title="American literature">
        American literature
       </a>
       began. America's identity as a nation was reflected alongside the development of its literature.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The term "Great American Novel" derives directly from the title of an 1868 essay
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
        American Civil War
       </a>
       novelist
       <a href="/wiki/John_William_De_Forest" title="John William De Forest">
        John William De Forest
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In modern usage, the term is often
       <a href="/wiki/Literal_and_figurative_language" title="Literal and figurative language">
        figurative
       </a>
       and represents a
       <a href="/wiki/Western_canon" title="Western canon">
        canonical
       </a>
       writing, making it a literary benchmark emblematic of what defines American literature in a given era. Aspiring writers of all ages, but especially students, are often said to be driven to write the Great American Novel. Theoretically, such a work would be the greatest American book on American culture which could ever be written. Thus, "Great American Novel" is a metaphor for identity, a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platonic_ideal" title="Platonic ideal">
        Platonic ideal
       </a>
       that is not achieved in any specific texts, but whose aim writers strive to mirror in their work.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Minimum salaries for union
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">
        screenwriters
       </a>
       are set by the
       <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America" title="Writers Guild of America">
        Writers Guild of America
       </a>
       . Non-union screenwriters may write for free; an established screenwriter may write for millions of dollars.
      </p>

      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Against
        </b>
        : A word used to describe a script's unproduced price relative to its value if approved for production—for example, if a script is sold for $300,000, but the writer gains an extra $200,000 if it leads to production, the screenwriter's salary is described as "$300,000 against $500,000".
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Option_(filmmaking)" title="Option (filmmaking)">
          Option
         </a>
        </b>
        : If a script is not purchased, it may be optioned. An option is money paid in exchange for the right (the "option") to produce—and therefore to purchase outright—a screenplay, treatment, or other work within a certain period.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Feature assignment
        </b>
        : The writer writes the script on assignment under contract with a studio, production company, or individual.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(filmmaking)" title="Pitch (filmmaking)">
          Pitch
         </a>
        </b>
        : The writer works up a five- to twenty-minute presentation of a prospective movie and presents it to buyers in a short meeting.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Rewriting
        </b>
        : The writer rewrites someone else's script for pay. The writer pitches his "take", much like he would an original pitch.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Spec_script" title="Spec script">
          Spec script
         </a>
        </b>
        : Short for "speculative" or "on speculation" as in; He wrote his script on spec. The writer writes the script (original or someone else's idea) without being paid, and, subsequently, tries to sell it.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_Purchase_Agreement">
        Standard Purchase Agreement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriter%27s_salary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Standard Purchase Agreement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A typical
       <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">
        screenwriter
       </a>
       's purchase agreement will typically contain the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Guarantee
        </b>
        : Literally, the money the writer is guaranteed to receive, whether the script is produced or not. This amount is usually divided into steps with payments and due dates. For example, a "three step deal" might include:
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <b>
           Step One:
          </b>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <dl>
             <dd>
              <ul>
               <li>
                First Draft Commencement (50% paid upon Commencement)
               </li>
               <li>
                First Draft Delivery (50% paid upon Completion)
               </li>
              </ul>
             </dd>
            </dl>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <b>
           Step Two:
          </b>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <dl>
             <dd>
              <ul>
               <li>
                First Rewrite Commencement (50% paid upon Commencement)
               </li>
               <li>
                First Rewrite Completion (50% paid upon Completion)
               </li>
              </ul>
             </dd>
            </dl>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <b>
           Step Three:
          </b>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <dl>
             <dd>
              <ul>
               <li>
                Polish Commencement (50% paid upon Commencement)
               </li>
               <li>
                Polish Completion (50% paid upon Completion)
                <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
                 <a href="#cite_note-1">
                  [1]
                 </a>
                </sup>
               </li>
              </ul>
             </dd>
            </dl>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The guaranteed money is sometimes referred to as the "front-end."
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Optional Steps
        </b>
        : The deal may often define optional steps that the studio can trigger at their discretion. For example:
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <b>
           Step Four:
          </b>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <dl>
             <dd>
              <ul>
               <li>
                Second Rewrite Commencement (50% paid upon Commencement)
               </li>
               <li>
                Second Rewrite Completion (50% paid upon Completion)
               </li>
              </ul>
             </dd>
            </dl>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <b>
           Step Five:
          </b>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <dl>
             <dd>
              <ul>
               <li>
                Second Polish Commencement (50% paid upon Commencement)
               </li>
               <li>
                Second Polish Completion (50% paid upon Completion)
               </li>
              </ul>
             </dd>
            </dl>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Bonus/Bonuses
        </b>
        : Also known as the "back-end". Typically, a production bonus is paid once the script goes into production, or, if there is more than one writer, after the final credit is determined. A typical contract will specify a smaller production bonus for shared credit. There may also be bonuses contingent upon budget (e.g., "if the movie's budget is greater than
        <i>
         x
        </i>
        ") or grosses. The cousin of the bonus is the "penalty", which might be paid by the writer if, for example, the script has not been put into production by a set date; penalties are rarely included in writer's deals, however.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Screenwriter%27s_salary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         1900
        </b>
        : One of America's first screenwriters, New York journalist
        <a href="/wiki/Roy_McCardell" title="Roy McCardell">
         Roy McCardell
        </a>
        , is hired to write ten scenarios (each about 90 seconds long) for $15 each (equivalent to $441 in 2017).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-US_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-inflation-US-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         1947
        </b>
        : The original screenplay
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Woman_of_the_Year" title="Woman of the Year">
          Woman of the Year
         </a>
        </i>
        is bought by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MGM" title="MGM">
         MGM
        </a>
        for $100,000 (about $1.1 million in 2017).
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         1949
        </b>
        :
        <a href="/wiki/Ben_Hecht" title="Ben Hecht">
         Ben Hecht
        </a>
        is paid $10,000 a week (about $103,000 in 2017).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Claims
        <a href="/wiki/David_O._Selznick" title="David O. Selznick">
         David O. Selznick
        </a>
        paid him $3,500 a day (about $36,000 in 2017).
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         1967
        </b>
        :
        <a href="/wiki/William_Goldman" title="William Goldman">
         William Goldman
        </a>
        's original screenplay
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Butch_Cassidy_and_the_Sundance_Kid" title="Butch Cassidy and the Sundance Kid">
          Butch Cassidy and the Sundance Kid
         </a>
        </i>
        was purchased for $400,000 (about $2.9 million in 2017).
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         1972
        </b>
        :
        <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Schrader" title="Leonard Schrader">
         Leonard
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Paul_Schrader" title="Paul Schrader">
         Paul Schrader
        </a>
        's
        <a href="/wiki/Spec_script" title="Spec script">
         spec script
        </a>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Yakuza" title="The Yakuza">
          The Yakuza
         </a>
        </i>
        is sold for $350,000 (about $2.0 million in 2017). Paul Schrader and his agent receive 40% each; novelist Leonard Schrader, who conceived the idea for the story, is persuaded to take just 20% and a story credit.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         1984
        </b>
        :
        <a href="/wiki/Shane_Black" title="Shane Black">
         Shane Black
        </a>
        sells the screenplay to
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Lethal_Weapon" title="Lethal Weapon">
          Lethal Weapon
         </a>
